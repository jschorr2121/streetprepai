# Street Prep AI — IB Recruiting Prep Research Blueprint

> **Product tagline:** *"The recruiting cycle, reimagined with AI."*
> 
> **Core thesis:** IB recruiting is won in the details — technical mastery, strong behavioral stories, warm relationships, and firm-specific intel. Every existing tool handles one slice in isolation. Street Prep AI unifies the entire cycle.

---

## The Recruiting Reality (Product Context)

Investment banking summer analyst recruiting begins **15–18 months before the internship starts** — as early as Spring of sophomore year. Applications at bulge brackets open in January or February of sophomore year and run on a **rolling basis**, meaning early applicants get interviews before the deadline even closes.

Most students discover this too late. Your product's core value is meeting them at the start of their confusion and walking them through every phase of the cycle.

**Key filters banks apply before interviews:**
- GPA: 3.5+ at target schools; 3.7+ at non-target schools
- School prestige (ATS systems filter by university name)
- Relevant internship experience (ideally finance-related by sophomore summer)
- Finance club involvement

## The 6 Pillars of IB Prep

### Pillar 1 — Foundation & Timeline Awareness

**What candidates need to understand first:**
- The landscape: Bulge Bracket (GS, MS, JPM, BAML, Citi) vs. Elite Boutique (Evercore, Lazard, Moelis, PJT) vs. Middle Market (Jefferies, Houlihan Lokey, William Blair)
- The process flow: Application → HireVue → First Round → Superday → Offer
- Group types: M&A, Leveraged Finance, ECM/DCM, sector groups (TMT, Healthcare, Energy, FIG, Real Estate)
- The timeline: when each stage happens and what to be doing each semester from freshman through junior year

**Recruiting timeline by semester:**

| Semester | Key Actions |
|---|---|
| Freshman Fall | Join finance club, start networking broadly, build GPA |
| Freshman Spring | Pursue early insights / diversity programs, begin learning technical basics |
| Sophomore Summer | Secure a finance-adjacent internship (boutique, wealth mgmt, accounting) |
| Sophomore Fall | Attend bank info sessions and coffee chats; applications open at some firms |
| Sophomore Spring | **Peak recruiting season** — BB applications open Jan–Feb; HireVues; first rounds; Superdays; offers by April |
| Junior Fall | Off-cycle and boutique recruiting continues; some banks still open |
| Junior Spring | Last chance roles, boutiques, regional banks |

### Pillar 2 — Resume & Applications

**Resume conventions:**
- Strictly one page
- Reverse chronological order
- Bullet points: Action verb + what you did + quantified result
- GPA displayed if 3.5+; omit if below (unless strong enough to offset)
- Sections: Education → Experience → Activities/Leadership → Skills
- No objective statement; no photos; no color (most banks)
- ATS-friendly formatting: standard fonts, no tables or text boxes

**Cover letter (when required):**
- Three paragraphs max: why banking → why this firm → why you
- Name-drop a real person you spoke with at the firm — instantly differentiates
- Reference a specific deal the bank worked on
- Never use to explain weaknesses (low GPA, non-target school)
- One page maximum; match resume formatting

**Application strategy:**
- Apply the day applications open — rolling review means early = more interviews
- Target 30–40 firms across BB, EB, and MM
- Non-target students should lean heavily into MM and regional banks (40% of MM hires come from non-targets vs. ~9% at BBs)

### Pillar 3 — Networking & Coffee Chats

This is the most underserved prep area and one of the highest-leverage activities in recruiting.

**Why it matters:** A banker who knows you can push your resume directly to the recruiting team, bypassing ATS filters. 2–3 internal advocates at a firm dramatically increases interview odds.

#### Finding Bankers to Reach Out To
- LinkedIn alumni search: `[Your School] + Investment Banking`
- Finance club alumni networks
- Career center alumni database
- Search by non-target school — those bankers understand your path and are often more responsive

#### Cold Email / LinkedIn Outreach
- Keep emails under 6 sentences
- Personalize each one — reference something specific about their career or the firm
- Target school students: send 30–50 emails → expect 8–15 responses
- Semi-target students: send 50–100 emails
- Non-target students: send 100–200+ emails, focus on MM and boutiques
- Follow up once after 1 week if no response; then move on

**Cold email template structure:**
1. Who you are + school + year
2. Why you're reaching out (specific reason tied to their background or firm)
3. Simple ask: "Would you have 20 minutes for a call?"
4. One-line sign-off

#### Coffee Chat Prep
**Before the call:**
- Research their LinkedIn career path thoroughly
- Look up 2–3 recent deals the firm announced
- Prepare 10–15 questions (you'll use 5–8)

**During the call:**
- Let them talk 80% of the time
- Never ask about compensation or hours
- Ask about their path, the culture, what they wish they'd known
- Don't ask for a referral on the first call

**After the call:**
- Send a personalized thank-you within 24 hours referencing something specific they said
- Stay in touch every 2–3 months with a short update
- When recruiting opens, reach back out directly

**Questions to ask bankers:**
- "What made you choose [firm] over others you were considering?"
- "How would you describe the culture of your group?"
- "What does a typical first-year analyst's day look like?"
- "What do you wish you'd known going into recruiting?"
- "Is there anyone else on your team you'd recommend I speak with?"

### Pillar 4 — Technical Mastery

**This is the core of IB interview prep.** Candidates spend 2–3 months drilling, typically 1–3 hours daily in the months before Superdays.

#### The Canonical Study Sequence

Study in this order — each topic builds on the previous:

1. **Accounting & the 3 Financial Statements**
2. **Enterprise Value vs. Equity Value**
3. **Valuation: Comps, Precedent Transactions, DCF**
4. **M&A / Merger Model concepts**
5. **LBO Model concepts**
6. **Brain teasers & mental math**

---

#### Topic 1: Accounting & the 3 Financial Statements

**Key questions candidates must master:**
- Walk me through the 3 financial statements
- How do the 3 statements link to each other?
- If depreciation increases by $10, what happens to each statement?
- What is working capital? Operating working capital?
- Walk me through the cash flow statement
- What is deferred revenue? Deferred tax liability?
- How does an operating lease vs. capital lease affect the statements?

**Core concepts:**
- Income Statement → Balance Sheet → Cash Flow Statement linkage
- Net Income flows to Retained Earnings (Balance Sheet) and is the starting point for Cash Flow from Operations
- D&A: non-cash expense that reduces taxes but not cash
- Working Capital = Current Assets – Current Liabilities
- Operating Working Capital excludes cash and debt

---

#### Topic 2: Enterprise Value vs. Equity Value

**Key questions:**
- What is Enterprise Value? What is Equity Value?
- What is the formula for Enterprise Value?
- Walk me through the bridge from Equity Value to Enterprise Value
- Why can't you use Equity Value / EBITDA as a multiple?
- What happens to EV if you issue $50M of debt and hold it as cash?

**Core concepts:**
- Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interest – Cash
- EV represents the value of the whole business (available to all investors)
- Equity Value represents value to shareholders only
- Items that are "EV items" vs. "Equity Value items" — critical to understand

---

#### Topic 3: Valuation — Comps, Precedent Transactions, DCF

DCF alone accounts for ~23% of all reported IB interview questions — the single most tested topic.

**Comparable Company Analysis (Trading Comps):**
- Find a set of public companies similar in business model, size, growth, and geography
- Calculate key multiples: EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, P/E
- Apply those multiples to the target company's metrics to derive a valuation range
- Pros: market-based, current; Cons: no control premium, affected by market sentiment

**Precedent Transactions:**
- Use multiples paid in similar historical M&A deals
- Typically produces higher valuations than comps due to the **control premium**
- Pros: reflects what acquirers actually paid; Cons: stale data, hard to find true comparables

**Discounted Cash Flow (DCF):**
- Project Free Cash Flow 5–10 years out
- Calculate Terminal Value using: (a) Gordon Growth Model or (b) Exit Multiple Method
- Discount everything back at WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)
- Pros: intrinsic value, not dependent on market; Cons: highly sensitive to assumptions

**Key DCF questions:**
- Walk me through a DCF
- How do you calculate WACC?
- What is WACC and what does it represent?
- What is terminal value and how do you calculate it?
- Which produces the highest valuation: comps, precedents, or DCF?
- When would a DCF not be appropriate?

**Key formula:** `FCF = EBIT × (1 – Tax Rate) + D&A – CapEx – Change in Working Capital`

**Valuation hierarchy insight:** Precedent Transactions > Trading Comps (due to control premium). DCF is the most variable — highly dependent on assumptions.

#### Topic 4: M&A / Merger Models

**Key questions:**
- Is this deal accretive or dilutive? Walk me through how you'd determine that.
- What are synergies? How do you model them?
- What's the difference between a stock deal and a cash deal?
- Walk me through a merger model
- What is goodwill? How is it created in an acquisition?
- What is purchase price allocation?

**Core concepts:**
- **Accretive deal:** Acquirer's EPS increases post-acquisition
- **Dilutive deal:** Acquirer's EPS decreases post-acquisition
- **Accretion/Dilution test:** Compare the earnings yield of the target to the after-tax cost of the acquisition financing
- **Synergies:** Revenue synergies (cross-sell, pricing power) and cost synergies (headcount reduction, facility consolidation)
- **Goodwill** = Purchase Price – Fair Value of Net Assets acquired

---

#### Topic 5: LBO Models

**Key questions:**
- Walk me through an LBO
- What makes a good LBO candidate?
- What is the goal of an LBO model?
- How do you calculate returns in an LBO?
- What is a dividend recapitalization?

**Core concepts:**
- **LBO = Leveraged Buyout:** A PE firm acquires a company using a mix of equity (~30–40%) and debt (~60–70%)
- The goal: maximize IRR (Internal Rate of Return) on the equity invested
- Returns driven by: EBITDA growth, multiple expansion, and debt paydown
- **Good LBO candidate:** stable/predictable cash flows, strong market position, low existing debt, identifiable cost cuts, defensible industry

**LBO model structure:**
1. Sources & Uses (where the money comes from and goes)
2. Operating model (project revenue, EBITDA, FCF)
3. Debt schedule (model interest, amortization, cash sweep)
4. Returns analysis (IRR and MOIC at exit)

#### Study Method That Works

The most effective prep approach candidates use:

1. **Read** conceptual guides (BIWS, M&I, or AI-generated explanations) to understand the concept
2. **Drill Q&A** — flashcard-style, covering basic then advanced questions per topic
3. **Explain verbally** — practice saying answers out loud without notes (interviewers evaluate how you communicate, not just whether you know the answer)
4. **Timed mock sessions** — simulate interview conditions with a timer
5. **Spaced repetition** — revisit weak topics every 2–3 days

**Primary resources used by candidates:**
- BIWS "400 Questions" guide — the canonical reference
- Mergers & Inquisitions (M&I) website and articles
- Wall Street Prep (WSP) Red Book
- IB Vine — praised for comprehensive question bank and UI
- Rosenbaum & Pearl *Investment Banking* textbook — the industry gold standard for technical frameworks
- Wall Street Oasis forums — real interview reports and question banks

### Pillar 5 — Behavioral & Story Crafting

**The key insight most candidates miss:** Technical questions are pass/fail filters. Behavioral questions determine offers. Banks hire people they want to be around during 100-hour weeks.

#### The STAR Method

Structure every behavioral answer as:
- **Situation:** Set the scene (1–2 sentences, just enough context)
- **Task:** What were you responsible for?
- **Action:** What specifically did YOU do? (This should be 60–70% of your answer)
- **Result:** What was the outcome? Quantify if possible. What did you learn?

#### Building a Story Bank

Prepare 5–8 core stories that can flex across multiple question types. Stories should cover:

| Theme | Example source |
|---|---|
| Leadership | Leading a club project, managing a team, taking initiative |
| Overcoming failure | A bad exam, a project that didn't go well, a rejection |
| Conflict resolution | Disagreement with a teammate or supervisor |
| Teamwork | Group project success under pressure |
| Persuading someone | Convincing a professor, manager, or group to change course |
| Ethics / integrity | Facing a difficult decision with no easy answer |
| Why investment banking | A genuine spark moment — deal you read about, exposure through a class or internship |

#### The "Walk Me Through Your Resume" Answer

This is essentially a rehearsed 60–90 second narrative that threads your experiences into a coherent story ending in "...which is why I'm here today, excited about banking." Structure:

1. Brief background (school, hometown if relevant)
2. Experience 1 (earliest relevant) → what you learned
3. Experience 2 (more recent) → how it built on experience 1
4. Current position/activities
5. Why banking, why now

#### Firm-Specific Behavioral Prep

"Why [Bank]?" answers must never be generic. Required research:
- The bank's culture and stated values (publicly available)
- A specific group or deal that interests you
- The name of someone at the firm you've spoken with
- Something that differentiates this bank from competitors

**Bank culture differentiators (high-level):**
- Goldman Sachs: client service, teamwork, integrity
- J.P. Morgan: exceptional client service, operational excellence
- Morgan Stanley: doing the right thing, extraordinary ideas

### Pillar 6 — Interview Stage Preparation

#### HireVue (Pre-Recorded Video)
- Format: 3–6 questions, 30 seconds prep, 60–180 seconds to respond
- Almost entirely behavioral
- **Most common failure:** Never practicing under real time constraints before the actual interview
- Prep: Record yourself on your phone answering timed behavioral questions; watch it back
- Look at the camera, not the screen; speak at a measured pace; show genuine energy

#### First-Round Interview
- Mix of behavioral ("tell me about yourself," "why banking," "why this firm") and light technicals
- Light technicals: walk me through the 3 statements, what is DCF, what is enterprise value
- Answers must be crisp and correct — first round filters heavily on whether you know the basics
- Prepare 3–4 thoughtful questions to ask the interviewer at the end

#### Superday
- A full day of back-to-back 30-minute interviews with analysts, associates, VPs, and MDs
- Technical difficulty increases significantly
- Behavioral consistency matters — you'll tell your story 5–7 times in one day
- Energy management: bring water, stay composed, treat every interview as the last one
- Send thank-you emails to each interviewer within 24 hours

#### Questions to Ask Interviewers (Prepared List)
- "What has surprised you most about working here?"
- "How would you describe the culture of your group specifically?"
- "What does success look like in the first 90 days as an analyst?"
- "What kinds of deals has the group been working on recently?"
- "Looking back, what advice would you give yourself going into your first year?"

## Key Product Insights

**1. AI-generate the technical content.** The 400Q guide, BIWS, and WSP are all static PDFs that candidates find frustrating to use. You can generate infinite novel questions with varied parameters, real-time explanations, and adaptive follow-ups. This is your moat.

**2. Behavioral coaching is the biggest white space.** No existing tool coaches story-crafting well. An AI that takes raw resume experiences and helps build a polished, flexible story bank is genuinely novel.

**3. Coffee chat prep is completely underserved.** A tool that auto-researches a specific banker and generates a personalized prep sheet before the call doesn't exist anywhere.

**4. Non-target students are a high-urgency segment.** ~40% of middle-market hires come from non-targets; most tools ignore this segment's specific challenges (higher networking volume, different firm targeting, boutique-first strategy).

**5. The recruiting timeline creates urgency.** Students who discover the timeline too late are extremely motivated to catch up fast. A clear "where are you in the cycle" onboarding flow that meets students wherever they are creates immediate product-market fit.
