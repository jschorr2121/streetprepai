## What real estate actually covers

Real estate coverage splits primarily along property type and around a distinct legal structure: the **Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)**. REITs are companies that own, operate, and often develop income-producing real estate — office, retail, industrial/warehouse, multifamily residential, hotels, data centers, healthcare facilities — and are required by law to distribute the large majority of their taxable income to shareholders as dividends in exchange for favorable tax treatment at the corporate level. Not every real estate company is a REIT — homebuilders, real estate services firms, and privately held property owners are valued with standard tools — but REITs are common enough, and different enough, that they get their own dedicated section here.

## What deals look like

Real estate M&A activity includes REIT-to-REIT mergers (combining property portfolios to gain scale and diversify tenant concentration), private equity and institutional buyers taking REITs private when public market valuations lag the value of the underlying properties, and large portfolio or single-asset property transactions that don't involve a public company at all. Real estate is also unusually sensitive to interest rates relative to other sectors — since properties are typically financed with significant leverage and valued partly by discounting future income, moves in the cost of debt and in the required return on real estate investment (the cap rate, covered below) directly move valuations in a way that's more immediate than in most other industries.

## Why REITs use FFO instead of net income

Net income for a REIT is distorted by two large, non-cash-flow-representative items: **depreciation**, which is substantial for real estate given the size of the assets, and **gains or losses on property sales**, which show up as one-time swings that don't reflect the ongoing operating business. A REIT that owns a stable, fully-leased portfolio and sells one older building at a large gain could show a huge net income spike that has nothing to do with how well its core leasing business is performing that year — and the opposite is true if it sells a building at a loss.

**Funds From Operations (FFO)** strips both distortions out: FFO = Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization − Gains + Losses on property sales. This leaves a number that reflects the REIT's actual recurring operating performance, which is why REITs report FFO alongside (and often instead of) net income, and why the standard REIT valuation multiple is **Equity Value / FFO** rather than a P/E ratio. Note this multiple is built off equity value, not enterprise value — REITs are one of the few sectors, alongside FIG, where the primary multiple skips Enterprise Value entirely, because FFO already deducts net interest expense, making it an equity-level (not enterprise-level) cash flow metric.

**Adjusted Funds From Operations (AFFO)** goes a step further, adjusting FFO for items that are real cash costs even though they're not always obvious from the income statement — recurring maintenance CapEx needed just to keep existing properties in working condition, amortization of lease intangibles, and the straight-lining of rent (an accounting convention that smooths rental income evenly over a lease term rather than recognizing it as actually collected). AFFO is considered a closer approximation of true distributable cash flow, and it's the number most analysts actually use to judge whether a REIT's dividend is sustainable.

**Worked example.** Say a REIT reports net income of $180M for the year, including $210M of depreciation and a $40M gain on the sale of an office property. FFO = $180M + $210M − $40M = $350M. If the REIT trades at an equity value of $4.2B, its Equity Value / FFO multiple is 12.0x. Now suppose that same REIT needs $55M of recurring maintenance CapEx annually to keep its properties competitive, and has $15M of straight-line rent adjustments to back out. AFFO = $350M − $55M − $15M = $280M. If the REIT is paying out $260M in annual dividends, comparing that to AFFO (not FFO) tells you the payout ratio is a manageable 93% — comparing the same dividend to FFO instead would understate how tight that coverage actually is, since FFO hasn't accounted for the real cash the REIT needs to spend maintaining its properties.

## NAV and cap rates

Alongside FFO/AFFO multiples, REITs are commonly valued using a **Net Asset Value (NAV) model** — a balance-sheet-driven approach rather than an income-statement multiple. Instead of projecting cash flows for the whole company, you value each property individually (typically by capitalizing its net operating income), sum those values, subtract net debt and other liabilities, and arrive at an implied equity value per share.

The core tool in an NAV model is the **capitalization rate (cap rate)** — a property's annual net operating income divided by its value, expressed as a percentage. Rearranged, Property Value = Net Operating Income / Cap Rate. A lower cap rate implies a higher property value for the same income (investors are accepting a lower yield, typically because the property or market is viewed as lower-risk or higher-growth), and a higher cap rate implies a lower value for the same income. Cap rates vary by property type, market, and asset quality — a well-located multifamily property in a supply-constrained city might trade at a 4.5% cap rate, while an older suburban office property might trade at 8% or higher given weaker demand fundamentals.

**Quick NAV example.** A property generates $6M of annual net operating income and comparable properties in that market are trading at a 6.0% cap rate. Implied property value = $6M / 6.0% = $100M. If a REIT owns ten similar properties worth $100M each ($1B total), carries $400M of net debt, and has 40M shares outstanding, implied NAV per share = ($1B − $400M) / 40M shares = $15.00. Comparing that $15.00 NAV per share to the REIT's current trading price tells you whether the public market is valuing the portfolio above or below what the underlying properties are worth individually.

## Terminology to know cold

- **Occupancy rate** — the percentage of a property's rentable space currently leased; the most basic health indicator for any REIT.
- **Same-store NOI growth** — the REIT equivalent of same-store sales: net operating income growth from properties owned for the full comparison period, isolating organic performance from acquisitions or new development.
- **Triple net lease** — a lease structure where the tenant pays property taxes, insurance, and maintenance directly, shifting most operating cost risk off the landlord; common in single-tenant retail and industrial REITs.
- **Development pipeline** — properties a REIT is currently building or has planned, representing future NOI not yet reflected in current financials.
- **Payout ratio** — dividends paid divided by FFO or AFFO; how much of distributable cash flow is actually being paid out versus retained.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect "why do REITs use FFO instead of net income?" as a standard opening question in this group — the answer centers on depreciation and property-sale gains/losses distorting net income for asset-heavy businesses. You'll likely also get asked to explain how a cap rate works, or to walk through what happens to property values as cap rates compress or expand (lower cap rate → higher value for the same income, and vice versa) — a relationship that trips up candidates who haven't internalized that cap rates move inversely to valuation for a fixed income stream.

## Common mistakes

- **Using net income or a P/E multiple as the primary valuation tool for a REIT.** This is the fastest way to signal you haven't studied the sector; FFO or AFFO should be the first thing you reach for.
- **Confusing FFO and AFFO as interchangeable.** AFFO is the more conservative, cash-flow-accurate number, and it's the one that matters when judging dividend sustainability — conflating the two in an interview reads as imprecise.
- **Getting the cap rate relationship backwards.** A lower cap rate means a higher implied value for the same income, not a lower one — a common slip under interview pressure.
