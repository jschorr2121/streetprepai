## The Evercore identity

Evercore is the largest and one of the most prestigious elite boutiques in investment banking. Founded by Roger Altman in 1995, the firm built its reputation doing one thing: providing independent, senior-led M&A advisory to the world's most important companies and boards. No underwriting, no sales and trading, no balance sheet to offer — just advice.

That purity is a genuine culture driver. At Evercore, there are no conflicts from proprietary capital or underwriting relationships. An Evercore banker's only job is to give the client the best strategic advice possible. Candidates who genuinely value that model — and can articulate why it appeals to them — stand out in a way they wouldn't if they said the same thing at a bulge bracket.

The firm has a reputation for being intellectually intense, entrepreneurial, and meritocratic. The partner class is tightly managed; promotion from senior banker to Partner is a real achievement, not a given. That creates an environment where people are very focused on doing excellent work and building strong client relationships rather than climbing an internal political ladder.

## Business and group structure

Evercore operates as a pure advisory firm:

**Advisory:** M&A, activist defense, restructuring, strategic advisory. This is the core.

**Equity research and institutional equities:** Separate businesses that don't create advisory conflicts.

Coverage groups include Technology, Healthcare, Consumer, Industrials, Energy, Financial Sponsors, Restructuring, and Sovereign/Public Finance. The firm has offices in New York, London, San Francisco, Houston, and several international locations.

Evercore's **restructuring** practice has historically been a strength — the firm has advised on some of the most significant Chapter 11 cases and out-of-court restructurings. If restructuring interests you, Evercore is a top destination.

## What Evercore looks for

**Genuine interest in advisory work, not just the brand.** Evercore interviewers have a sharp radar for candidates who want "an EB" generically and have landed on Evercore by process of elimination. They want to see that you understand and value the pure advisory model.

**Intellectual seriousness.** Evercore's culture rewards people who go deep — on deal mechanics, on industry analysis, on client strategy. Candidates who bring intellectual curiosity and genuine analytical ambition fit well.

**Entrepreneurial instinct.** The firm is smaller than bulge brackets, which means analysts take on more responsibility earlier. People who are energized by that — rather than wanting more structure and handholding — thrive at Evercore.

**Communication quality.** Because client interaction happens earlier and more directly at a boutique, Evercore cares about whether you can present clearly and hold a room. Your behavioral answers should demonstrate that you've operated confidently in high-stakes situations.

## Interview process

Evercore's recruiting can happen through on-campus visits, info sessions, and coffee chats (networking matters more at boutiques than at bulge brackets). The interview structure typically involves:

1. **First-round** with junior bankers — Technical and behavioral. Technicals at Evercore can be quite deep; they may ask you to walk through a full DCF or dig into nuanced accounting questions.
2. **Second round / superday** — Multiple rounds, including senior bankers. Behavioral questions often probe for leadership, judgment under ambiguity, and intellectual passion.

Networking before the interview is more important at Evercore than at a bulge bracket. Reach out early, have substantive conversations, and express specific interest in the firm's work. Being known before your resume lands in the pile genuinely matters.

## Technical expectations

Evercore's technical bar is high and the interviews can go deeper than some bulge brackets because the deal teams are smaller and expect more from day one. Expect:

- Full DCF, including terminal value methodology choices and sensitivity analysis
- Detailed accounting linkages (know the $10 depreciation question cold)
- Valuation methodology comparisons and when each is appropriate
- Restructuring concepts if interviewing for that group: priority of claims, enterprise value vs. reorganization value, DIP financing

## Restructuring at Evercore

If restructuring interests you, Evercore is one of the most prestigious places to do it. Key things to know:

- Restructuring bankers are hired in both good markets and bad — the pipeline is more counter-cyclical than M&A
- Technical prep should include: enterprise value vs. equity value in distress (when EV is less than debt face value, equity is worthless), absolute priority rule, plan of reorganization structure
- Read about a notable Chapter 11 case (Evercore's roles in high-profile restructurings are often publicly disclosed) and be able to discuss it

## What "why Evercore" sounds like when it works

> "I've done enough coffee chats with people at bulge brackets and boutiques to have a clear sense of what I want: I want to be in the room where strategic advice is given at the highest level, without the conflicts that come from balance sheet banking. Evercore's model is built around that. And honestly, the people I've spoken with there have been unusually sharp and direct — the culture sounds like one where you get better faster."

That answer is honest, specific, and shows you've done real research.

## Practice this

Read about one M&A deal or restructuring advisory that Evercore has been publicly credited with in the last two years. Understand the strategic context: who hired them, what advice they likely provided, how the deal played out. Then ask yourself: why would that company hire a boutique instead of a bulge bracket for this mandate? The answer to that question is the core of the Evercore value proposition.
