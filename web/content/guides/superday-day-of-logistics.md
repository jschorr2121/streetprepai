## What a superday actually looks like

A superday (also called a final round or on-site round) is typically 3–6 back-to-back interviews over half a day, sometimes a full day, either in person at the office or virtually. You'll meet people across seniority levels — analysts, associates, VPs, and sometimes MDs. Each round is a fresh conversation; interviewers typically don't coordinate their questions in real time, though they'll debrief afterward.

The structural challenge of a superday is not any individual interview — it's staying sharp, present, and consistent across 4–6 rounds when you're nervous and running on adrenaline. That's a skill you prepare for separately from the content.

## The night before

### 1. Finalize your content review (90 minutes maximum)

Do not try to learn new material the night before a superday. You should be reviewing, not cramming. Run through the key concepts you've already prepared: your resume walkthrough, three or four behavioral stories in STAR format, your "why this firm" answer, and the top five technical concepts (three statements, DCF, WACC, comps, accretion/dilution). Read them once, say each answer out loud once.

Then stop. Additional cramming past 90 minutes creates anxiety without adding retention.

### 2. Prepare your materials

- Print 5–7 copies of your resume (some offices still want a physical copy)
- Prepare a small notepad and a pen (for taking notes between rounds or if you're given scratch paper for math questions)
- Look up the exact address and floor (for in-person), or test your video call setup and link (for virtual)
- Know who your recruiting contact is and have their number in your phone

### 3. Prepare your outfit

This is a remove-the-decision moment. Hang your suit, lay out your shirt, shoes, and accessories the night before. For in-person: conservative suit (navy or charcoal), pressed shirt, simple tie (for men), simple professional attire (for women). No surprises in the morning.

### 4. Sleep

Genuinely prioritize this. 7–8 hours is not a luxury — it is a performance input. Your ability to think under pressure, recall information, and maintain affect is materially worse when you're tired. No late-night cramming. Set two alarms.

## The morning of

### Eat breakfast

Doesn't need to be elaborate. Needs to exist. Low blood sugar mid-superday is a real thing, and the crash is invisible until it isn't.

### Arrive early (or log on early)

For in-person: arrive at the building 15–20 minutes early. Wait in the lobby or nearby. Walk into the reception area 10 minutes before your scheduled time.

For virtual: log on 5–10 minutes early. Have your space set up: clean background, good lighting (facing a window, not backlit), earbuds or headset tested, a glass of water, and your resume on the desk.

### The check-in conversation matters

When you meet the recruiter or the first analyst coordinator, be warm and engaged. This is not an interview yet, but the people you're friendly and natural with in the lobby or waiting room often circle back with impressions. Don't perform — just be a person.

## Between rounds

### Reset mentally

Each interview is a new conversation with someone who has no idea how the previous one went. Don't carry nervousness or momentum from one round into the next. Between rounds: take two slow breaths, drink some water, and remind yourself that this is a fresh start.

### Review your notes if you have time

Keep a small notecard with the topics you want to hit — your STAR stories, your "why this firm" close, one or two technical frameworks. Glancing at it between rounds can bring something back to mind without requiring memory under pressure.

### Don't debrief out loud

If you're doing an in-person superday with other candidates (some banks run group superdays where multiple candidates are present), resist the urge to debrief your interview with them in the hallway. The comparison game is not useful, and what they were asked tells you nothing about what you'll be asked next.

### A note on the lunch or group dinner

Some superdays include a group lunch or dinner with junior bankers — analysts and associates. This is not a break. Interviewers are watching: are you engaging naturally with the group? Are you respectful to everyone in the room, not just the most senior person? Are you curious and warm, or do you seem like you're just grinding through?

The right mindset: genuine interest in the people, no shop talk about other interviews or offers, and normal human conversation. Ask people about their experience, their favorite project, how they got into the group. This is actually the easiest part of the day if you treat it like a real conversation rather than an extended audition.

## After the last round

Thank your recruiting contact warmly before you leave. If you can, quickly write down any memorable details from each conversation — topics discussed, names, something specific they said. You'll want this for your thank-you emails.

Send a brief thank-you note to each interviewer within 24 hours. A few sentences, something specific to your conversation, and genuine appreciation for their time. This is not a formality — bankers remember students who were thoughtful in follow-up.

## What to do if a round goes badly

Every superday has one round that feels harder than the rest — a technical question you fumbled, a behavioral answer that didn't land, an MD who seemed unimpressed. This is normal. The debrief process after a superday accounts for variance across rounds. One rough interview does not end your candidacy.

The mistake is letting one bad round poison the next one. Notice the setback, reset, and walk into the next room fully present.

## Practice this

The week before your superday, do a full mock interview day with a friend or career advisor: three to four interviews back to back with five-minute breaks between. The goal isn't to practice perfect answers — it's to practice staying focused and natural for three hours straight. That endurance is the thing most people don't prepare for.
