## Why the first round is different

A first-round IB interview is rarely a deep technical exam. It's a filter. The banker on the other end of the line usually has six or eight of these calls stacked back to back, and their job is to answer one question: is this person worth flying in for a superday? That means the call rewards clarity and composure over encyclopedic knowledge. You need to be fast, organized, and likeable in roughly the same breath — and you need to do it without seeing the interviewer's face half the time, since most first rounds still happen by phone.

## How the 30 minutes actually breaks down

Assume a 30-minute slot and plan for it explicitly, because interviewers rarely announce the transition points.

- Minutes 0-3: small talk and scheduling logistics
- Minutes 3-10: "walk me through your resume" plus one or two fit follow-ups
- Minutes 10-22: technical questions, usually accounting first, then valuation, sometimes a quick brainteaser or market question
- Minutes 22-27: two or three more fit or "why this firm" questions
- Minutes 27-30: your questions for them

That middle technical block is roughly 12 minutes. If the interviewer asks five technical questions in that window, you have about two and a half minutes per answer including their follow-ups. A 90-second answer to "walk me through a DCF" is generous; a four-minute answer eats the time budget for two other questions and reads as rambling, not thoroughness. Practice compressing your core technical answers — DCF walkthrough, the three financial statements, an LBO overview — to 60-90 seconds each, then let follow-up questions pull out the depth. Don't front-load every caveat into the first answer.

## Mixing fit and technical without whiplash

The hardest skill in a first round isn't any single answer — it's the gear shift. You'll go from "tell me about a time you worked on a team" straight into "walk me through how a $10 write-down in inventory flows through the three statements," often with no transition. Two habits make that shift smooth:

1. **Keep a mental buffer, not a script.** Have three fit stories (a leadership moment, a failure, a conflict) that you can recombine to answer almost any behavioral prompt, so you're retrieving rather than composing under pressure. That frees working memory for the technical switch.
2. **Reset your tone on the handoff.** Fit answers should sound conversational and a little slower; technical answers should sound crisp and structured, often with a one-line preview ("There are three ways to think about this — I'll focus on the two that matter here"). Interviewers notice when a candidate answers a technical question in the same rambling cadence as a "tell me about yourself" — it reads as not knowing the material cold.

## Worked example: pacing a technical answer under the clock

Say you get asked: "A company buys a machine for $50,000 cash, depreciated straight-line over 5 years with no salvage value. Walk me through year one on all three statements."

A well-paced 75-second answer:

- Income statement: depreciation expense of $50,000 / 5 = $10,000 reduces EBIT by $10,000. At a 25% tax rate, net income falls by $10,000 × (1 − 0.25) = $7,500.
- Cash flow statement: net income is down $7,500, but depreciation is a non-cash expense, so you add back the full $10,000. Combined effect on cash from operations: −$7,500 + $10,000 = +$2,500. Separately, the $50,000 purchase hits investing activities as a −$50,000 outflow. Net change in cash for the year: $2,500 − $50,000 = −$47,500.
- Balance sheet: cash is down $47,500, PP&E is up $50,000 gross but down $10,000 in accumulated depreciation, so PP&E's net book value rose by $40,000. Retained earnings falls by $7,500 from the net income hit. Check: assets change = −$47,500 (cash) + $40,000 (net PP&E) = −$7,500. Liabilities are unchanged, so equity must also fall $7,500 — which matches the retained earnings drop. Balance sheet balances.

Notice the shape: state the number, show one line of arithmetic, state the result, move to the next statement. No interviewer wants you narrating your own thought process line by line — they want the answer with just enough math shown that they can verify you didn't guess.

## The reverse question is not a formality

When the interviewer says "what questions do you have for me," treat it as a scored part of the interview, not a courtesy exit ramp. Skip anything answerable from the firm's website (asking about "the culture" or "a typical day" reads as not having done homework). Instead, ask something that only this specific person can answer well — about a live deal they can discuss at a high level, how their group staffs analysts across sectors, or what surprised them most in their own first year. One sharp, specific question beats three generic ones; you don't need to fill the full three minutes, and a good exchange here is often what the interviewer remembers when writing you up.

## Phone vs. video etiquette

Many first rounds are still audio-only. On a call, over-articulate transitions ("First, on the income statement... second, on cash flow...") since the interviewer can't see you gesturing through your logic, and a short silence reads as dead air rather than thinking time — say "let me think through that for a second" rather than going quiet. On video, treat it like an in-person meeting: camera at eye level, plain background, and look at the camera lens (not the screen) when you're delivering your answer, since eye contact reads through the lens, not through where your eyes are actually pointed on-screen.

## Common mistakes

- Spending five minutes on the resume walkthrough and running the technical block short — practice a 90-second version.
- Answering every technical question with the same over-caveated cadence used for fit questions, instead of shifting to a crisper register.
- Treating the reverse question as filler and asking something generic that signals no real research.
- On phone calls, going silent while thinking instead of narrating that you're thinking — silence over the phone reads as being stuck, not composed.
