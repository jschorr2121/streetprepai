## The Goldman identity

Goldman Sachs has a reputation that precedes it into every recruiting conversation, and that reputation is both an asset and a challenge when you're recruiting. Yes, it's the most recognized name in investment banking. But that recognition means the interviewing standard is high, the competition is intense, and interviewers have a finely tuned sense for candidates who want "Goldman" as a brand versus candidates who actually understand what working there means.

The culture is intensely meritocratic, collaborative within teams, and — more than almost any other bank — built around the sense that individuals succeed when the institution succeeds. Goldman calls itself a partnership, and even post-IPO, that ethos of shared ownership and collective responsibility runs through the culture in observable ways.

## Group structure and recruiting

Goldman recruits into **Investment Banking Division (IBD)** at the Summer Analyst level across its core coverage and product groups:

**Coverage groups (industry-aligned):** Technology, Media & Telecom (TMT), Healthcare, Consumer Retail, Natural Resources, Financial Institutions Group (FIG), Real Estate, Public Sector.

**Product groups:** Mergers & Acquisitions (M&A), Leveraged Finance, Equity Capital Markets (ECM), Debt Capital Markets (DCM).

Most Summer Analysts are placed into a group after an initial generalist pool or express preferences during the offer process. Some offices do rotational programs first-year.

Goldman's **Global Banking & Markets** division has expanded significantly — the bank has a substantial markets and FICC business alongside IBD. Understanding that Goldman is more than just advisory banking is useful context when you're discussing "why Goldman."

## What Goldman looks for

Goldman interviewers consistently evaluate three things:

**Commercial instinct.** Can you think about a business not just analytically but strategically? Goldman advises its clients — it's not just a modeling shop. Candidates who can connect financial analysis to real business judgment stand out.

**Intellectual curiosity.** Goldman's culture rewards people who go deeper. The best interview answers at Goldman don't just answer the question — they surface an interesting adjacent thought or acknowledge a genuine complexity.

**Fit with the partnership model.** Goldman's team structure requires people who are confident enough to contribute but humble enough to recognize that the analysis is in service of the client outcome, not their own glory. Candidates who are aggressively self-promotional can feel off-key in Goldman interviews.

## Interview process

Goldman's IB recruiting typically involves:

1. **HireVue or first-round screening** — Video interview with behavioral and technical questions. Prepare the same way you'd prepare for a live first round.
2. **First-round interviews** — Often phone or video, with one or two analysts/associates. Technical screens plus behavioral.
3. **Superday** — Multiple rounds at the office (or virtually) with analysts, associates, VPs, and sometimes MDs. Expect a mix of technical, behavioral, and market knowledge questions.

Goldman is known for asking thoughtful behavioral questions in addition to technical ones. They want to understand how you think, not just whether you can answer a WACC question.

## Technical expectations

Goldman's technical bar is high but not unusual for a bulge bracket. Expect:

- Three-statement linkages and accounting questions
- Full DCF walkthrough (see the guide "Walk Me Through a DCF")
- Valuation methodology comparisons
- Basic M&A accretion/dilution
- WACC and CAPM
- Possibly one market or macro question: "Where do you see interest rates going, and what's your view?"

The market question is where Goldman often distinguishes candidates. Have a clear, defensible view on one or two macro or market topics. Don't say "I think rates will go up because of inflation" and stop — explain the transmission mechanism to credit markets, to equity multiples, to M&A activity.

## Recent deal activity to know

Goldman is an advisor on some of the largest M&A transactions globally. Look up Goldman's publicly announced deals in the last 6–12 months in the sector you're interviewing for. Goldman's press page and financial news (Bloomberg, Reuters, WSJ deal tracker) are good sources. Know one deal in depth — not just the headline, but the strategic rationale and what Goldman's advisory role was.

## What "why Goldman" actually sounds like

A strong "why Goldman" for a bulge bracket should include something specific about:
- A deal Goldman advised on that you found strategically interesting
- A specific group (if you know where you want to work) and why Goldman's franchise in that sector is distinctive
- Something from a coffee chat with a Goldman banker — a specific detail about team culture or how work gets done

Weak "why Goldman": "Goldman is the top-ranked investment bank and I want to work with the best clients on the biggest deals." Every single candidate says this.

## Practice this

Before your Goldman interview, read three Goldman-advised deal announcements from the past year. For each one, understand: what was the strategic rationale, who was the buyer, and what did Goldman's role likely look like (advising buy-side, sell-side, or both)? Then be ready to discuss one of them intelligently if asked about recent market activity.
