## Why banks use recorded video

Most bulge brackets and many elite boutiques now run their first-round screen as a recorded, one-way video interview rather than a live call. The bank sends you a link, you answer a fixed set of questions on camera with no interviewer present, and a mix of software and human reviewers scores the recording afterward. From the bank's side, this replaces hundreds of hours of live analyst time spent on first rounds with a format that can process thousands of candidates on a fixed timeline — which is exactly why it shows up disproportionately at firms with the largest applicant pools.

The content doesn't change much from a live first round: you still get standard fit questions ("why banking," "why this firm," "walk me through your resume") and sometimes a technical question or two. What changes is the format, and the format is what trips people up.

## The format, mechanically

- **Fixed prep time.** You typically get around **30 seconds** to read each question and think before recording starts. There's no rephrasing, no "can you repeat that" — you read it once and the clock runs.
- **Fixed answer time.** Most platforms cap your response around **2 minutes** per question, and the recording often stops automatically at the limit, mid-sentence if you're not done.
- **No do-overs.** Some platforms allow one retake per question; many allow none. You need to treat the first take as the take.
- **No interviewer feedback.** There's no nod, no follow-up, no chance to read the room and adjust. You're speaking into a camera with a countdown timer on screen, which is a genuinely different skill from speaking to a person.

## How scoring actually works

Recorded interviews are graded through a combination of automated analysis and human review, and the automated layer is scoring dimensions most candidates don't think to optimize for:

- **Structure.** Does your answer have a clear shape — a point up front, supporting detail, a close — or does it wander? Rambling, unstructured answers score worse even when the content is fine, because structure is one of the more reliably measurable signals in a scored transcript.
- **Vocabulary and word choice.** Precise, finance-appropriate language (specific deal terms, specific reasons rather than vague ones) reads as more competent than filler-heavy, generic phrasing, both to a human reviewer and to automated language analysis.
- **Confidence signals.** Pace, filler words ("um," "like," "you know"), and vocal steadiness are tracked. This doesn't mean fake enthusiasm — it means eliminating the verbal tics that read as uncertainty even when your content is strong.
- **Eye contact and framing (the human-review layer).** Look at the camera lens, not the screen — it's the difference between reading as engaged versus reading as distracted. Frame yourself centered, well-lit, with a plain background.

The practical takeaway: because part of the scoring is mechanical, a well-structured 90-second answer with clean pacing reliably outperforms a rambling 2-minute answer that technically covers more ground. Concision is rewarded, not penalized.

## Numerical, verbal, and logical reasoning tests

Separate from the video interview, many firms — especially in EMEA, and increasingly at US firms as a supplementary screen — administer standardized online tests before or alongside the first round. Three types show up repeatedly:

- **Numerical reasoning.** Data interpretation questions built around a table or chart: percentage changes, ratios, unit conversions, done under a strict per-question time limit (often 60–90 seconds per question).
- **Verbal reasoning.** Short business-context passages followed by true/false/cannot-say questions testing whether you can draw only the conclusions the passage actually supports.
- **Logical reasoning.** Pattern and sequence questions with no finance content at all — testing raw processing speed and abstract reasoning under time pressure.

### Worked example: a numerical reasoning question

A typical question gives you a small table and asks you to compute something under time pressure. Try this one:

> A firm's revenue by region last year was: North America $420M, Europe $310M, Asia $185M. This year, North America grew 8%, Europe grew 5%, and Asia grew 20%. What is total company revenue this year, and what percentage of total revenue does Asia represent this year?

Work it in the order the test rewards — fast, not elegant:

1. North America this year: $420M × 1.08 = $453.6M
2. Europe this year: $310M × 1.05 = $325.5M
3. Asia this year: $185M × 1.20 = $222.0M
4. Total this year: $453.6M + $325.5M + $222.0M = $1,001.1M
5. Asia's share: $222.0M / $1,001.1M = 22.2%

Notice the two things the test is actually checking: can you apply a percentage growth rate correctly (multiply by 1 + growth rate, not just add the percentage as a flat dollar figure), and can you compute a share of a total accurately under time pressure without a calculator, since many platforms restrict or ban calculator use. Getting the mechanics right matters more than getting the arithmetic to extra decimal places — round sensibly and move to the next question.

## How to practice

- **For recorded video:** record yourself answering your five most likely fit questions with a real 30-second prep timer and a real 2-minute answer timer, using your phone. Watch the playback with the sound off first — you'll notice posture, fidgeting, and eye contact issues you'd otherwise miss while focused on content. Then watch again with sound to check pacing and filler words.
- **For numerical/verbal/logical tests:** timed practice sets are the only real preparation, because the skill being tested is speed under pressure, not raw ability. Do sets at the actual per-question time limit, not untimed — untimed practice builds false confidence, since a correct answer worked out slowly doesn't transfer to a 60-second constraint.
- **Build the reflex for percentage math specifically.** Growth rates, margins, and share-of-total calculations dominate these tests. See [mental math that transfers](/guide/mental-math-that-transfers) for drills that build the same reflexes you'll need here.

## Common mistakes

- **Treating the recorded format as lower-stakes than a live interview.** It isn't — it's frequently the harder cut, since it processes the largest applicant pool with the least room for a bad take to be forgiven by an interviewer's goodwill.
- **Writing out a full script and reading it.** It's visible on camera, it kills the vocal-confidence scoring signal, and if you lose your place you have no live interviewer to help you recover. Prepare bullet-point structures, not scripts.
- **Running out the clock mid-sentence.** If you're not close to done at the time warning, wrap the point you're on rather than starting a new one — an unfinished thought scores worse than a slightly shorter, complete one.
- **Skipping timed practice for the numerical tests.** Most people can solve the practice questions untimed. The test isn't measuring whether you can do the math — it's measuring whether you can do it fast, and that only improves with timed repetition.
