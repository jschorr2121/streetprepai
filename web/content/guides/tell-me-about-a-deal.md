## What this question is really testing

"Tell me about a deal you've been following" sits at the intersection of behavioral and technical. Interviewers want to see two things: that you're reading the news and engaging with financial markets, and that you can analyze a transaction with some depth — not just recite the headline number.

A student who says "Broadcom acquired VMware for $69 billion" has read the news. A student who says "Broadcom acquired VMware for $69 billion and the core strategic logic was pivoting from a semiconductor-focused company into an enterprise software platform with recurring revenue — which changes the multiples the market assigns the combined entity, and also creates interesting questions about whether the margin structure holds post-integration" has thought about it.

You want to be the second kind.

## How to pick the right deal

### Choose a deal you actually understand

Don't pick a deal just because it's big or famous. If you can't speak intelligently to the strategic rationale, the financing structure, and the outcome, pick something else. Interviewers will ask follow-ups. "I liked that it was big" is not a follow-up answer.

### Choose a relatively recent deal

Something announced or closed in the last 12–18 months is safest. Older deals raise the question of why you've been following this one rather than something current.

### Choose a deal with clear strategic logic

The best deals to discuss have an interesting "why" — a strategic pivot, a response to competitive pressure, a consolidation play, a financially engineered return. The Marvell/Innovium acquisition (networking chips, AI infrastructure thesis), the ExxonMobil/Pioneer deal (Permian scale), the Microsoft/Activision deal (gaming and subscription ecosystem) all have a coherent narrative beyond "big company bought smaller company."

### Match the deal to the firm you're interviewing with

If you're interviewing for the TMT group at a firm, discussing a tech deal shows intentionality. If you're at a firm known for energy M&A, knowing an energy transaction in depth signals that you've done your homework on the firm's business.

## The structure of a good deal discussion

Use this as a loose guide — don't recite it rigidly, but make sure you hit each element:

### 1. What the deal was (30 seconds)

Acquirer, target, approximate deal value, structure (cash/stock/mix), when it was announced or closed. Keep it crisp — they know you can Google.

> "The ExxonMobil/Pioneer deal, announced in late 2023, was an all-stock acquisition for roughly $60 billion. It was one of the largest oil and gas deals in decades."

### 2. The strategic rationale (60 seconds)

This is the meat. Why did the buyer do this? What problem does it solve? What does the combined entity look like?

> "For Exxon, the thesis was Permian Basin scale — Pioneer had one of the largest positions in the Delaware Basin, and Exxon's view is that low-cost, large-scale production is the right strategy in a world where energy transition is happening over decades, not years. Combining the acreage dramatically reduces Exxon's per-barrel costs through operating leverage and shared infrastructure. The strategic bet is that the Permian will be one of the last major hydrocarbon plays standing as the energy mix shifts."

### 3. The financial logic (30 seconds)

How was it priced? Does the deal math make sense? Any synergy targets mentioned?

> "The price looked fair relative to precedents in the space — roughly in line with how the market was valuing high-quality Permian assets. Exxon guided to significant cost synergies through combined operations. The all-stock structure meant no new debt, which matters when you're acquiring at a point where energy prices could move significantly."

### 4. Your view or one open question (15–20 seconds)

This is what separates a good discussion from a great one. What do you find interesting, uncertain, or worth watching?

> "The thing I'm watching is integration. Pioneer was an operator with a specific culture — Exxon is much more process-driven. How they retain operational talent post-close will matter a lot to whether the production targets are achievable."

## Handling follow-up questions

Expect at least one follow-up. Common ones:

- "Do you think they overpaid?" (Have a view, defend it briefly, acknowledge uncertainty)
- "How would you value the target?" (Run through the methodology — comps, precedents, NAV for resource companies)
- "What happens to the combined company's balance sheet?" (Know whether they took on debt, how leverage changed)
- "What's the risk?" (Integration, commodity prices, regulatory, strategic pivot risk)

You don't need to know everything. You need to be able to reason through what you don't know. "That's a great question — I'd want to look at the reserve replacement rate and how production is distributed across their acreage before forming a strong view on that" is a perfectly good answer.

## Practice drill

Pick one deal announced in the last 12 months that you can actually explain. Read the press release, one or two news articles analyzing the deal, and if the target is public, look at the proxy statement which often includes a fairness opinion. Build a 2-minute version of the deal using the structure above. Then have a friend ask you two follow-up questions and answer them without notes. Do this for at least two different deals before your first interview.
