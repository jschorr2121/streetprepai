## The question behind the question

"Why would Company A want to buy Company B?" sounds like a warm-up question, but it's testing something specific: can you separate a rationale you could defend with numbers from a rationale that's really just a good story? Interviewers ask this constantly because most real deals are a mix of both, and knowing which parts are which shows you understand how M&A actually gets decided — not just how it gets modeled.

## Financial rationale: the part you can defend with a spreadsheet

Financial reasons are ones you could, in principle, back into with a projection and a return calculation. A banker pitching the deal internally could put a number next to each one:

- **Consolidation and cost synergies.** Combine the #2 and #3 players in a fragmented market and you can close overlapping facilities, cut duplicate corporate functions, and get better volume pricing from suppliers.
- **Geographic or channel expansion.** A buyer strong in the Midwest acquires a competitor with a built-out presence on the coasts, buying distribution it would otherwise take years to build organically.
- **Market share consolidation.** In a commoditized market where nobody is growing, buying a competitor is often the fastest way to grow revenue at all.
- **Undervaluation.** The target's stock has sold off and the buyer believes the market is wrong about the business's long-term earning power.
- **Product or end-market diversification.** A buyer in a maturing category acquires a business in an adjacent, faster-growing category to change its own growth trajectory.

The common thread: you could write a sentence like "we'll cross-sell into 15% of their customer base, generating $9 million of incremental annual revenue," and someone could go check whether that happened.

## "Fuzzy" rationale: the part that's harder to prove

Fuzzy reasons are the ones where the buyer can't really quantify the payoff in advance, even though the deal might still make sense:

- **Acqui-hires.** The buyer isn't paying for revenue or a customer base — it's paying to shortcut hiring 40 engineers one at a time.
- **Defensive acquisitions.** The buyer is worried a smaller, faster-moving competitor will eventually eat its lunch, so it removes the threat by buying it.
- **Technology or IP land-grabs.** The target owns a patent portfolio or a piece of technology the buyer thinks it will need eventually, even if it can't say exactly how much that's worth today.
- **Prestige and scale for its own sake.** Some large deals happen because the acquirer's leadership wants to be bigger, not because the math clearly works. This is more common than bankers like to admit, especially above the $10 billion mark.

Fuzzy rationale isn't automatically bad — some acqui-hires and defensive deals turn out to be the smartest move a company makes. It's fuzzy because the payoff can't be modeled with confidence beforehand, which means it's harder to know if the price paid was reasonable until well after the fact.

In practice, most large deals get pitched using financial language even when the real driver is fuzzy. A CEO who wants to buy a competitor for ego or fear of disruption will still ask the deal team to build a synergy case, because "we were scared of them" doesn't work in a board presentation.

## Merger vs. acquisition — a size distinction, not a mechanical one

The words get used almost interchangeably, but there's a real difference: in a **merger**, the two companies are roughly comparable in size (often within 2–3x of each other by revenue or market cap), and the deal is frequently structured as a stock-for-stock combination presented as a partnership. In an **acquisition**, the buyer is meaningfully larger and is clearly absorbing the smaller company.

Mechanically, a merger model works identically either way — you're still combining income statements, allocating a purchase price, and testing accretion/dilution. The label mostly affects tone (a "merger of equals" press release) and occasionally the deal structure (mergers of near-equal companies more often use stock, since neither side wants to write a huge check).

## How a sell-side process actually runs

If you're advising the seller, the process typically moves through five stages:

1. **Plan and prepare materials.** You and the management team build a **teaser** — a short, anonymized 5–10 page document that describes the business (recurring revenue, long-term customer contracts, low capital intensity — whatever makes it attractive) without naming it — and a much longer **Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM)**, often 50–100 pages, that lays out the full financial and operating picture for buyers who sign a non-disclosure agreement.
2. **Contact buyers.** You send the teaser to a curated list of potential acquirers. Interested parties sign an NDA and receive the CIM plus additional data.
3. **Management meetings.** Buyers who stay interested meet the company's leadership team, usually via a formal management presentation, and get to ask direct questions.
4. **Solicit bids.** You set a deadline for **indications of interest** (also called letters of interest, or informally "bids") — non-binding term sheets stating a proposed price, form of consideration, and remaining diligence needs. This can run through one or several rounds before a winner is chosen.
5. **Negotiate and close.** The winning buyer runs final due diligence, both sides negotiate the **definitive agreement** covering price, employee retention, treatment of options, and other legal terms, financing gets arranged if needed, and the deal is announced.

The whole process typically runs several months to over a year depending on deal size, financing complexity, and regulatory review.

## Why deals fail

Knowing the failure modes shows you understand M&A isn't just spreadsheet math. The most common ones:

- **Integration difficulties.** Combining two supply chains, two sales forces, or two IT systems is operationally much harder than the deck implies.
- **Cultural mismatch.** A fast-moving, informal acquirer bolted onto a bureaucratic, hierarchical target (or vice versa) often loses key people within the first year.
- **Synergies that don't materialize.** The buyer projected $50 million of cost savings and only realized $15 million, or assumed customers would buy more and they didn't.
- **Overpaying.** The buyer wins a competitive process by bidding above what the business is actually worth, then has to write down goodwill once reality sets in.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect a two-part question: "Why would Company A want to buy Company B?" followed immediately by "Is that a good enough reason?" The strong answer names the specific financial lever (scale, geography, growth, cost synergies) rather than a generic "it makes them bigger," and then acknowledges the risk — that the rationale sounds cleaner in the pitch than it will be in integration. If you can name one way the specific deal could fail (cultural mismatch, synergy shortfall, overpayment), you've shown you're not just reciting a framework.
