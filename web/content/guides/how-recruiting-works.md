## The funnel, stage by stage

Investment banking recruiting is a filter, not a single event. You go through roughly five stages before an offer, and each one exists to cut the pool down before the bank spends real interviewer hours on you. Understanding the shape of the funnel — not just the content of each round — changes how you prepare.

### Stage 1: The screen

Every application gets run through an automated filter before a human looks at it. Recruiting systems screen on GPA (a 3.5 cutoff is common, though it varies by bank and school tier), school, major, and sometimes keyword matches against the job description. This stage has nothing to do with how good an interviewer you'll be — it's pure pattern-matching against a resume database.

If you're networking effectively, you can sometimes route around a weak screen: a referral or a banker flagging your name to HR moves your application into a different review pile. That's the single biggest reason networking matters before applications open, not after.

### Stage 2: HireVue and online assessments

If you clear the screen, most banks send a pre-recorded video interview (commonly built on the HireVue platform) or a set of online numerical/verbal reasoning tests, sometimes both. You answer 3-6 behavioral or motivational questions on camera with a short prep timer and a hard time limit per answer, and no do-overs.

This stage tests two things: whether you can deliver a structured answer under time pressure, and whether your on-camera presence is professional. It is graded by a mix of algorithmic scoring and human reviewers, so treat every question like a live interview — because functionally, it is one.

### Stage 3: First round

If you pass the video/test stage, you get a live first-round interview — usually 20-30 minutes, often with one or two junior bankers (analysts or associates), conducted by phone, video call, or on campus. First rounds lean technical: expect a few core accounting and valuation questions layered with a couple of fit questions like "walk me through your resume."

First rounds are usually pass/fail on competence. If you fumble basic technicals — for example, you can't explain why a $10 increase in depreciation lowers cash flow by less than $10 after tax — you're cut here regardless of how personable you are.

### Stage 4: Superday

Clearing first round gets you invited to a superday: a single day where you interview back-to-back with 5-10 bankers across seniority levels, often ending with a group dinner the night before or a debrief lunch after. Superdays are exhausting by design — the bank wants to see whether you stay sharp and consistent on interview 7 the way you were on interview 1.

Each interviewer scores you independently and the team debriefs afterward. A single bad interview doesn't automatically sink you if the rest of your rounds are strong, but a pattern of inconsistency (nailing technicals with analysts, going blank on fit questions with a VP) will.

### Stage 5: The decision

Superday conversion is the highest-odds stage in the whole process. If a bank invites you to superday, it already believes you're a plausible hire — a rough industry benchmark is that somewhere around 30-40% of superday candidates convert to an offer, compared to single-digit conversion from application to first round. The bar at superday isn't "prove you're brilliant," it's "don't give them a reason to say no."

## Worked example: sizing your odds

Say a bank receives 4,000 applications for its analyst class. A realistic funnel might look like this:

- 4,000 applications → 800 pass the resume/GPA screen (20%)
- 800 → 300 complete HireVue/testing and pass (37.5%)
- 300 → 90 get a first round (30%)
- 90 → 25 get invited to superday (28%)
- 25 → 9 receive an offer (36%)

Your unconditional odds from application to offer look brutal — 9/4,000, about 0.2%. But notice where the leverage is: the screen and HireVue stages eliminate the most people, and networking is what improves your odds of clearing the screen in the first place. Once you're at superday, your odds jump to better than 1 in 3. The lesson: don't measure your effort by "will I get an offer" at the application stage — measure it by "am I doing what it takes to clear the screen," because everything downstream of that is a much fairer fight.

## Rolling review versus fixed deadlines

Most banks that recruit on a resume-received basis review applications on a rolling basis, not all at once after the deadline closes. That means two candidates with identical resumes can get very different outcomes depending on when they applied — the bank may have already filled half its interview slots by the time a late applicant's resume gets read. This is the single most common reason strong candidates get passed over with no explanation: they weren't rejected on merit, they were late into a shrinking funnel.

The practical implication is blunt: apply the day applications open, not the week before the deadline. See [how the application calendar unfolds by class year](/guide/recruiting-calendar-by-class-year) for exact timing.

## Common mistakes

- **Treating the screen as a formality.** A 3.4 GPA with no referral gets filtered by software before any human reads your bullet points. If your GPA is borderline, networking your way to a referral matters more than another resume edit.
- **Under-preparing for HireVue because it "isn't a real interview."** It is graded, sometimes more strictly than a live round, because reviewers are comparing dozens of near-identical recorded answers back to back.
- **Peaking too early or too late across a superday.** Interviewers compare notes. Bring the same energy and technical sharpness to interview 8 as interview 1.
- **Waiting for the deadline to apply.** Rolling review means the funnel narrows every day applications are open. Early and mediocre usually beats polished and late.

## How interviewers probe this

Interviewers rarely ask directly about the recruiting process, but they'll notice whether you understand it. If you mention "I know this is a rolling process, so I applied the first day" in a fit answer about work ethic, it signals you've done your homework. Conversely, candidates who seem surprised by the format of a superday (multiple back-to-back interviews, a dinner the night before) read as underprepared — a small signal, but an avoidable one.
