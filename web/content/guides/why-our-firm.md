## The question they're actually asking

"Why our firm?" is a filter question, not an invitation to compliment them. Interviewers are listening for two things:

1. **Have you actually done your research?** Or are you about to tell them things they already know about themselves from their own website?
2. **Is your interest specific and genuine?** Or are you interviewing at 20 firms and saying the same thing to all of them?

A strong answer has to be specific enough that it could not have come from any other candidate at any other firm. If you swap out the firm name and it still works — rewrite it.

## What doesn't work

**Generic superlatives:** "You're consistently ranked as one of the top investment banks..." So are the nine other firms this student is interviewing with. Bankers hear this and immediately stop listening.

**Website language:** "Your client-first culture and commitment to excellence..." That's from the About page. Don't.

**Name-dropping without substance:** "I know you advised on some major M&A deals..." Which ones? What about them interested you? If you can't go deeper, don't start.

**Circular logic:** "I want to work at Goldman because it's Goldman." This is more common than you think and just as useless.

## The three layers of a strong answer

### Layer 1 — Something specific about the firm's work

Name a deal, a group, a strategic shift, or an area of strength that is genuinely distinctive. Be specific and brief:

> "I've been following Evercore's restructuring practice — the work on [a known restructuring engagement] showed how a lean advisory team can add real strategic value on the most complex side of M&A. I find that type of high-stakes advisory work more interesting than execution-focused banking, and Evercore's model seems built around that."

Even if you don't mention a specific deal, showing that you understand the firm's positioning within the market is meaningful:

> "Centerview's model — generalist senior bankers, no conflicts from underwriting, direct principal access — is different from the bulge bracket in a way that matters to me at this stage. I want to understand how deals get won and advised, not just executed."

### Layer 2 — A connection through people

If you've had a coffee chat with a current analyst or associate, this is the place to mention it. Not as namedropping, but as evidence that you've done the work:

> "I had a conversation with [first name], a second-year analyst in your consumer group. The way she described the team's approach to pitch work — that analysts actually drive the narrative, not just the formatting — was different from what I'd heard at other places."

This layer is optional but powerful. It shows you networked intentionally, not just clicked "Apply" on the career portal.

### Layer 3 — Why it connects to you

Close by making it personal — briefly. Why does this specific thing about this firm matter to you at this moment in your career?

> "I'm at the stage where I want to learn as much as possible about how advisory actually works, not just banking mechanics. The model here — small teams, high responsibility from day one — is the environment where I think I'd grow fastest."

That's honest, not sycophantic. It says "here's what I value and here's why you fit."

## Tailoring by firm type

**Bulge brackets (GS, MS, JPM):** Emphasize scale, global client access, and specific group strength. Know a recent landmark deal from their group. Understand the internal culture differences — GS is partnership-oriented, JPM is institutionalized, MS has strong equity capital markets DNA.

**Elite boutiques (Evercore, Centerview, Lazard, PJT):** Emphasize pure advisory, no underwriting conflicts, lean deal teams, senior access. Each EB has a distinct identity — know it. Don't call Centerview "similar to Evercore." They're different.

**Middle market (Jefferies, Houlihan Lokey, Harris Williams):** Emphasize the depth of sponsor coverage, the volume of processes run, and the hands-on execution experience. Don't treat MM as a backup — the best candidates at these firms chose them deliberately.

## The research that actually sets answers apart

Don't just read the firm's website. Do these three things:

1. **Read the firm's recent press releases** — deals announced, senior hires made, new capabilities built. The most recent 6 months are fair game.
2. **Look up league table rankings** in the relevant sector (Refinitiv/LSEG or Bloomberg league tables are publicly referenced in press). Know where the firm sits in areas you care about.
3. **Actually talk to someone at the firm.** One 30-minute coffee chat will teach you more about culture fit than an hour of website reading.

## Practice drill

Write your "why [firm]" answer for your top three targets. Then read each one and ask: could this have been said by any other candidate? Could the firm name be swapped out? If yes, go back and add one more specific layer. Practice saying it out loud — it should feel like you're talking, not reading a cover letter.
