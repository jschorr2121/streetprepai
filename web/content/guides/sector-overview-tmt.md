## What TMT actually covers

Technology, Media & Telecom is usually the largest coverage group by headcount at most bulge bracket and elite boutique banks, because it spans three very different kinds of businesses that got bundled together as advisory practice grew up around them. **Technology** covers software (SaaS, enterprise, consumer), hardware, semiconductors, and internet companies. **Media** covers entertainment, publishing, advertising, and increasingly streaming and gaming. **Telecom** covers wireless and wireline carriers, cable, and telecom infrastructure. Some banks split these into separate groups (Technology as its own franchise, Media/Telecom as another); others keep TMT unified. Either way, it's the group most exposed to high-growth, high-multiple, headline-generating deal activity — take-privates of software companies, platform consolidation in media, spectrum and infrastructure deals in telecom.

## What deals look like

TMT deal flow skews toward a few recurring shapes: strategic acquisitions of smaller software companies by larger platforms buying a product line or a customer base; private equity take-privates of profitable but slower-growing software businesses; IPOs and follow-on offerings for venture-backed companies reaching scale; and, in telecom specifically, large infrastructure and spectrum transactions that involve regulatory approval as a central deal risk. A distinguishing feature of TMT relative to more mature sectors is how much deal activity involves companies with little or no current profit — which directly shapes how they get valued.

## Why growth companies get valued differently

A mature industrial company gets valued primarily on EBITDA or EBIT multiples, because its earnings are stable and its growth is modest. A high-growth software company with negative or barely-positive EBITDA can't be meaningfully valued that way — a company burning cash to grow 60% a year might have an EBITDA multiple of negative 40x, which tells you nothing useful. Instead, bankers pivot to **EV/Revenue**, because revenue is the one line on the income statement that's both large and predictable even when profitability isn't yet real.

Within EV/Revenue, growth rate is the single biggest driver of where a company trades. Two companies with identical revenue can trade at wildly different multiples if one is growing 15% a year and the other is growing 50%.

**Worked example.** Say you're comping a software company, NimbusCo, against three public peers:

| Company | EV ($M) | Revenue ($M) | Growth rate | EV/Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Peer A | 4,200 | 600 | 18% | 7.0x |
| Peer B | 6,300 | 450 | 45% | 14.0x |
| Peer C | 2,850 | 380 | 22% | 7.5x |

NimbusCo has $500M of revenue growing at 40% a year. Peer B is the closest growth-rate match, so you'd weight its multiple more heavily than a simple average of the three (which would be roughly 9.5x and would understate NimbusCo's growth premium). Applying something closer to 12–13x (below Peer B, since NimbusCo is slightly smaller and less proven) gets you an implied EV of $6.0–6.5B. This is the core skill in TMT valuation: matching multiples to growth rather than averaging blindly across a comp set with very different growth profiles.

For companies with real profitability — mature software, large-cap hardware, telecom carriers — EV/EBITDA comes back into play alongside EV/Revenue, and you'd present both.

## Operational metrics that matter

Because financial statements lag the story for growth companies, TMT bankers lean on operational metrics that show up before revenue does:

- **Users or subscribers** — Monthly Active Users (MAU), Daily Active Users (DAU), paying subscribers.
- **ARPU (Average Revenue Per User)** — revenue divided by the user base; tells you how well a company monetizes its audience.
- **Net revenue retention** — for SaaS specifically, how much revenue a company keeps (or grows) from its existing customer base year over year, net of churn and upsells; above 100% means existing customers are spending more, not just replacing churned ones.
- **Churn rate** — the percentage of customers or revenue lost in a period; the mirror image of retention.
- **CAC and LTV** — Customer Acquisition Cost and Lifetime Value; used together to judge whether growth spend is actually efficient.

For pre-revenue or early-revenue companies (a common case in consumer internet and gaming), you'll sometimes see valuation anchored entirely to user counts — an EV/MAU multiple — because there's no revenue line stable enough to build a multiple around yet.

## Terminology to know cold

- **ARR / MRR** — Annual/Monthly Recurring Revenue, the standard revenue metric for subscription businesses.
- **TAM** — Total Addressable Market, used in growth-story pitches to argue a company has room to keep expanding.
- **Rule of 40** — a software heuristic: growth rate + profit margin should exceed 40%; a fast-growing but deeply unprofitable company can still pass if growth is high enough.
- **Platform vs. point solution** — whether a company sells a broad suite (platform) or a narrow, single-purpose product (point solution); platforms typically command higher multiples because they're stickier and cross-sell more.
- **Take-private** — a private equity firm buying a publicly traded company and delisting it; common in mature, cash-generative software names.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect a version of "how would you value a company with no earnings?" — the answer is EV/Revenue, paired with an explanation of why growth rate (not just size) drives the multiple. You may also get "why would a DCF be hard to build for an early-stage tech company?" (answer: unpredictable or negative near-term cash flows and a terminal value that dominates the whole valuation, making the output extremely sensitive to assumptions you can't defend with confidence). Be ready to name at least one or two current TMT deals or trends — interviewers in this group specifically expect you to follow the news, since it moves quickly.

## Common mistakes

- **Averaging comp multiples without adjusting for growth.** A flat average across companies growing at 15% and 50% produces a meaningless number; always sort or weight by growth rate first.
- **Defaulting to EV/EBITDA for every TMT company.** It's the wrong tool for a business that's intentionally unprofitable while it scales.
- **Confusing ARR with GAAP revenue.** ARR is a run-rate metric, not a recognized-revenue line item — the two can diverge meaningfully for a company with lumpy contract timing.
