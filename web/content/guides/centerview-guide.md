## The Centerview identity

Centerview Partners is one of the most selective investment banks in the world — and one of the least discussed outside of banking circles. Founded in 2006 by Blair Effron and Robert Pruzan (both former UBS investment bankers), Centerview has built a reputation for advising on the most consequential M&A transactions globally, often on the buy side of the largest strategic deals.

The firm operates with an unusual degree of secrecy. It doesn't have a public-facing deals page with all of its advisory work. Its partners are among the most respected senior bankers in the industry. And it operates with a model that most banks claim to have but few actually practice: every senior partner is directly involved in every client relationship.

Centerview hires very few analysts — making it one of the most competitive seats in banking, more selective on a per-opening basis than most bulge brackets.

## The model: generalist, senior-led, conflict-free

Centerview's model has three defining features:

**Generalist.** Analysts at Centerview are not placed into a TMT group or a Healthcare group — they work across industries, on whatever deal the firm is engaged on. This produces unusual breadth of exposure but also demands that you be genuinely curious across sectors.

**Senior-led.** Every Centerview engagement is led by a Partner with a direct client relationship. Analysts and associates don't manage client relationships independently — they support Partners who are deeply involved in day-to-day deal management. The pitch is that you'll see how the very best senior bankers think and advise, at close range.

**Conflict-free pure advisory.** Like other elite boutiques, Centerview has no balance sheet, no underwriting business, and no trading operations that would create conflicts with its advisory work. Clients hire Centerview precisely for this reason — they want advice untainted by the bank's own financial interests.

## What Centerview looks for

**Exceptional intellectual caliber.** Centerview hires a very small number of people and places enormous weight on raw analytical ability, intellectual curiosity, and the capacity to handle complex, ambiguous strategic problems. Technical excellence is expected but not sufficient.

**Maturity and presence.** Because partners are in the room with clients on every engagement, the people supporting those Partners need to carry themselves well. Analysts are expected to be polished, confident, and capable of operating in high-stakes environments earlier than at most firms.

**Genuine interest in the Centerview model.** The generalist model is not for everyone. Centerview interviewers are sensitive to candidates who would prefer a defined sector focus. If you're genuinely energized by working across industries and want direct exposure to how the most important strategic decisions get made, say that — and mean it.

**Personal connection.** Centerview's hiring process is intensely relational. Partners play a meaningful role in who gets offers. This makes pre-interview networking not just helpful but close to necessary. If you can get a call with a Centerview banker before your interview, prioritize it.

## Interview process

Centerview's process is known to be among the most thorough in the industry. Expect:

1. **First-round** — Deep technical and behavioral. Centerview technicals can go well beyond first-round depth at other firms — think full LBO mechanics, detailed DCF and WACC derivation, and complex accounting scenarios.
2. **Partner-level rounds** — Multiple rounds with Partners, not just junior bankers. These conversations are often more strategic and less formulaic — they want to see how you think, not just whether you've memorized the answers.

The behavioral questions at Centerview tend to probe for intellectual initiative: "Tell me about a time you figured something out on your own." "What's the most complex problem you've solved analytically?" "What do you read and why?"

## Technical expectations

Centerview's technical bar is very high. Expect everything in the standard curriculum (see the guides on DCF, three statements, LBO basics, WACC) to be tested at a deep level. Additionally:

- Be ready to defend every assumption in your DCF, not just state the framework
- Know the nuances of precedent transactions vs. trading comps — when each is more or less reliable and why
- Know LBO returns math: MOIC, IRR, what drives each, how to do quick paper math
- Be ready for "What would you change about this analysis?" — a question designed to see how you critique your own thinking

## What "why Centerview" sounds like when it works

Candidates who land at Centerview typically have answers that reflect genuine understanding of the model:

> "I've talked with three people at Centerview and what strikes me is how consistent they are about one thing: Partners are actually in the work. Not signing off on a junior team's output — in the model, in the negotiation, in the room. I want to learn how the best strategic advisors actually think, and I think being that close to that level of decision-making early on is rare. The generalist model appeals to me because I'm genuinely curious about multiple industries, and I think forcing yourself to get up to speed on a new sector for every deal makes you a better analyst faster."

That answer is honest, specific, and shows independent thought — exactly what Centerview wants to see.

## Practice this

Centerview rarely publicizes its deals directly. Follow financial news (Bloomberg, Reuters, Wall Street Journal) for M&A transactions where Centerview is named as an advisor. When you find one, dig into the strategic rationale: why did the company choose pure advisory, why Centerview specifically, and what was the deal's complexity? Being able to discuss a Centerview-advised deal shows that you've done research beyond the firm's (minimal) website.
