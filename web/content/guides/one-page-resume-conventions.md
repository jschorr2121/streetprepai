## Why format is not a detail

Before a recruiter reads a single bullet, they've already made a snap judgment based on layout. IB resumes follow a narrow, well-known template, and deviating from it — even with good content underneath — reads as either inexperience or a lack of attention to detail. Both are disqualifying in a business built on precision.

Treat format as a constraint you work within, not a place to express personality. The personality goes in your story and your bullets. The format should be almost invisible.

## The one-page rule

Your resume is one page. Not one page plus a little overflow, not one page at 9-point font with 0.3-inch margins. One page, readable, with room to breathe.

This holds regardless of how much you've done. A junior with three internships and five leadership roles still cuts down to one page — you select the highest-signal items and drop the rest. A recruiter or associate reviewing hundreds of applications during a single recruiting cycle spends roughly 20-30 seconds on a first pass. A second page doesn't get read; it gets you flagged as someone who can't prioritize.

The only exception is a CV used for academic or research-heavy full-time roles at the graduate level, and even then, check the specific firm's expectations before assuming a second page is acceptable.

### Working the math backward

One page isn't a vague target — you can budget it like a model. A standard resume page with 0.6-inch margins, 10.5-point body text, and single spacing gives you roughly 46-50 usable lines before it feels cramped.

Here's how that budget typically breaks down for a sophomore or junior:

- Header (name, contact, school): 2 lines
- Education section (school, dates, GPA, relevant coursework): 5-6 lines
- Experience section, 2 entries at 3-4 bullets each: 8 bullets, roughly 16 lines including entry headers
- Leadership/activities, 2 entries at 2 bullets each: 4 bullets, roughly 10 lines
- Skills & interests: 3-4 lines

Total: roughly 36-40 lines, leaving a margin of error for line wrapping. Run this math on your own draft before you start agonizing over font size — if you're at 55 lines, the fix is cutting a bullet or an entire entry, not shrinking to 9-point font and 0.4-inch margins, which reads as visibly non-standard the moment someone opens the file.

## Section order

Use this order unless you have a specific reason not to:

1. **Header** — name, phone, email, city (no photo, no full mailing address)
2. **Education** — most recent/relevant degree first, GPA, expected graduation date, relevant coursework or honors if space allows
3. **Experience** — reverse chronological, most relevant or most recent first
4. **Leadership & activities** — clubs, competitions, varsity athletics, volunteer work
5. **Skills & interests** — languages, technical tools (Excel, Bloomberg, Python), and 2-3 genuine interests

As a sophomore with limited work history, education and activities carry more relative weight — don't pad experience with unrelated part-time jobs just to fill space; a well-described campus leadership role often signals more than a generic retail job. As a junior with one or two relevant internships, experience should be your strongest section, and it's fine to trim activities down to the two or three most relevant.

## ATS-safe formatting

Most banks route applications through an applicant tracking system before a human sees them. ATS software parses text — it does not reliably parse design.

Follow these rules so your resume survives the parse:

- **No tables, text boxes, or multi-column layouts.** ATS parsers read left to right, top to bottom; a two-column layout can scramble your work history into nonsense.
- **No graphics, icons, or a photo.** Beyond breaking parsers, headshots and icon sets are a US recruiting norm violation and read as a template resume.
- **No headers or footers for contact info.** Some parsers skip headers/footers entirely, which means your name and contact info silently disappear from the extracted text.
- **Standard section headings.** "Experience," "Education," "Leadership & Activities" — not "My Journey" or "What I've Built."
- **Simple bullet characters.** A plain round or square bullet. Skip arrows, checkmarks, or decorative glyphs.
- **Save and submit as a PDF unless the application explicitly asks for a Word doc.** PDF preserves formatting across devices; a .docx can shift fonts and break line wraps on someone else's machine.

## Fonts and margins

Pick one font and use it everywhere — mixing fonts across sections is one of the fastest tells that a resume was assembled from templates. Reasonable choices: Garamond, Georgia, Calibri, or Times New Roman. Avoid anything with personality — no Comic Sans obviously, but also skip trendy sans-serifs that look more like a design portfolio than a finance document.

Size and spacing:

- Body text: 10-11 point
- Name/header: 14-16 point, bold
- Section headings: 11-12 point, bold, possibly small caps
- Margins: 0.5-0.75 inch on all sides — tighter than a default Word document, but not so tight the page looks stuffed

Keep spacing consistent. If one bullet has 2 points of space after it and the next has 6, it's visible even to someone skimming quickly, and it signals the document was edited carelessly across multiple sessions.

## How interviewers and recruiters probe this

Format issues rarely come up as interview questions — they cause your resume to get screened out before you're ever in the room. But a few formatting-adjacent things do surface once you're in front of a person:

- **"Walk me through your resume."** If your section order and emphasis don't match the narrative you tell out loud, the mismatch is noticeable. Your format should set up the story you're about to tell — see [Walk me through your resume](/guide/walk-me-through-your-resume) for how the two connect.
- **Inconsistent dates or degrees.** If your header says "Expected May 2027" but your education section says "Class of 2026," that's a five-second red flag an interviewer will ask about, and it undermines trust in everything else on the page.
- **A resume that looks templated.** Recruiters who read hundreds of these per cycle recognize free templates on sight — colored sidebars, skill bars, icon rows — and they read it as a candidate who didn't do the extra diligence to learn the norms of the industry they're recruiting into.

## Common mistakes

- **Shrinking font and margins to force a fit.** This is the most common failure mode and the most visible one. Cut content instead — see [Red flags & fixes](/guide/resume-red-flags) for how to decide what to cut.
- **Using a resume template downloaded from a general job-search site.** These are built for corporate/generalist hiring, not IB, and the visual style will stand out for the wrong reasons.
- **Inconsistent bullet punctuation.** Either every bullet ends with a period or none do — pick one and audit the whole page.
- **Different date formats across sections.** "Jun 2025 - Aug 2025" in one place and "6/2025 - 8/2025" in another. Standardize on one format and use it everywhere, including on your cover letter.
- **Justifying text.** Left-align only. Justified text creates uneven letter spacing that looks unprofessional at small font sizes.

Before you submit anything, print the resume (or view it at 100% zoom, actual size) and look at it as a whole page for five seconds before reading a word. If your eye doesn't land cleanly on your name, your most recent role, and your school, tighten the hierarchy before you send it.
