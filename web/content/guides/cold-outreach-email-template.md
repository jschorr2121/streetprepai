## The core reality of cold outreach

Most cold outreach emails don't get a response. Not because bankers are rude, but because the emails are too long, too generic, or ask for too much upfront. You can't control whether someone responds, but you can dramatically increase the odds by writing an email that respects their time, shows genuine research, and makes it easy to say yes.

A well-written cold email is not about impressing them. It's about making it effortless for a busy person to spend 20–30 minutes on the phone with you.

## The anatomy of an effective email

### Subject line

Short, specific, and clear. The goal is one glance to understand why you're writing and feel no friction in opening it.

Strong: **"[School] Student — Quick Question About [Firm] Coverage Groups"**
Strong: **"[Your Name] — [Mutual Connection] Suggested I Reach Out"**
Weak: "Informational Interview Request"
Weak: "Interested in Investment Banking Opportunity"

The subject line that performs best among undergrad recruiters: your school + a clear, low-pressure framing. Mentioning a mutual connection, if you have one, nearly doubles response rate.

### Opening — who you are in one sentence

Don't lead with your GPA or your list of accomplishments. Lead with a clear, specific identification:

> "I'm a junior at Columbia studying economics and finance, currently recruiting for IB summer analyst roles."

One sentence. They now know exactly who you are and why you might be writing.

### The specific ask — why this person, why now

This is the most important sentence. You need to explain why you're reaching out to this specific person, not just "a banker." Generic language here ("I'd love to learn about your experience in investment banking") is the #1 reason emails don't get responses.

Good options:
- You found their name via an alumni database and you're specifically interested in the group they work in
- You read an article or saw their name in a deal press release
- You have a genuine question about something specific to their work

> "I came across your name in our alumni database and saw that you work in JPMorgan's Healthcare group — I've been following the biotech M&A market closely and would love to hear how you think about the sector."

That's specific. It tells them you've done research and have a real reason for reaching out to them specifically.

### The actual ask — make it small

The single biggest mistake in cold outreach is asking for a job or an internship. Even asking for a "30-minute informational interview" can feel like too much before any relationship exists.

The right ask: a 15–20 minute phone call, framed as a conversation where you're trying to learn, not impress.

> "Would you have 15–20 minutes for a quick call in the next few weeks? I'm not looking for anything specific — I'd just find your perspective on recruiting and the Healthcare group genuinely valuable."

"I'm not looking for anything specific" is a small but powerful line. It removes the implicit pressure of "this person wants something from me."

### Close — brief and easy

Short, respectful close. Offer your resume as optional context (not a demand to review it).

> "Happy to work around your schedule completely. I've attached my resume in case it's helpful context. Thank you for any time you're able to give."

## The full template

---

**Subject:** Columbia Junior — Quick Question About JPMorgan Healthcare Group

Hi [Name],

I'm a junior at Columbia studying economics and finance, currently recruiting for IB summer analyst roles.

I came across your profile through our alumni network and saw that you're in JPMorgan's Healthcare coverage group. I've been closely following biotech M&A activity and would love to hear your perspective on the space — specifically how deal flow has evolved and what a summer analyst would realistically work on in your group.

Would you have 15–20 minutes for a quick call over the next few weeks? I'm happy to work around your schedule completely. I've attached my resume in case it's useful context.

Thank you so much — I really appreciate any time you're able to give.

Best,
[Your Name]
[School, Year, Major]
[Phone]
[LinkedIn URL]

---

## What to customize

Every email should be personalized to the individual. At minimum:
- Their name and their specific group/firm
- One specific hook (a deal, an article, a shared connection, something you found in their background)
- Your specific question or area of interest

If you're sending 50 identical emails, you're doing it wrong. Thirty thoughtful, personalized emails will outperform 200 generic ones.

## Follow-up

If you haven't heard back in 7–10 business days, one gentle follow-up is appropriate:

> "Hi [Name] — just wanted to follow up on my note from [date]. Completely understand if you're swamped. Happy to revisit at a less busy time if that's easier. Thank you."

Do not send a third email. Two is the limit.

## Practice this

Write your cold outreach template today — the general structure with blanks where the specific hook goes. Then identify five people at your target firms using your school's alumni database or LinkedIn. Research each one for 10 minutes: What group are they in? What deals has their firm done recently? What specific question can you ask that connects your background to their work? Write five individual emails. Send them. Measure what gets a response and learn from the pattern.
