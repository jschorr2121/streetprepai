## The JPMorgan identity

JPMorgan is the largest bank in the United States by assets, and its investment banking division is consistently one of the top global franchises across M&A, DCM, and ECM. If Goldman and Morgan Stanley are the two firms most associated with elite boutique-level prestige at scale, JPMorgan is the firm most associated with institutional breadth — the widest client coverage, the deepest balance sheet, and the most global footprint.

The culture at JPMorgan is institutional, process-oriented, and team-driven. It's less personality-driven than some boutiques and less intensely hierarchical than Goldman in certain respects. People who thrive at JPMorgan tend to value the scale of the platform, the breadth of client relationships, and the opportunity to work across products within a single institution.

Understanding Jamie Dimon's stewardship of the firm — the focus on client service, balance sheet strength, and long-term institutional thinking — is a useful cultural frame. JPMorgan emphasizes client relationships and long-term advisory trust in a way that shapes how its bankers think about their role.

## Group structure

JPMorgan recruits Summer Analysts into **Investment Banking** across a broad set of coverage and product groups:

**Coverage:** TMT, Healthcare, Consumer Retail, Industrials, Energy, FIG, Real Estate, Media.

**Products:** M&A, Leveraged Finance, ECM, DCM.

JPMorgan's scale means that some of the largest, most complex transactions — particularly those requiring balance sheet commitment alongside advisory (bridge loans, commitment letters) — tend to run through JPMorgan. Understanding how advisory and financing interact at a bank like JPMorgan (versus a pure advisory boutique) is a useful differentiator in your "why JPMorgan" answer.

## What JPMorgan looks for

**Work ethic and execution capability.** JPMorgan runs high-volume deal flow across a broad client base. The bank values analysts who can execute efficiently under pressure, maintain quality across multiple live deals, and operate as reliable team members.

**Client orientation.** JPMorgan's culture emphasizes long-term client relationships. Candidates who show genuine curiosity about industries and clients — not just financial modeling — tend to land well.

**Versatility.** Given the breadth of the platform, JPMorgan appreciates analysts who are comfortable across both advisory and financing work. Understanding the difference between an M&A mandate and a high-yield debt offering — and how both might live on the same deal — is a useful level of sophistication.

## Interview process

JPMorgan recruits through a fairly structured process:

1. **Online application and HireVue** — Standard behavioral and technical screening questions in video format.
2. **First-round interview** — Typically with junior bankers (analysts, associates). Technical and behavioral.
3. **Superday** — Multiple rounds, usually four to six. Behavioral questions are thorough at JPMorgan — expect detailed situational and competency questions alongside technicals.

JPMorgan's superday can include more senior interviewers earlier than some banks. Being prepared to speak comfortably to MDs — maintaining confidence while being respectful of the dynamic — matters.

## Technical expectations

The standard bulge bracket technical curriculum applies: three statements, DCF, WACC, comps, precedents, M&A accretion/dilution. JPMorgan may probe slightly harder on:

**Capital markets mechanics.** Given the bank's DCM and ECM strength, expect possible questions on debt structures (term loans, revolving credit, high-yield bonds, investment-grade bonds), covenant mechanics, and when a company would issue equity vs. debt.

**Leveraged finance.** "What makes a good LBO candidate?" "How do you think about leverage ratios for a leveraged buyout?" JPMorgan's leveraged finance group is among the largest in the world — having a view on this is valuable.

**Credit analysis basics.** "How would you evaluate a company's ability to service debt?" Know the basics: interest coverage ratio, debt/EBITDA, free cash flow to debt service.

## Balance sheet banking — what it means

One thing that distinguishes JPMorgan from elite boutiques: JPMorgan can commit its own capital. In large leveraged buyouts or M&A transactions requiring bridge financing, JPMorgan can underwrite the debt needed to close a deal — boutiques can't. This creates a different dynamic in client relationships and deal sourcing. Understanding this distinction, and being able to articulate why it matters to some clients, elevates a "why JPMorgan" answer from generic to specific.

## What "why JPMorgan" sounds like when it works

- "The scale of the platform means that on a complex leveraged deal, advisory and financing sit in the same building — I find the interplay between those two things genuinely interesting, and JPMorgan is one of the few places where an analyst gets visibility into both."
- Naming a specific large-cap M&A deal JPMorgan advised on in the last year and why the deal's structure (bridge financing, regulatory complexity, global coordination) was interesting
- Something learned from a JPMorgan banker — how deal teams work across groups, the culture within a specific group

## Practice this

Look up JPMorgan's league table position in M&A and DCM for the most recent year (public league tables are reported by Bloomberg and Refinitiv). Then find one JPMorgan-advised deal where the firm also provided financing — these are common in leveraged buyouts. Try to understand what role the bank played on both the advisory and financing sides. That dual role is what makes JPMorgan a different beast than a boutique, and being able to articulate it is a genuine differentiator.
