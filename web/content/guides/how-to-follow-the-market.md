## Why market awareness matters in IB recruiting

In almost every IB interview — first round, superday, coffee chat — someone will ask you a market or current events question. Sometimes it's formal: "What's your view on M&A activity in the current rate environment?" Sometimes it's casual: "Have you been following what's happening with [recent deal]?" Either way, you need to have a real answer.

The difference between a prepared and unprepared candidate isn't the width of your reading — it's whether you've moved from consuming information to forming views. Anyone can say "M&A volumes are down." The candidate who stands out says "M&A volumes are compressed partly because of rate-driven financing costs — but I think the more interesting constraint is valuation gaps: sellers with 2021-era price expectations and buyers with 2024-era cost of capital. That gap takes time to close, and I expect it to start narrowing as we move further from the rate shock."

That's a view. Build those.

## What to read daily (realistic version)

You don't need to read six financial publications front to back every morning. You need a daily routine you'll actually maintain. Here's a realistic stack:

**Every morning (15–20 minutes):**

**The Wall Street Journal** — Read the front page and the Markets section. Know the top 2–3 stories of the day. The WSJ is the closest thing to required reading for a banking candidate.

**Bloomberg Markets** or **Reuters** — Either works. Pick one. Skim the markets wrap for overnight moves, key data releases, and major deal announcements.

**A financial newsletter of your choice** — Options that work: Axios Pro Rata (M&A deals and private markets), The Information (tech-focused), Dealbook (NYT's deal-focused daily). Pick one that matches your sector interest.

**Weekly (30–45 minutes, weekend):**

**The Economist** — Big-picture macro and geopolitics, beautifully written. The Buttonwood column (finance) and Briefing sections are especially valuable.

**Barron's** — Market-focused, good for equity market context and sector commentary.

**Financial Times** — Particularly good for European and cross-border deals.

## What to actually read within each source

Don't read everything. Read selectively:

- **Deal announcements and M&A news** — These are your interview currency. For every deal you see, spend 3 minutes asking: who bought whom, what was the strategic rationale, what did they pay (approximate multiple), and does it make sense?
- **Macro and rates** — Know the direction of interest rates, the Fed's current posture, and the general state of credit markets. You don't need to predict the next rate move — you need to understand the transmission to deal activity and valuations.
- **Sector moves** — Follow one or two sectors closely (your target group, or a sector you're genuinely interested in). Track the leading companies, recent earnings themes, and any major deals or regulatory developments.
- **Earnings season** — During earnings season (quarterly, following fiscal quarters), major companies report. Know how your sector's companies have been doing versus expectations. "Microsoft beat on cloud revenue but gave softer guidance on enterprise spend" is the kind of context that shows real engagement.

## Building genuine views

Passively reading the news is not the same as preparing for IB interviews. You need to practice forming and defending opinions. Some frameworks:

**The "so what" discipline.** After every article you read, ask: so what does this mean for valuations, deal activity, or a sector I'm following? Force yourself to derive an implication, not just absorb a fact.

**Track one deal from announcement to close.** When a major M&A deal is announced, follow it through: press release, analyst reactions, regulatory developments, closing conditions, any surprises. By the time you're done, you'll know that deal well enough to discuss it as a case study.

**Have three views ready at all times.** At any given time, prepare to defend a view on: (1) one macro or market topic ("where are credit spreads going and what does that mean for LBO activity"), (2) one sector ("why pharma M&A is accelerating"), and (3) one specific company or deal ("why I think Broadcom's VMware integration will play out better than the bears expect"). These three views should be your answer when someone says "what's on your mind in markets right now?"

## Tracking M&A specifically

M&A deal flow is directly relevant to IB. Read M&A news with these questions in mind:

- **Who is the buyer and why?** Strategic acquirer or financial sponsor? What was the strategic rationale — scale, capability, geography, product gap?
- **How much did they pay?** If disclosed, what's the approximate EV/EBITDA or EV/Revenue multiple? Is that high or low relative to how similar companies trade publicly?
- **How is it financed?** All cash, stock, or mixed? If cash, is there leverage? A highly leveraged deal signals the financing environment was conducive.
- **Who advised on it?** Note which banks are active in your target sector.

Reading M&A news this way — not just skimming the headline — will build your intuition for deal pricing and strategic logic faster than almost anything else.

## What not to do

**Don't memorize interest rate levels for the sake of sounding informed.** Interviewers will ask follow-up questions, and if you don't understand why rates moved or what the implications are, the number you memorized will hurt more than help.

**Don't adopt someone else's views without understanding them.** Interviewers will probe. If you say "I think rates will fall because of the inverted yield curve" and can't explain what an inverted curve means or why it signals a rate cut, you've created a problem.

**Don't only follow your target sector.** Markets are interconnected. A student who understands how rising rates affect both LBO activity and equity multiples is more sophisticated than one who can only speak to their preferred sector.

## Building the habit

Consistency beats intensity. Fifteen minutes of focused reading every morning will compound faster than a three-hour weekend binge followed by nothing for two weeks. Set a specific time — morning coffee, commute, lunch — and protect it.

## Practice this

Write down three market views you could defend today — one macro, one sector, one company or deal. For each one, write two or three sentences of supporting logic. Then practice saying each one out loud, and add a counterargument: "The bear case would be..." Being able to hold your own view while acknowledging the other side is the hallmark of a genuinely market-aware candidate.
