## The core idea

A leveraged buyout is the simplest thing in finance that people make sound complicated: a private equity firm buys a company using mostly borrowed money, operates it for a few years, then sells it — ideally for more than they paid. The debt sits on the acquired company's balance sheet, and the company's own cash flows pay it down. The equity sponsor puts in a minority of the purchase price and collects the upside.

The word "leveraged" is doing all the work here. Leverage is what makes the returns look attractive — and what makes a bad deal go very wrong.

## The LBO structure

Imagine a private equity firm (the sponsor) buys a company for $1,000. They finance it roughly 60–70% with debt:

- **Debt:** $650 (bank loans, high-yield bonds, or a mix)
- **Equity:** $350 (the sponsor's fund capital, the "equity check")

The debt sits on the acquired company's balance sheet. The company's EBITDA and free cash flows service the interest payments and pay down principal over time.

When the firm exits — usually 4–7 years later — they sell the company. If the company is worth $1,600 at exit:

- Pay off remaining debt (say $450 after principal paydown)
- The equity holders receive: $1,600 − $450 = **$1,150**
- Equity invested was $350, so equity value roughly **3.3x**

That's the mechanics of how $350 of equity turns into $1,150 — by using borrowed money to fund the majority of the purchase and letting the business pay it off.

## The three sources of return

Private equity firms generate returns from three places, and knowing all three shows real sophistication:

### 1. EBITDA growth

If the company's earnings grow from $100 to $140 over the holding period, that growth feeds through to a higher exit value. PE firms often work to accelerate this through operational improvements, add-on acquisitions, or geographic expansion.

### 2. Multiple expansion

If the firm buys at 8x EBITDA and sells at 10x EBITDA, they've captured two turns of multiple expansion — even if earnings didn't grow. This is partly a bet on market conditions (a higher-multiple exit environment) and partly strategic (improving the business's quality to command a higher multiple).

### 3. Debt paydown (leverage)

As the company generates free cash flow and pays down debt, the equity value grows — the total enterprise value (debt + equity) stays roughly constant, but the debt shrinks, so the equity's slice grows. This is the mechanical source of return unique to leveraged structures.

> Returns = f(Entry Price, EBITDA Growth, Exit Multiple, Leverage, Debt Paydown)

## The returns math — IRR and MOIC

PE firms measure returns two ways:

**MOIC (Multiple of Invested Capital):** How many times did we get back what we put in?

> MOIC = Total Equity Proceeds ÷ Equity Invested

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):** What's the annualized return, accounting for time?

A 3.0x MOIC over 5 years is roughly a 25% IRR. Over 7 years, the same MOIC is roughly a 17% IRR. Time kills returns — which is why sponsors care intensely about exit timing.

Target returns for a PE fund: **20–25%+ IRR**, **2.5–3.0x+ MOIC**. A deal that returns 2.0x in 3 years (roughly 26% IRR) is better than one that returns 2.5x in 7 years (roughly 14% IRR).

## What makes a good LBO candidate

Not every business is a good LBO target. The ideal candidate has:

- **Stable, predictable cash flows** — the company needs to service debt. Cyclical, lumpy, or capital-intensive businesses can struggle when times get tough.
- **Pricing power / defensible market position** — you need to sustain margins while carrying more debt.
- **Low existing leverage** — if the company is already heavily indebted, there's no room to add acquisition financing.
- **Clear operational improvement opportunities** — what will the PE firm actually do to make this business more valuable?
- **A realistic exit path** — strategic buyers, IPO, or another sponsor. If nobody wants to buy it in 5 years, IRR collapses.

Classic LBO sectors: software (recurring revenue), healthcare services, consumer brands with loyal customers, industrials with long-term contracts.

## Why leverage amplifies both upside and downside

This is the most important concept to understand — and the one that makes LBOs interesting and dangerous:

If you buy a $1,000 company with $650 of debt and $350 of equity:
- If it grows to $1,400, your equity is worth $750 — a **2.1x** on your equity (vs. 1.4x if you had bought it with 100% equity).
- If it falls to $700, your equity is worth $50 — an **85% loss** on equity (vs. only a 30% loss on a 100% equity purchase).

Leverage magnifies percentage changes in enterprise value into much larger percentage changes in equity value. This is why lenders care deeply about the company's ability to service debt in a downside scenario — and why sponsors model stress cases.

## How to explain LBOs in an interview

A clean 90-second answer:

> "An LBO is when a financial sponsor acquires a company, typically using 60–70% debt financing. The debt sits on the acquired company's balance sheet and is paid down with its own cash flows. The sponsor aims to exit the investment 4–7 years later at a profit, generating returns from three sources: EBITDA growth, multiple expansion, and debt paydown. The leverage amplifies returns when things go well and amplifies losses when they don't, which is why the ideal LBO target has stable, predictable cash flows and a clear exit path."

## Practice this

Pick a company you know well — a retailer, a software company, a services business. Ask yourself: would this make a good LBO target? Work through the checklist: stable FCF? Predictable revenue? Not already over-leveraged? Operational improvement opportunities? Viable exit? Then think about what the company trades at today (EV/EBITDA) and whether that entry price would leave enough room for the sponsor to generate a 20%+ IRR. The instinct for "does this work as an LBO?" is something you build by doing this exercise repeatedly.
