## Why this is the step people botch

Most students treat the [coffee chat](/guide/coffee-chat-framework) as the finish line. It isn't. A 20-minute call rarely gets you a referral or an interview by itself — what gets you there is what you do in the six weeks after. Bankers meet dozens of students every recruiting season. The ones who get remembered, referred, and eventually hired are the ones who follow up with discipline and don't disappear the moment the call ends.

Referrals are not a favor you cash in after one conversation. They're something a banker extends once they trust that putting their name next to yours won't embarrass them. Your job in the follow-up phase is to build that trust, not to extract a favor as fast as possible.

## The 24-hour thank-you

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours of any call — same day is better. Keep it short: three to five sentences.

Structure:

1. Thank them specifically for their time and for one thing they said that was genuinely useful (not generic flattery — reference an actual detail from the conversation).
2. Mention one concrete thing you're doing as a result of the call (a resource they suggested, a group you're now looking into).
3. Leave the door open without asking for anything yet.

Example:

> Hi Mark, thank you for taking the time to talk yesterday — the way you described how your group's deal flow shifted after the two carve-outs last year was really helpful for understanding what the day-to-day actually looks like. I'm going to read up on the sector coverage split you mentioned before our applications open. I'll keep you posted on how recruiting goes, and thanks again for the insight.

Notice there's no ask in that email. Asking for a referral in the same message as the thank-you is the single most common mistake — it recasts the entire conversation as transactional in the banker's mind, retroactively.

## The cadence: a six-week map

Think of the relationship in phases, not a single touchpoint.

- **Day 0–1**: Thank-you email.
- **Week 2–3**: A light, no-ask touchpoint — share a relevant news article, a follow-up question you thought of, or an update on something you discussed ("I ended up reaching out to the group you mentioned and got a call scheduled — thanks for the nudge").
- **Week 4–6 (as applications open)**: This is when it's appropriate to ask for a referral, if the relationship has warmed enough. Reference the earlier conversation explicitly.
- **Ongoing, every 4–8 weeks**: A genuine, low-effort update — not a ping for its own sake. Only reach out when you have something real to say.

If you only ever contact someone twice — once to ask for the chat, once to ask for the referral — you're a stranger asking a stranger for something. The middle touchpoints are what convert you from "a name in my inbox" to "a person I'd vouch for."

## How referrals actually happen

Referrals rarely happen because you formally requested one. More often, one of two things happens:

1. **The banker offers.** If the conversation went well and they see genuine fit, they'll say something like "let me know when you apply, I can flag your resume." When this happens, say thank you, confirm the firm's application deadline, and follow up right when you submit — don't let the offer go stale.
2. **You ask, once, at the right moment.** This is appropriate once you've had at least one substantive conversation and the application window is open or about to open. Make it easy to say yes: be specific about the role, give them everything they need in one email (resume attached, exact position/req if you have it), and make clear you understand a referral is a favor, not an entitlement.

A workable ask, sent 4–6 weeks after the original chat:

> Hi Mark, hope the deal you mentioned closed smoothly. Applications for the summer analyst program just opened, and after our conversation I'm even more sure your group is where I'd want to be. Would you be open to referring me? Happy to send anything that would help — resume attached. No worries at all if the timing isn't right.

That last line matters. It gives them a graceful out, which paradoxically makes people more likely to say yes — you've signaled you understand the ask has a cost to them.

## Asking without being transactional

The tell of a transactional networker is that every message has an ask attached. Avoid this by following a simple rule: **for every ask, there should be at least two non-ask touchpoints first.** Concretely:

- Don't ask for a referral in the thank-you email.
- Don't ask for a second call within a week of the first.
- Don't only reach out when you need something (an interview date moved, a status update, a second referral).

If you find yourself only ever emailing someone when you want something from them, that's the pattern to break. A genuinely warm relationship has more give than take in the early stages — you're not going to have much to give a first-year analyst, so the "give" is mostly attention: remembering details, following up on things they mentioned, treating them like a person rather than a lead.

## A worked example: tracking cadence across a cycle

Say you start networking in early August for a process where applications open October 1 and first rounds run late October. Here's what a well-paced cadence looks like on the calendar:

- **Aug 12**: Coffee chat with a 2nd-year analyst.
- **Aug 13**: Thank-you email (1 day later).
- **Aug 28**: Light touchpoint — you noticed the firm advised on a deal that closed; you send a one-line congratulations (16 days later).
- **Oct 1**: Applications open. You email to say you've submitted and ask if they'd be willing to refer you (34 days after the chat, 2 touchpoints deep already).
- **Oct 3**: They confirm the referral is in.
- **Oct 15**: You get a first-round invite. You send a short note letting them know and thanking them again (12 days after the referral).

Total elapsed time from first chat to first-round invite: 64 days, five total touchpoints, only one of which was an ask. That ratio — one ask against several genuine, low-effort updates — is what separates a relationship from a transaction.

## Keeping it warm across months, not weeks

For contacts you meet early (freshman or sophomore year, well before recruiting), the cadence stretches out but the logic is the same. A check-in every couple of months is enough — a relevant article, a congratulations on a promotion you saw on LinkedIn, an update on your own trajectory ("just declared my major, thanks for the advice on coursework"). The goal isn't frequency, it's that when recruiting season actually arrives, you're not a cold reintroduction — you're someone they've heard from consistently and remember positively.

## Common mistakes

- **Asking too early.** Requesting a referral in the first follow-up email, before any trust has been built, is the fastest way to get politely ignored.
- **Going silent after the ask.** If you get a referral, close the loop — tell them what happened (interview scheduled, offer received or not) rather than vanishing once you've gotten what you needed.
- **Over-templating.** Bankers can spot a copy-pasted "hope you're doing well!" message instantly. Every follow-up should reference something specific from the actual conversation.
- **Treating a "no" as final.** If someone says the timing isn't right or they're not comfortable referring you yet, that's not necessarily a permanent no — a respectful, no-pressure relationship maintained over months can change the answer later in the cycle or in a future cycle.
- **Forgetting to say thank you after the outcome.** Whether or not you get the job, the person who referred you spent social capital on your behalf. A short note after the process ends, regardless of outcome, is what keeps the relationship open for next time — internships, full-time recruiting, or just as a professional contact for years afterward.

## How interviewers and bankers probe this

You won't get asked directly about your follow-up cadence in an interview, but it shows up indirectly. Interviewers sometimes ask "who else have you spoken with at the firm?" or "how did you hear about this role?" — a candidate who can name specific people and specific, non-generic details from those conversations demonstrates the same relationship-building instinct you're practicing here. Vague answers ("I talked to a few people on LinkedIn") read as exactly the transactional, low-effort networking this guide is telling you to avoid.
