## Recruiting has moved earlier every year — plan for that, not for last year

The single most important fact about IB recruiting timing is that it keeps moving earlier. Two decades ago, banks hired analysts in the winter for internships that started that same summer. Today, the process for many bulge brackets and elite boutiques effectively runs a full year or more before the internship start date. There's no sign this trend reverses — if anything, the 2025-26 cycle has continued to compress, with some banks opening applications and even extending offers to sophomores earlier than in prior years. Treat any specific date you read online as provisional and verify current-cycle deadlines yourself; the shape of the calendar below is durable, the exact weeks are not.

## Freshman year: exploration, not recruiting

If you're a freshman, you are not recruiting yet — you're building the foundation that makes recruiting possible later. Use this year to:

- Get involved in a finance-adjacent club (investment club, trading competition, case competition) so you have something to talk about besides classes.
- Start reading financial news regularly so market fluency becomes a habit, not a cram session.
- Identify which majors and GPA trajectory keep IB realistic — most banks screen around a 3.5 GPA.

Freshman-year networking (reaching out to alumni, attending info sessions) is low-stakes and useful for learning the landscape, but it is not the year that determines your outcome. Don't panic if you're starting from zero here.

## Sophomore fall: networking begins in earnest

This is the semester that matters most for undergrads targeting a top-tier bank. Because sophomore-summer internships are what feed the pipeline into junior-summer internships (which are themselves the pipeline into full-time offers), the networking that determines your sophomore-summer outcome has to start now — roughly a year and a half before that internship begins.

Concretely, sophomore fall is when you:

- Build a target list of alumni and second-degree contacts at banks you're interested in.
- Start sending cold outreach and booking coffee chats (see [who to reach and how to find them](/guide/alumni-networking) and [the coffee chat framework](/guide/coffee-chat-framework)).
- Polish your resume to a state where you'd hand it to a banker without embarrassment.

If you're reading this in the spring of sophomore year and haven't networked yet, you haven't missed everything — but you're behind, and the fix is to compress months of outreach into weeks, not to wait for junior year.

## Sophomore summer: the prerequisite reality

Here's the uncomfortable truth most students don't hear until it's too late: a sophomore-summer internship, ideally in a finance-related role (IB, S&T, corporate finance, even a smaller boutique or a Big 4 valuation practice), has become close to a prerequisite for a junior-summer bulge bracket or elite boutique internship. Banks increasingly hire their junior class from a pool that already has one finance internship on the resume, because "sophomore internship then full-time IB analyst" is now a well-worn two-summer track at target schools.

If you can't land a bank internship after sophomore year, the next-best options are corporate finance, corporate development, a Big 4 advisory or valuation group, or a smaller regional boutique — anything that gives you a legitimate finance bullet point to put in front of junior-summer recruiters. Landing nothing finance-related after sophomore year doesn't end your chances, but it does mean you're competing from a weaker starting position for junior-summer recruiting.

## Junior fall/winter: applications open

This is the main event for the traditional undergraduate pipeline. Junior-summer internship applications for many bulge brackets and elite boutiques now open in the December-January window — roughly a year and a half before the internship itself starts (a junior-summer internship the following June is being recruited for during the preceding winter of sophomore-into-junior year, in the accelerated version of the calendar). Some elite boutiques and a subset of bulge brackets have pushed even earlier, opening applications in the fall of sophomore year for what is effectively "junior-summer" recruiting compressed a full year forward.

The mechanics: applications open on a specific date, interviews (first round, then superday) often happen on a rolling basis within days to a few weeks of the application opening, and offers for the most competitive seats can be extended within the same week interviews start. Because review is rolling (see [how recruiting actually works](/guide/how-recruiting-works)), applying the day applications open is materially better than applying two weeks later, even if your resume doesn't change at all in that window.

## Spring into summer: off-cycle and middle-market stragglers

Not every seat fills during the accelerated fall/winter window. Middle-market banks, some boutiques, and off-cycle roles at any tier continue recruiting into the spring and even into the summer before the internship starts — sometimes as late as June or July for a limited number of last-minute openings. If you missed the main cycle, this is your recovery path, not a dead end. It requires more direct outreach (often skipping HR entirely and emailing group heads or MDs directly) because these processes aren't run through a structured on-campus pipeline.

## Worked example: mapping your own timeline

Say you're a sophomore starting in January of your sophomore year, and you're aiming for a bulge bracket junior-summer internship two summers from now. Working backward:

- Junior-summer internship start: June, roughly 17 months from now.
- Junior-summer applications likely open: the preceding December-January, roughly 11-12 months from now.
- You need a sophomore-summer internship on your resume before those applications open: that recruiting cycle is happening right now, this semester.
- To land that sophomore-summer internship, you need networking outreach starting immediately — not next semester.

That's the calendar compressed into one sentence: what you do this month determines your resume bullet for next month's recruiting cycle, which determines your shot at the cycle after that.

## Common mistakes

- **Assuming "junior year" means junior spring.** By the time junior spring arrives at many target schools, the junior-summer cycle already closed months earlier.
- **Skipping the sophomore-summer internship because "it's not IB anyway."** Any credible finance internship beats no internship when junior-summer recruiters screen resumes.
- **Waiting for exact dates before starting networking.** Outreach and relationship-building don't need a locked application date — start now and let the application, when it opens, be the culmination of a relationship, not the start of one.

## How interviewers probe this

Interviewers will often ask "why didn't you do an internship after freshman/sophomore year" if there's a gap. Have a clean, honest answer ready — a gap isn't disqualifying, but a vague one raises doubt about your seriousness.
