## The application isn't one thing

Most students picture "applying" as a single event: fill out a form, attach a resume, hit submit. In reality your application passes through three separate filters before a banker reads a word you wrote, and each filter can end your candidacy on its own. Understanding the three-filter structure changes how you spend your prep time.

**Filter one** is an automated applicant tracking system (ATS) that checks hard, binary criteria — GPA, school, work authorization, graduation date. **Filter two** is a resume screen, usually done by a junior HR recruiter or a first-year analyst, that skims for relevant experience and red flags. **Filter three** is the actual interview invite decision, often made or confirmed by someone in the group you're trying to join. You need to clear all three, and each one rewards a different kind of preparation.

## Where applications live

Three portal types show up repeatedly:

- **The bank's own careers site.** Every bulge bracket and most elite boutiques run a proprietary portal (Workday, iCIMS, or a custom build) where you create a profile, upload documents, and answer screening questions.
- **Campus recruiting platforms.** Handshake and similar university-run systems aggregate postings and, at target schools, sometimes let you apply with a saved profile in under a minute.
- **LinkedIn Easy Apply and firm-specific job boards.** Increasingly common for smaller and middle-market shops that don't maintain a heavyweight portal.

Whichever portal you use, treat the online application questions (short-answer "why this firm," availability dates, GPA self-report) as seriously as your resume. Recruiters cross-reference what you type into the form against your resume and your networking conversations. A GPA typed into a form that doesn't match your transcript, or a "why this group" answer that contradicts what you told an alum on a coffee chat, is an easy reason to cut you.

## The ATS: what it actually filters on

An ATS is not reading your resume for quality. It's checking a small number of structured fields:

- **GPA cutoff.** Most bulge brackets set a soft or hard line around 3.3–3.5 (occasionally 3.5+ for the most selective groups). "Soft" cutoffs get waived for a compelling story or a strong referral; "hard" cutoffs auto-reject regardless of anything else on your application.
- **School / target list membership.** Some ATS configurations route candidates from a firm's target and non-target school lists into different review queues, with non-target applications getting a lighter, faster screen — or none at all without a referral.
- **Graduation date and class year.** Applying for the wrong recruiting cycle (e.g., you're a junior applying to a role scoped for seniors) gets auto-filtered before anyone notices your resume is strong.
- **Parsed keywords, sometimes.** Some systems do lightweight keyword parsing against the job description. This is a much smaller factor at most banks than in other industries, but it's still a reason to write "financial modeling," "valuation," and "Excel" out in full rather than only as acronyms or abbreviations your parser might not catch.

**Practical implication:** submit a clean, simply formatted resume (standard fonts, no text boxes, no tables, no graphics) as a PDF unless the portal explicitly asks for a Word document. Complex formatting is the single most common reason a real resume gets mangled or dropped by parsing software.

## Rolling review: why day one beats day twenty

Most IB internship recruiting is **rolling** — the bank reviews and extends interview invites continuously as applications come in, rather than waiting for a fixed deadline and comparing the full pool at once. This single fact should reorganize your entire application strategy.

Here's the mechanic with numbers. Say a group is hiring for 4 summer analyst spots and expects to extend roughly 25 first-round interview invites total across the cycle. If the portal is open for 30 days and the bank starts reviewing on day one:

- On **day 1**, the applicant pool is small — maybe 40 people have applied. The recruiter reviewing that day is comparing you against 40 people, and 25 invites against a pool of 40 is very favorable odds.
- By **day 20**, the same portal might have 1,200 applicants. The bar for a day-20 invite is now set by the strongest resumes across the entire pool so far, and many of the 25 invite slots may already be gone — rolling review means invites go out continuously, not only after the deadline.

Applying on day one doesn't just mean "arriving early to a queue" — it means being evaluated against a much weaker comparison set while more invite capacity is still open. This is true even if your resume is identical on day 1 and day 20. The only thing that changed is timing.

**What this means for you in practice:**

1. Have your resume, cover letter (where required), and short-answer responses finished and proofread *before* the portal opens — not the week after.
2. Set calendar reminders for portal open dates the moment a firm publishes them, and check firm-specific and campus-recruiting-platform postings daily during peak season.
3. Never wait on a "maybe" from a networking contact before applying. A referral that arrives three weeks after the portal opens helps far less than a referral that arrives on day one — apply immediately, and let networking strengthen an application that's already in the system.

## Common mistakes

- **Waiting for the "perfect" resume draft.** A very good resume submitted on day one beats a perfect resume submitted on day fifteen. Ship it, then keep refining for firms whose portals open later.
- **Assuming a referral removes the need to apply fast.** Referrals help you clear the resume screen; they don't stop the ATS's GPA or eligibility filters, and they don't change the fact that invite capacity shrinks over time.
- **Inconsistent self-reported data.** Typing a rounded-up GPA, a different graduation date, or a different "preferred group" into two different portals is a fast way to get flagged during the human review stage, since recruiters do compare notes across systems informally.
- **Ignoring smaller portals.** Middle-market and boutique firms often have simpler, less automated processes — sometimes just an email inbox — where a fast, direct application matters even more because there's no large applicant pool diluting your odds.
