## The Lazard identity

Lazard is one of the oldest investment banks in the world, founded in 1848, and it has built a reputation that is genuinely distinct from any other bank on the Street. Two things define Lazard: an extraordinary global footprint in financial advisory (particularly in sovereign and cross-border transactions) and a restructuring practice that is consistently one of the top one or two in the world.

Lazard is a pure advisory firm — no balance sheet, no underwriting, no sales and trading. Its model is built entirely on the quality of its advice and the depth of its senior relationships. The culture is intellectual, global, and slightly more understated than some American banking cultures — the firm has strong French roots and a significant European presence that gives it a different character than most US-centric boutiques.

## Business lines and groups

**Financial Advisory:** M&A, activist defense, strategic advisory. Lazard advises on a broad range of M&A transactions globally, with particular depth in cross-border deals, where its international network gives it a genuine competitive advantage over US-centric boutiques.

**Restructuring:** Lazard's restructuring franchise is elite. The group advises on both debtor-side (helping companies restructure their own balance sheets) and creditor-side (helping lenders and bondholders navigate distressed situations) mandates. In major Chapter 11 cases and out-of-court restructurings, Lazard is consistently one of the first names that gets called.

**Asset Management (Lazard Asset Management):** Lazard has a standalone asset management business with significant AUM. This is separate from investment banking but worth knowing as context for the firm's overall identity.

## What makes Lazard distinctive

### The restructuring franchise

Lazard's restructuring practice deserves its own spotlight. While many banks have restructuring advisory capabilities, Lazard — alongside Evercore and PJT Partners — has built a franchise that attracts the most complex and consequential distressed situations. If you're interested in restructuring, Lazard should be near the top of your list.

Restructuring advisory requires an understanding of:
- Distressed capital structures (who holds what debt, what the priorities are in a bankruptcy)
- The absolute priority rule (secured debt > unsecured debt > equity in a liquidation)
- Reorganization value vs. liquidation value
- DIP (debtor-in-possession) financing
- Chapter 11 process timeline and key stakeholders

If you're interviewing for restructuring specifically, your technical prep should include these concepts alongside standard M&A technicals.

### The global advisory network

Lazard's cross-border M&A capability is genuinely differentiated. The firm has senior bankers in major financial centers around the world (London, Paris, Frankfurt, Hong Kong, Tokyo, among others) and has historically advised on high-profile government and sovereign transactions that other boutiques don't see. For students interested in international careers or cross-border transactions, Lazard's global model is a legitimate draw.

## What Lazard looks for

**Intellectual depth and analytical seriousness.** Lazard's culture rewards people who think carefully and go deep. The firm has a strong tradition of intellectual rigor — partners often came from backgrounds in law, economics, or academia alongside banking. Show that you're a serious thinker, not just a formula-memorizer.

**Global curiosity.** The firm's cross-border work means that analysts who are genuinely interested in geopolitics, international economics, and global capital markets fit the culture better than those narrowly focused on US deal flow.

**Independent thinking.** In interviews, Lazard tends to probe for candidates who form their own views — on industries, on deals, on market dynamics — rather than just reciting received wisdom. Have a genuine perspective on something.

**Restructuring candidates specifically:** Technical precision in distressed analysis, knowledge of the bankruptcy process, and the ability to think about credit and equity simultaneously.

## Interview process

1. **First-round** — Technical and behavioral, with analysts or associates. Restructuring interviews include distressed-specific questions; M&A advisory interviews focus on standard valuation and deal mechanics.
2. **Superday** — Multiple rounds including senior bankers. Lazard superday conversations often feel more like genuine intellectual discussions than scripted screens — the Partners want to understand how you think.

Networking matters at Lazard. Reach out to Lazard bankers through LinkedIn or your school's alumni network. Express specific interest in the firm's approach (cross-border M&A, restructuring, or a specific sector) and show that you've thought about why Lazard's model appeals to you.

## What "why Lazard" sounds like when it works

> "I've been drawn to Lazard for two reasons that aren't separate in my mind: the restructuring franchise and the global platform. The most complex advisory situations — companies under financial stress navigating multiple creditor classes across multiple jurisdictions — seem to gravitate to Lazard. That combination of restructuring depth and cross-border reach is rare, and I think the work that results is genuinely more analytically interesting than straightforward M&A."

Alternatively, for a student interested in M&A: mention a cross-border transaction Lazard advised on and why the complexity of a deal involving multiple jurisdictions, regulatory regimes, or currencies interested you.

## Practice this

Read about one major restructuring Lazard has been publicly credited with advising on. Understand: who hired Lazard (debtor or creditor), what the capital structure looked like before the process, what happened in the restructuring, and what the outcome was for different stakeholders. Then read about one cross-border M&A deal Lazard advised on. Between those two, you'll have a foundation for a substantive "why Lazard" conversation.
