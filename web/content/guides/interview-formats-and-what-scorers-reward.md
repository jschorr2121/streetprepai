## Why the format matters as much as the answer

Most candidates prepare one thing: better answers. That's necessary but not sufficient. The same "walk me through a DCF" response can score a 9 in a first-round phone call and a 4 in a HireVue, because the two formats are measuring different things. If you don't know what's being scored, you're optimizing blind.

There are three formats you'll move through, usually in this order: an asynchronous video screen (HireVue-style), a live first-round interview (phone or video, one-on-one or panel), and a superday (a compressed day of 4-8 back-to-back interviews on-site or over video). Each has a different grader, a different time budget, and a different tolerance for imperfection.

## The three formats, side by side

**Asynchronous video screen.** You get a prompt on a screen, a short prep window, a fixed recording window, and no human on the other end while you're talking. It's usually the very first gate after you submit an application, and it exists to cut volume — a firm might send this to 800 candidates and only advance 150. Nobody watches most of these live; a mix of algorithmic scoring and fast human skimming does the first pass. That changes what "good" means here: you are graded on delivery signals (pace, eye contact with the lens, hitting the time limit cleanly) at least as much as content. See the dedicated guide on this format for the exact mechanics.

**First round.** A real person, usually a junior or mid-level banker, talking to you for 20-30 minutes, often by phone. This is a mix of fit and technical, and it's conversational — the interviewer reacts to what you say and follows up on it. The bar here is "would I want to work sixteen hours next to this person and trust their model isn't wrong." Content accuracy matters much more than in the async screen, but so does composure under a real-time follow-up.

**Superday.** Several first-round-style conversations stacked back to back, often with senior people mixed in, sometimes ending in a group case or lunch that's being watched even though it's billed as informal. The grading question shifts again: it's no longer "is this person good," it's "does this person hold up interview five the same way they held up interview one." Consistency, not peak performance, is what's being measured. There's a separate guide for pacing and logistics on superday day itself.

## Content scoring vs. delivery scoring

Every one of these formats scores you on two axes that are easy to conflate:

**Content** is whether your answer is technically correct and complete — did you use WACC and not cost of equity to discount unlevered cash flows, did you get the accretion/dilution direction right, did you name the right EV bridge items. Content is binary-ish: you either know it or you're guessing.

**Delivery** is how the correct content arrives — structure, pace, eye contact or vocal energy, whether you signal confidence versus hedge on every sentence. Delivery is what separates two candidates who both got the DCF steps right, and it's the axis most candidates under-practice because it's harder to self-grade. You can't easily hear yourself the way an interviewer hears you.

The weighting between the two shifts by format. Async video screens lean delivery-heavy because there's often no live grader parsing nuance in real time. First rounds and superdays lean content-heavy on technical questions and delivery-heavy on fit questions — nobody is fact-checking your "tell me about a time you led a team" story, but they are absolutely grading how you tell it.

## How real interviewers probe with follow-ups

Live interviews are not a script you recite once and move on from — they're a conversation the interviewer steers to see how you handle pressure. A few patterns worth internalizing:

They will interrupt you to narrow scope. If you start an answer that assumes more time has passed than the question intended, a good interviewer stops you mid-sentence and re-anchors the question ("assume this is immediately after, nothing else has happened yet") rather than let you finish a wrong answer. That's not hostility — it's testing whether you can adjust on the fly instead of finishing a memorized script that no longer fits.

They will ask you to defend a rule of thumb the moment you state one. If you say "precedent transactions usually value higher than comps because of a control premium," expect the next question to be some version of "does that always hold?" The interviewer already knows the exception; they want to see whether you volunteer the caveat or need it dragged out of you.

They will change one variable and ask you to redo the logic. A classic move is to take a scenario you just solved cleanly (debt issuance, an inventory purchase) and add a second event, or move the timeline, and watch whether you re-derive the answer or just pattern-match to what you said before.

They will occasionally let you sit in "I'm not sure." A candidate who says "I'm not 100% certain, but here's my best reasoning" and then reasons correctly usually scores better than one who guesses confidently and gets it wrong. Interviewers are listening for how you think, not just whether you land on the memorized answer.

## What this means for how you practice

Practicing in front of a mirror or writing out answers doesn't train you for any of this, because nothing pushes back. You need reps where something interrupts you, narrows a scope, or asks "why" a second time — which is exactly what live-format mock practice and the Interview Studio's follow-up mode are built to simulate. Drilling content alone gets you to "technically correct." Drilling under follow-up pressure is what gets you to "survives a real interviewer."

## Common mistakes

- **Prepping one format and assuming it transfers.** A candidate who nails written flashcards can still freeze on an async video timer, because they never practiced against a clock with no ability to pause.
- **Treating delivery as unfakeable and content as everything.** Recruiters see hundreds of technically correct, flat, unconvincing answers every cycle. Both axes are trainable — invest in both.
- **Answering the question you prepared instead of the one that was asked.** When an interviewer narrows scope mid-question, candidates who don't listen closely finish their rehearsed answer anyway. That's an instant flag.
- **Treating a superday as one long interview.** Energy and structure that are strong in interview one but visibly fade by interview five read as a consistency problem, even if the content in interview five was technically fine.
