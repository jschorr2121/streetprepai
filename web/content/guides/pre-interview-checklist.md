## Why a checklist beats "studying more"

Most candidates walking into a superday have technically prepared enough. What sinks them isn't a missing LBO mechanic — it's a gap they discover mid-answer: a story that trails off, a market view they can't defend past one follow-up, a resume line they can't explain crisply. A checklist forces you to find those gaps on your own couch, not in front of a VP.

Run this the way a pilot runs a pre-flight check: not because you doubt the plane works, but because the cost of finding a problem at 30,000 feet is categorically different from finding it on the tarmac.

## The 8-item checklist

### 1. Your resume story, cold

You should be able to walk through your resume in under 90 seconds, chronologically, with each transition answering "why did you do that next." If any line makes you pause to remember what you did, rewrite the bullet in your own prep notes until it's automatic. Interviewers open with "walk me through your resume" specifically to see if you own your own narrative.

### 2. Three STAR stories, fully built

Pick three experiences — one leadership, one conflict/failure, one achievement you're proud of — and write each out in full Situation-Task-Action-Result form. "Full" means you know the specific numbers, the names of the stakeholders (roles, not necessarily names), and the actual outcome, not a vague "it went well." A story you can only summarize in one sentence isn't ready; you'll get asked "what did you do specifically" and stall.

### 3. Strengths and weaknesses, with evidence

Don't pick a strength you can't back with a two-sentence example, and don't pick a weakness that's secretly a strength ("I work too hard"). A weakness interviewers respect sounds like: "I used to over-index on getting every model perfectly formatted before sharing it, which slowed down team turnaround. I've fixed this by sharing rough drafts earlier and iterating live." That shows self-awareness and a fix, which is what's actually being tested.

### 4. Objection prep — the thing they'll push back on

Every candidate has one weak point in their story: a low GPA semester, a gap year, switching majors, no prior finance internship. Write down the single most likely objection an interviewer would raise about your candidacy, and prepare a direct, non-defensive answer. If you dodge or over-explain, it reads as insecurity. State it plainly, give context in one sentence, move on.

### 5. One deal, cold — for the specific firm

Pick one live or recent deal that the firm you're interviewing with actually worked on (check the firm's website, recent press, or league tables). Know the acquirer, target, deal size, and — if disclosed — the multiple paid. You don't need every detail, but you need enough to have a real opinion if asked "why do you think they did this deal."

### 6. One market view you can defend

Have a point of view on one current market theme — rates, an M&A wave in a sector you're interested in, IPO market conditions — and be ready for "why" three times in a row. If your view collapses after the second follow-up, it's not actually your view; it's something you read once. Rebuild it with your own reasoning, not just headlines.

### 7. Your own deal or project, defensible

If you've done a stock pitch, a case competition deal, or a personal investment thesis, be ready to defend the valuation methodology and the key assumption that would break it. Interviewers love asking "what's the one number that, if wrong, kills your thesis" — know your answer before they ask.

### 8. Technical readiness, mapped by chapter

Don't cram randomly. Map your remaining prep time against a syllabus: accounting (the three statements and how they link), valuation (comps, DCF, precedent transactions), and LBO mechanics (sources and uses, returns math). If you can't say which chapter you're weakest in, you haven't actually assessed yourself yet — spend an hour doing that before doing more drilling.

## A worked example: sanity-checking your own deal

Say your stock pitch is a long thesis on a retailer trading at $40/share with 50 million shares outstanding (a $2.0 billion market cap), $500 million of net debt, giving an $2.5 billion enterprise value. You project next-twelve-months EBITDA of $350 million, so your entry multiple is:

EV / EBITDA = $2,500M / $350M = 7.14x

You think the stock re-rates to 8.5x as margins improve. If EBITDA also grows 10% to $385 million, your target enterprise value is:

$385M × 8.5x = $3,272.5M

Subtract the same $500 million net debt to get target equity value: $3,272.5M − $500M = $2,772.5M

Divide by 50 million shares: $2,772.5M / 50M = $55.45/share

That's roughly 39% upside from $40. Now find the assumption that breaks it: if margins don't improve and the multiple stays at 7.14x instead of re-rating to 8.5x, your target equity value is only ($385M × 7.14x) − $500M = $2,248.9M, or about $44.98/share — just 12% upside. The multiple expansion, not the EBITDA growth, is doing most of the work in your thesis. That's the number an interviewer will find in about ninety seconds, so you should find it first.

## How interviewers probe this

They rarely ask a checklist item head-on. Instead they ask an open question, then use follow-ups to see if your answer has a floor. "Walk me through a time you disagreed with someone" gets a fine first answer from almost anyone — the real test is "what would you have done differently" and "how did the other person react," which only someone who actually lived (or deeply rehearsed) the story can answer without inventing details on the spot. Rehearsed-but-hollow answers crack on the second follow-up; real preparation survives the third.

## Common mistakes

The most common failure isn't lack of knowledge — it's uneven prep. Candidates over-index on technical drilling because it feels measurable, and under-prepare stories and market views because those feel "soft." But superdays are usually lost on the soft items, because that's where interviators differentiate between similarly-qualified candidates. A second mistake is rehearsing stories so heavily they sound scripted; aim for knowing the bones of the story cold while keeping the delivery conversational.

## Running the checklist 48 hours out

Block two sessions, one per day. On day one (T-48h), go through items 1–4 out loud, ideally recorded on your phone — you'll hear the gaps you can't see on paper. On day two (T-24h), cover items 5–8, and use the evening before your superday only for light review: skim your stories once, don't cram new technical material. New information the night before tends to crowd out what you've already internalized, which is the opposite of what you want walking in.
