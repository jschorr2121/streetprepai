## Why a list, not a spree

Applying to every bank you can find feels productive but wastes your two scarcest resources: time you could spend networking into a smaller set of firms, and attention you could spend tailoring each application. A target list forces you to decide, in advance, which firms deserve real effort — and it gives your tracker something concrete to track. If you haven't opened it yet, the [application tracker](/tools/applications) is built to hold exactly this list and every status update that follows.

## The three tiers

Borrow the reach/match/safety framing from college admissions — it maps cleanly onto banking recruiting because both are competitive processes where your background predicts, but doesn't guarantee, outcomes.

**Reach.** Firms where your background is a stretch relative to what typically gets hired there — a group with a very low historical hit rate from your school, a bulge bracket with no prior connection, or a firm where you'd need a strong referral just to clear the resume screen. You should still apply, but you shouldn't expect these to carry your cycle.

**Match.** Firms where candidates with your GPA, school, and experience level regularly get interviews. This is where most of your networking hours should go, because your marginal effort has the highest payoff here — you're not fighting the odds, you're tipping them.

**Safety.** Firms with a track record of hiring from your background with real regularity — often middle-market or regional boutiques, or firms where you already have a strong internal contact. These exist so that a bad cycle at the reach and match tiers doesn't mean no offer at all.

A workable split for a first-time undergraduate summer analyst cycle: roughly **20% reach, 50% match, 30% safety**. If you're a stronger-than-typical candidate for your target tier (higher GPA, prior relevant internship, strong network), shift weight toward reach. If you're recruiting later than ideal or from a non-target background, shift weight toward safety and lean harder on off-cycle options — see [off-cycle and recovery paths](/guide/off-cycle-and-recovery-paths).

## Sizing the list

A common failure mode is a list that's too short to survive attrition (some firms won't respond, some interviews won't convert) and a list that's too long to service with real networking. A reasonable target for a full undergraduate cycle is **25–40 total applications**, worked example below.

### Worked example: sizing a 30-firm list

Say you're aiming for at least one offer and you estimate, based on your GPA, school, and how much networking you can realistically sustain, the following per-tier interview conversion rates (fraction of applications that turn into a first-round interview):

- Reach: 10% interview rate
- Match: 25% interview rate
- Safety: 45% interview rate

Using the 20/50/30 split on a 30-firm list:

- Reach: 6 firms × 10% = **0.6 expected interviews**
- Match: 15 firms × 25% = **3.75 expected interviews**
- Safety: 9 firms × 45% = **4.05 expected interviews**

Total expected first-round interviews: 0.6 + 3.75 + 4.05 = **8.4**.

Now apply a rough first-round-to-offer conversion rate. If you convert first rounds to offers at roughly 20% (a reasonable planning number once you're actually prepared — it will be far lower if you go in without practicing technicals and behaviorals), that's 8.4 × 20% ≈ **1.7 expected offers** from this list. That's enough cushion to plan around one real offer without being so large you can't network into most of the firms on it.

If your own math comes out under roughly 1.0 expected offers, the fix isn't necessarily "apply to more firms" — it's usually "shift the tier split toward safety" or "raise your interview conversion rate at match-tier firms by networking harder into them," since networking effort moves conversion rates far more than list size does.

## Mapping networking coverage to the list

Applications alone rarely carry a reach-tier firm, and rolling review (see [application mechanics](/guide/application-mechanics)) means an application sitting in the ATS with no internal advocate is easy to miss. Match networking intensity to tier:

- **Reach firms:** aim for at least one real relationship — someone who knows your name before your resume lands, ideally someone willing to forward it internally. This typically takes several coffee chats and follow-ups per firm, not one cold email.
- **Match firms:** at least one or two networking contacts each, enough that a recruiter recognizes your name if it comes up, even without a formal referral.
- **Safety firms:** light networking is enough, and for some smaller safety-tier firms with simpler processes, a strong application alone can be sufficient.

This means your 30-firm list doesn't require 30 firms' worth of deep relationships — it requires concentrated networking on the 6 reach firms and the 15 match firms, roughly 21 firms getting real attention, while the 9 safety firms mostly need a clean application on time.

## Keeping the list alive in the tracker

A target list is only useful if it's a living document, not a one-time spreadsheet you build in September and never touch again. For every firm, track: portal open date, application submitted date, networking contacts and their status, interview status, and next action. This is exactly what the [application tracker](/tools/applications) tool is for — use it from the day you build your list, not after you've already lost track of which portals you've applied to.

## Common mistakes

- **All reach, no safety.** A list of only brand-name bulge brackets and elite boutiques with no realistic safety tier means a rough cycle can leave you with nothing. Build the safety tier even if it feels like settling — you can always turn down a safety-tier offer once you have a better one.
- **Treating every firm on the list identically.** Sending the same generic effort to a reach firm and a safety firm wastes your best networking hours on firms where they won't move the needle and under-invests where they would.
- **Building the list once and never revisiting it.** Portal dates shift, firms open unannounced off-cycle roles, and your own conversion data updates as the cycle goes — revisit the list every couple of weeks during peak season.
- **Confusing list size with effort.** Forty scattershot applications with zero networking behind them convert worse than twenty-five applications backed by real relationships at the match and reach tiers.
