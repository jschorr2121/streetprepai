## Why "good enough" isn't good enough

A recruiter screening resumes for a competitive IB program is looking for reasons to say no, not reasons to say yes — when a role gets 400 applications for 6 spots, the fastest way to get through the pile is to find a disqualifying flaw and move on. That means a resume with one strong internship and zero red flags often beats a resume with three internships and one glaring issue.

This section walks through the flags that get resumes cut most often, and the specific fix for each. None of these require rewriting your whole document — they're targeted edits.

## Flag 1: Unexplained gaps

A gap of more than a summer or a semester with no listed activity reads as a question mark. Recruiters don't assume the best explanation (you were caring for a family member, doing unpaid research, recovering from an injury) — they assume you have nothing to show for that period.

**The fix:** Don't leave white space where the gap sits. If you did anything structured during that window — a self-directed project, an online certificate, part-time freelance work, an unpaid research assistantship — list it with dates that cover the gap, even if it's a single bullet. If you genuinely did nothing resume-worthy, consider whether a brief, factual explanation belongs in your cover letter rather than leaving the resume to imply the worst-case reading. What you never want is a recruiter doing math on your dates and coming up short with no explanation anywhere in the application.

## Flag 2: Vague bullets

This is the most common flag by volume. A bullet like "Assisted with financial analysis and market research" tells a reader nothing — not what you actually did, not how well you did it, not why it mattered. Vague bullets read as either inexperience (you don't know what you actually contributed) or padding (you're dressing up a task that wasn't substantive).

**The fix:** Every bullet needs three things: what you did (a strong action verb), the context or method (how), and the result (ideally quantified). See [Banker bullet structure](/guide/banker-bullet-structure) for the full framework — the short version is below.

Before: "Assisted with financial analysis and market research for a client project."

After: "Built a comparable-companies analysis across 14 regional grocery chains to benchmark a client's EBITDA margin, identifying a 3-point gap versus the peer median that informed the final recommendation."

Walk through why the second version works:

- "Built" is a stronger, more specific verb than "assisted with."
- "14 regional grocery chains" quantifies scope — a reader can gauge the size of the task.
- "3-point gap versus the peer median" is a real, checkable number, not a vague claim of impact.
- The bullet ends by connecting the work to a decision ("informed the final recommendation"), which shows the work mattered rather than existing in isolation.

If you genuinely can't quantify a bullet — some roles don't produce clean numbers — quantify scope instead of outcome: number of clients, number of models built, number of meetings run, size of a dataset. A number of *some* kind beats no number.

## Flag 3: GPA games

Recruiters see GPA manipulation constantly, and it's one of the fastest ways to lose credibility. The common versions:

- **Omitting GPA entirely with no explanation**, especially once you're a junior. Recruiters assume the worst — that it's below the firm's cutoff (often 3.3-3.5, varies by firm) — and an unexplained omission is treated the same as reporting a low number.
- **Listing major GPA without cumulative GPA** when the two diverge meaningfully. If your major GPA is 3.8 and your cumulative is 3.1, showing only the 3.8 looks like you're hiding something, and it usually gets caught in the interview when someone asks for the transcript.
- **Rounding up.** A 3.45 is not a 3.5.

**The fix — worked example:** Suppose your cumulative GPA is 3.45 on your school's 4.0 scale, but your school also reports a secondary 4.33 scale (common where A+ grades exist). Convert precisely rather than picking whichever number looks better:

(3.45 / 4.33) × 4.0 = 3.186, which you'd report as 3.19 — not rounded to 3.2, and clearly labeled as "3.19/4.0" if your transcript's native scale is 4.33, so a recruiter comparing across schools isn't misled.

If your GPA is genuinely below a firm's typical cutoff, the honest options are: report it as-is and let your experience carry the application, or omit it only if the firm's posting explicitly doesn't require it — never omit it hoping no one notices, because it will surface at the interview or offer stage and the omission itself becomes the story.

## Flag 4: Formatting tells

Beyond the general formatting standards covered in [One-page conventions & formatting](/guide/one-page-resume-conventions), a few specific formatting choices function as red flags on their own because they signal the resume wasn't built specifically for finance recruiting:

- **Free template features** — colored sidebars, skill percentage bars, a headshot, decorative icons next to section headers. These are built for design/marketing/general corporate resumes and stand out immediately against a stack of standard IB resumes.
- **Inconsistent verb tense.** Past roles in present tense ("Manage a team of five") reads as careless proofreading.
- **A summary/objective statement at the top.** IB resumes don't use these — the resume itself, plus your story in the interview, does that work. A generic objective line ("Seeking a challenging role in a fast-paced environment...") is dead space.
- **Listing high school achievements** once you're a junior with two internships. It signals you're padding because you don't trust your recent experience to stand on its own.

**The fix:** Strip the resume down to plain black text, standard fonts, and content-only sections. If you're unsure whether something reads as a template artifact, compare your resume side by side with a plain, standard-format resume from a peer who's been through the process — the visual differences are usually obvious once you look for them.

## How interviewers probe these flags

Even flags that don't get you screened out on paper tend to resurface as direct questions once you're in the room:

- **"I noticed a gap between your sophomore and junior summer — what were you working on?"** If your fix was a vague bullet with no real substance behind it, this question will expose that immediately. Only list something here if you can talk about it in detail.
- **"Walk me through this bullet — what exactly did you build?"** A quantified bullet invites a natural follow-up. Be ready to explain your number, not just recite it. If you claimed "reduced processing time by 25%," know how that 25% was measured.
- **"Why isn't your GPA on your resume?"** Have a real answer ready, not a deflection. If it's below a threshold, own it briefly and pivot to what your experience demonstrates instead.

## Common mistakes when self-editing

- **Only checking for typos.** Typos matter, but structural flags (vague bullets, unexplained gaps) get more resumes cut than a single misplaced comma.
- **Fixing the flag your friend pointed out and stopping there.** Run the full checklist — gaps, vagueness, GPA, formatting — every time, since these issues cluster and fixing one in isolation often leaves others in place.
- **Waiting until the resume is "final" to check for red flags.** Build this pass into every draft, not just the last one before submission, since fixes to vague bullets often require going back to add detail you didn't originally capture.
- **Assuming a strong GPA excuses vague bullets, or vice versa.** Recruiters read the whole page. A high GPA next to three vague bullets still reads as an unfinished resume.

Before you submit, read your resume once specifically hunting for these four flags and nothing else — not content quality, not typos, just gaps, vagueness, GPA presentation, and formatting tells. A dedicated pass catches things a general read-through misses.
