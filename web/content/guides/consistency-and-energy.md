## Why this is a skill separate from "knowing the answers"

Most candidates walking into a superday have already done the content work — they know their story, they've drilled the technicals, they can walk through a DCF in their sleep. What separates the candidates who get offers from the ones who don't is often not knowledge. It's whether the fourth interview sounds as sharp as the first.

Here's the mechanic you're up against: after your superday ends, the interviewers who met you go into a room together and compare notes. If your resume walkthrough in round two sounds meaningfully different from your resume walkthrough in round five — different emphasis, a dropped detail, a story that's grown or shrunk — someone notices. Inconsistency reads as either nerves or, worse, as embellishment. Neither helps you.

## Same story, every room

This doesn't mean memorizing a script word for word and reciting it like a robot — that reads as stiff and unnatural, which has its own cost. It means locking in the *content* of your answers so the facts, the emphasis, and the throughline don't drift, while letting the delivery breathe naturally each time.

Practically, that means before your superday you should have fixed versions of:

- Your resume walkthrough (the same 90 seconds, the same three "chapters" of your story)
- Your three behavioral stories in STAR form (the same situation, same numbers, same outcome)
- Your "why this firm" answer (the same two or three reasons, ideally anchored to something specific about the group)
- Your one firm deal and your one market view

If you told an interviewer in round one that your summer internship involved "modeling three acquisition scenarios," don't tell round four's interviewer it was "four scenarios." Small drifts like this are exactly what a debrief conversation catches, because someone will ask a follow-up that assumes the first number.

A useful discipline: after you finalize each story, write down the two or three specific facts inside it — the number, the name, the outcome — on a small notecard. Between rounds, a ten-second glance refreshes exact details instead of relying on memory that's now five interviews and several hours deep.

## Managing fatigue across five to ten rounds

A superday is an endurance event disguised as a series of short conversations. By interview six, most candidates are running on adrenaline and coffee, and it shows in flatter energy, slower response times, and shorter, less enthusiastic answers. Interviewers can tell the difference between someone who's tired and someone who's genuinely uninterested — but from across the table, tired often looks like uninterested.

A few concrete levers:

**Protect the physical basics.** Water between every round, not just when you remember. A bathroom break if you need one — nobody penalizes you for stepping out for sixty seconds between interviews. If there's food available (a green room, a lunch break), eat something, even a few bites. Low blood sugar four hours in is a real and avoidable failure mode.

**Reset the clock between rounds, not just the topic.** Each interviewer has zero information about how your last round went. If it went badly, they don't know that, and you don't need to carry it in with you. Give yourself a physical reset — two slow breaths, roll your shoulders, stand up if you can — before you walk into the next room. Treat the reset as literally starting a new day.

**Manufacture energy for the later rounds deliberately.** Your natural energy dips by round five or six; assume this in advance and compensate rather than being surprised by it. Before you walk in, remind yourself out loud (even under your breath) that this interviewer has never met you and deserves your full attention. A little forced enthusiasm in round six reads as identical to genuine enthusiasm in round one — the interviewer has no baseline to compare it to.

**Don't debrief with other candidates.** If your superday runs alongside other candidates in a shared waiting room, resist comparing notes on what you were each asked. It's tempting, and it does nothing but spike your anxiety about a round you can't redo, or give you false confidence about a round you haven't had yet.

## Reading the room: analyst versus MD

Interviewers at different levels are testing different things, and adjusting your register — not your content, your register — to who's across the table is a real edge.

**Analysts and associates** (typically your first one or two rounds) are usually testing technical fluency and whether they'd want to sit next to you at 11pm during a live deal. They tend to ask more granular technical questions and care about precision — get the WACC formula exactly right, know the difference between EV and equity value cold. They also respond well to genuine, slightly informal rapport; they were in your seat eighteen months ago and remember what it felt like.

**VPs** often sit in the middle: they'll test technicals but with more emphasis on how you think through an unfamiliar problem, not just whether you have a memorized answer. Show your reasoning out loud when you don't instantly know something — a VP would rather hear a structured guess than silence.

**MDs**, especially in a final round, are frequently testing something different from technical depth: judgment, poise, and whether they can picture you in front of a client. MDs ask fewer nitty-gritty technical questions and more "tell me about a time," "why banking," and market-view questions. They're also more likely to go off-script — a tangent about something in the news, a question that has no clean textbook answer. Don't panic when an MD's question doesn't map to anything you rehearsed; that's often the point. Stay conversational, structure your answer out loud, and don't be afraid to say "I haven't thought about that before, but here's how I'd approach it."

The adjustment isn't performative — it's about matching energy. An overly casual tone with an MD can read as unprepared for the room; an overly rehearsed, technical-recitation tone with a first-year analyst can read as robotic. Neither costs you the offer on its own, but small mismatches accumulate across a debrief.

## Common mistakes

- **Letting your story evolve mid-day.** Nerves and fatigue make people add hedges, extra detail, or forget details as the day goes on. Lock your key facts before you walk in, and refer to the notecard between rounds if needed.
- **Visibly checking out in the last two rounds.** Interviewers scheduled at the end of the day know they're the end of the day — and they're specifically watching for whether you still bring full energy. Treat interview seven like interview one.
- **Over-adjusting your personality by seniority.** You don't need to become a different person for an MD versus an analyst — small shifts in formality and pacing are enough. A wholesale personality switch reads as inauthentic to anyone paying attention.
- **Assuming a bad round is fatal.** One rough interview in a five-round day rarely sinks a candidate on its own — debriefs weigh the whole day. The real risk is letting that one round bleed into the next one's energy.
