## Why generic research gets you cut

Every candidate says a version of "I want to work at a firm with a collaborative culture and strong deal flow." Interviewers hear that sentence thirty times a superday. It doesn't cost you the interview by itself, but it wastes the one question — "why us" — that's supposed to be your easiest points.

The fix isn't more enthusiasm. It's more specificity, and specificity comes from a research process, not a vibe. Treat firm research the same way you'd treat modeling: a checklist you run every time, not something you improvise the night before.

## The four things worth knowing

You don't need to become an expert on a bank's entire history. You need four categories of knowledge, each cheap to gather and each hard to fake in a follow-up question.

### 1. Recent financial performance

Pull the firm's most recent quarterly or annual earnings release (public banks file these; look at the investment banking segment specifically, not firm-wide revenue). You're looking for:

- Whether IB fees are up or down year-over-year, and what management said drove it
- Which product lines are carrying the segment — M&A advisory, equity capital markets, debt capital markets — since that mix shifts every year with market conditions
- Any forward-looking commentary from the earnings call about pipeline or hiring

You don't need to memorize exact dollar figures. You need a directional read: "advisory fees were soft last year industry-wide, but the firm's DCM business held up because rate volatility kept issuers coming to market." That sentence signals you follow the business, not just the brand.

### 2. Recent deals

Find two or three deals the firm advised on in the last twelve months — a mix of one you find genuinely interesting and one that's recent enough to be current. For each, know:

- The two parties and roughly what the deal was (acquisition, merger of equals, restructuring, IPO)
- Which side the firm advised — buyer, seller, or both
- Why the deal happened, at a headline level (consolidation in a fragmenting industry, a company divesting a non-core unit, a take-private in a beaten-down sector)

You're not expected to know deal terms or valuation multiples unless the deal was heavily covered in the press and you looked them up on purpose. The point is to have something concrete to reference instead of "I read about your recent deals and was impressed."

### 3. League table position

League tables rank firms by deal volume or fee share within a sector, region, or deal type over a period. They're a compact way to talk about where a firm is strong. See the companion guide on [reading league tables](/guide/reading-league-tables) for how to interpret them without over-claiming — but at minimum, know roughly where the firm ranks in the sector or product group you're recruiting for. "Top five in healthcare M&A globally last year" is a fact you can drop into an answer in five words and it does real work.

### 4. Culture signals

This is the category most candidates skip because it's less quantifiable, and it's the one that actually differentiates firms of similar prestige from each other. Sources, in order of reliability:

- **Your own networking notes.** If you've done coffee chats (see the [networking guide](/guide/alumni-networking) if you haven't started), you likely already have two or three firsthand quotes about hours, staffing model, or how deal teams are run. These are gold — nobody else in the interview room has them.
- **Firm-published content.** Analyst program pages, "day in the life" content, and any public statements from group heads about how they run their team. Read for what's specific versus what's boilerplate — "we value excellence and teamwork" is boilerplate everywhere.
- **Recent public reporting on the firm.** Layoffs, promotions of note, changes in leadership, expansion into a new sector or region. This tells you what's live right now, which matters because interviewers are living it too.

## Assembling a "why us" that survives follow-ups

A weak "why us" answer is one sentence of praise. A strong one is a structure you can defend when the interviewer pushes back — and they will, because "why us" follow-ups are where interviewers test whether you actually did the work.

The structure that holds up:

1. **One specific, verifiable fact** about the firm (a deal, a league table position, a strategic move) that you find genuinely interesting.
2. **Why it's interesting to you** — tied to something real about your background or goals, not a platitude.
3. **A firsthand data point**, if you have one — something a banker told you in a coffee chat, or something you noticed comparing this firm to others you've researched.

### Worked example

Say you're prepping for a healthcare-coverage group interview at a firm that recently advised on a mid-cap hospital system's divestiture of its home-health division. A thin answer: "I'm really interested in healthcare and I think your firm does great work in the space." A defensible answer:

"I've been following consolidation in the hospital sector, and the [division] divestiture your team advised on this spring caught my attention because it's part of a broader pattern of health systems shedding non-core assets to focus capital on core clinical operations. I spoke with an associate on your healthcare team in March, and what stood out was how much cross-functional work happens between the M&A and DCM teams on deals like this, since divestitures of that size usually come with a financing component. That combination of sector focus and product breadth is specifically why I want to be here rather than at a pure boutique."

Notice what makes it hold up: if the interviewer asks "what do you think drove that divestiture," you have a real answer, because you understood the deal rather than just naming it. If they ask "who'd you talk to," you have a name and a real conversation behind it.

## How interviewers probe this

Interviewers who suspect a memorized answer will ask a layered follow-up rather than moving on. Common probes:

- **"What did you think of the rationale for that deal?"** — Tests whether you understood the deal or just googled the headline.
- **"How does that compare to what [competitor firm] is doing in the space?"** — Tests whether your research was firm-specific or copy-pasted across every application.
- **"What did that associate tell you that surprised you?"** — Tests whether the networking conversation actually happened or is a name-drop.

## Common mistakes

- **Reciting the firm's own marketing language back to them.** If a phrase sounds like it came from the "About Us" page, an interviewer has heard it a hundred times this cycle. Translate it into your own observation instead.
- **Naming a deal you can't explain.** If you mention a transaction, be ready for one follow-up question about it. If you can't answer, don't mention it — pick something you actually understand.
- **Treating every firm's "why us" as interchangeable.** If your answer would work verbatim for three different banks, it's not specific enough yet. Swap in the specific deal, group, or person and see if it still holds together.
- **Skipping firsthand sources entirely.** Public research alone reads as generic because every other candidate did the same googling. A single real conversation is worth more than an hour of additional reading.

## Practice this

Pick one firm you're actively recruiting for. In twenty minutes, fill in the four categories above with one item each — one earnings takeaway, one deal, one league table fact, one culture signal from a real conversation or firsthand source. Then write your "why us" answer using the three-part structure and say it out loud. If it would sound identical for a different firm with the names swapped, go back and make it more specific.
