## The most important 90 seconds of your interview

Almost every IB interview starts the same way: "So, walk me through your resume." It sounds casual. It isn't. The way you open this question signals your confidence, your ability to structure information under pressure, and whether you've actually thought about your own story or are going to wing it.

A great opening makes the interviewer want to ask follow-up questions. A weak one makes them feel like they're pulling teeth. You control which one happens.

## What they're really evaluating

Three things at once:

1. **Coherence** — Does your background have a logical thread? Does each step connect to the next, or does it feel random?
2. **Articulation** — Can you distill a complex story into 90 seconds with crisp, specific language? That's a core analyst skill.
3. **Judgment about what matters** — Do you know which parts of your resume are worth 30 seconds and which deserve three? Over-explaining sophomore semester coursework while breezing past a Goldman internship is a red flag.

## The structure: four beats

### Beat 1 — Brief context (10 seconds)

Name your school, major, and maybe year. Keep this extremely tight. They have your resume in front of them. They don't need a detailed biographical introduction.

> "I'm a junior at the University of Virginia studying economics with a focus in finance."

That's it. Move on.

### Beat 2 — The experience arc (50–60 seconds)

Hit your most relevant experiences in chronological or thematic order, connecting each one with a forward-looking thread. Don't recite bullet points — synthesize. For each role, one crisp sentence on what you did and one on what you took away.

> "Freshman summer, I interned at a regional commercial bank — I got exposure to credit analysis, which made me want to understand the equity and advisory side of transactions. That pushed me toward a sophomore-year internship at a boutique investment bank in [city], where I built models and drafted marketing materials for two sell-side M&A processes. From there, I joined my school's investment club as a portfolio manager and have been covering healthcare names. This summer, I'm interning at [firm] in their industrials group."

Notice: each experience answers "and then what?" in a way that builds to where you are today.

### Beat 3 — The connecting thread (15 seconds)

Name the through-line. Why does this progression make sense? What does it say about what you value or what you're building toward?

> "The through-line for me has been wanting to get closer to real transactions — understanding not just the models but the judgment and strategy behind them. Banking is where that happens."

You don't need to be dramatic. You just need to show that your resume isn't random.

### Beat 4 — The handoff (5 seconds)

End with a forward-looking sentence that signals you're done and invites them to dig in.

> "Happy to go deeper on any part of that."

This is better than trailing off or awkwardly waiting. It gives the interviewer a clear cue that you've finished.

## Common mistakes

**Running too long.** If you're still talking at the 2-minute mark, you've lost them. Practice until you can hit the whole structure in 90 seconds comfortably.

**Reciting the resume linearly.** They can read. Your job is to tell them what to notice, not read the document back to them.

**Treating every item as equal.** You have 90 seconds. A relevant IB internship deserves more airtime than a freshman course you liked. Prioritize ruthlessly.

**No connecting thread.** If the interviewer can't figure out why you did each thing, your story feels unfocused. This is especially important for non-traditional backgrounds — you need to work harder to show the thread.

**Being passive about your progression.** "I got an internship at X" is weaker than "I sought out an internship at X because I wanted Y." Agency matters. Show that you made deliberate choices.

## For students with non-traditional backgrounds

If your background includes research, entrepreneurship, military service, or something else that isn't a straight line to banking — that's not a liability unless you make it one. The key is to find the thread:

- Research → "working with imperfect data to find signal" → modeling and due diligence
- Entrepreneurship → "commercial judgment and working across functions" → understanding what drives business value
- A different industry → "I wanted to see how the client side worked before moving to the advisory side"

The thread doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be honest and specific.

## Practice drill

Write your four beats on a notecard. Time yourself at 90 seconds. Do it three times out loud — not mumbling, actually speaking at interview volume and pace. On the third run, see if you can drop the notecard and deliver it from memory. If any part sounds robotic, that's the part to rework in your own words.
