## Why the words matter as much as the answer

Interviewers can't see your model of the problem — they can only hear it. Two candidates can land on the same final number, but the one who narrates a clear path gets the offer and the one who goes quiet gets a "strong technicals, weak communication" note. In IB interviews, the answer is the artifact; the reasoning is the actual work sample. If you only speak once you're sure, the interviewer has no data until the very end, and any wrong turn along the way looks like you never had a plan at all.

Thinking out loud is a skill you practice, not a personality trait. It has a structure.

## Structure before you answer

The instinct under pressure is to start talking immediately to fill the silence. Resist it. Take three to five seconds, say "let me think through this for a second," and actually organize your approach before your first substantive sentence. A structured opening does two things: it buys you real thinking time without looking lost, and it tells the interviewer you have a framework before you've committed to any numbers.

A reliable pattern for almost any technical prompt:

1. **Restate the question** in your own words, in one sentence.
2. **Name the framework** you'll use ("I'll walk through this using a basic DCF logic" or "I'll think about this as enterprise value bridging to equity value").
3. **List the inputs you need**, even if you already have them — this is where clarifying questions live.
4. **Work the math out loud**, narrating each step before you do it, not after.
5. **State the answer and sanity-check it** against something you already know (a rule of thumb, a comparable, a directional check).

This isn't padding. Interviewers who ask "walk me through a DCF" or "how would you value this company" are grading step 2 and step 3 as heavily as step 4.

## Clarifying questions: ask, don't assume

Many technical questions are deliberately underspecified — that's the point. If you're asked "what's the enterprise value of a company with $50 million in revenue," a candidate who plows ahead without asking about margins, growth, or comparable multiples has revealed that they don't know what actually drives value. A candidate who asks one sharp clarifying question ("do we have an EBITDA margin or a sector multiple to work with?") has revealed the opposite.

The rule: ask when the missing input changes your approach, not to stall. One good clarifying question is a signal of rigor. Five questions in a row before you've said anything substantive reads as stalling or fishing for the interviewer to do the problem for you. If the interviewer says "make an assumption," state it explicitly and move — don't keep pushing for the "real" number.

## Silence versus narrated thinking

Silence is ambiguous. When you go quiet mid-problem, the interviewer cannot tell whether you're doing careful mental math, whether you've frozen, or whether you've lost track of the question — and they will usually assume the worst of those three. Narrated thinking removes that ambiguity. It doesn't mean talking constantly; it means every few seconds of silence should be bracketed by a sentence that tells the listener what's happening in your head: "okay, so I need to get from EBITDA to free cash flow first" — pause to calculate — "that gives me about 14 million."

A useful habit: never let more than about ten seconds pass without saying something, even if it's just "give me one second to run this multiplication." That single sentence converts dead air into visible process.

## Worked example: staying narrated through a multi-step calculation

Say you're asked to estimate the implied share price from a simple set of assumptions: a company has $80 million in EBITDA, the interviewer tells you to use a 9.0x EV/EBITDA multiple, the company has $120 million in net debt, and there are 20 million shares outstanding.

Here's how you'd narrate it rather than just stating the final number:

"I'll start with enterprise value, then bridge down to equity value, then divide by share count.

Enterprise value: 80 million times 9.0x. That's 80 times 9, which is 720. So EV is $720 million.

To get to equity value, I subtract net debt, since net debt is value that belongs to lenders, not shareholders. 720 minus 120 is 600. So equity value is $600 million.

Now I divide by shares outstanding: 600 million divided by 20 million shares. 600 divided by 20 is 30. So implied share price is $30.00.

As a quick sanity check, EV of $720 million against $80 million of EBITDA is the 9x multiple I started with, so that's internally consistent, and a $600 million equity value on 20 million shares giving $30 a share also checks out arithmetically."

Notice what happened: every number appeared attached to a sentence explaining why it belonged there, and the check at the end wasn't decorative — it re-verified the multiple and the division rather than just repeating the answer.

## Recovering from errors gracefully

You will make arithmetic or logic errors in live interviews — everyone does. The error itself rarely sinks a candidate; how you handle discovering it does. Two things separate a graceful recovery from a spiral:

**Catch it yourself, out loud, without apologizing excessively.** If you notice a mistake, say "wait, let me correct that — I subtracted instead of added" and fix it in one clean sentence. One brief acknowledgment is enough. Repeated apologies ("I'm so sorry, I always mess this up") pull focus onto your anxiety instead of the fix and make the interviewer more nervous about you, not less.

**If the interviewer corrects you, treat it as new information, not an attack.** Say "got it, let me redo that with the right number" and continue. Don't argue for your wrong answer to save face, and don't go silent out of embarrassment — silence after a correction is the single worst response, because it reads as if the mistake broke your composure rather than just your math.

The recovery is actually a second opportunity to demonstrate structure: showing that you can isolate exactly which step broke, fix only that step, and carry the correct downstream logic forward is more impressive than never having erred at all.

### How interviewers probe this

Interviewers deliberately test composure in a few common ways: they'll give you an intentionally ambiguous prompt to see if you ask a clarifying question before diving in; they'll stay silent after your answer to see if you fill the space by second-guessing yourself; and they'll sometimes challenge a correct answer just to see whether you cave under pushback. The consistent tell they're looking for is whether your narration stays steady and specific under each of these — not whether you're ever perfectly correct.

### Common mistakes

- **Going silent for 20+ seconds** while doing mental math, leaving the interviewer no signal that you're on track.
- **Asking too many clarifying questions** before attempting the problem, which reads as avoidance rather than rigor.
- **Restating the final answer instead of sanity-checking it** — repeating "$30 a share" isn't a check, recomputing the multiple from your own output is.
- **Over-apologizing for small errors**, which draws more attention to the mistake than the correction itself deserves.
- **Abandoning your structure mid-problem** once you're deep in a calculation — keep narrating even in the arithmetic-heavy middle, not just at the open and close.
