## Why sector matters before you've even applied

Most undergrad recruiting happens through generalist analyst programs — you apply to "investment banking" at a bank, not to a specific group, and you get staffed into a coverage or product group after you're hired (sometimes after a bidding process at the end of your first summer). But the sector question shows up much earlier than staffing day. It shows up the moment you start networking, because "what group are you interested in?" is one of the first questions any banker asks you on a call, and your answer shapes everything that follows: who you talk to, what technical questions you get, and how coherent your story sounds.

Lateral recruiting and MBA recruiting work differently — there, you're often recruiting directly into a named group (Healthcare, TMT, FIG), and the sector choice is not optional. If your pre-MBA background is a mechanical engineer who spent three years doing corporate development at an auto-parts supplier, showing up to interviews saying you're open to "anything" is a red flag, not a strength. Groups want people who can point to a reason they belong there.

## What "coverage group" actually means

Banks organize bankers into two broad categories. **Coverage groups** (also called industry groups) specialize by sector — TMT, Healthcare, FIG, Energy, Industrials, Consumer & Retail, Real Estate — and their bankers build long-term relationships with companies in that sector, understand the sector's specific accounting and valuation quirks, and pitch deals across products (M&A, equity, debt) to their clients. **Product groups** specialize by transaction type instead — M&A, Leveraged Finance, Equity Capital Markets, Debt Capital Markets — and work across every sector, partnering with coverage bankers when a deal needs their expertise. A generalist analyst program at a bulge bracket bank staffs you across both; a boutique advisory shop might be built entirely around one or two sectors.

## How sector choice shapes networking and interviews

Once you tell a banker or an alumnus you're interested in a specific group, three things start happening:

**Your networking gets narrower and deeper.** You'll be pointed toward alumni who actually sit in that group, and you're expected to know the group's current deal activity — a recent transaction, a notable client, a piece of sector-specific news — by the time you get on a call. Vague enthusiasm ("I like tech") does not hold up against a specific one ("I've been following how streaming bundling deals are reshaping media coverage").

**Your interviews shift toward sector-specific technicals.** A generalist interview asks you to walk through a DCF. A sector-specific interview asks you to walk through a DCF *and* explain why a bank wouldn't use one for a pre-revenue biotech, or why Enterprise Value doesn't mean much for a commercial bank. Interviewers in a specific coverage group expect you to have absorbed the sector's key metrics and multiples — not at expert depth, but enough to speak fluently about what makes the sector different from a generic industrial company.

**Your story needs a throughline.** If you say you want Healthcare, and your resume shows a hospital internship or a bioengineering major, that's a coherent story that interviewers don't have to work to believe. If your background has no connection to the sector at all, you need a specific, credible reason ("my father runs a specialty pharmacy and I've always tracked reimbursement policy") rather than a generic one ("healthcare seems interesting").

## How locked-in you actually are

The honest answer: less than it feels like in the moment, but more than "not at all."

At the undergraduate full-time or summer analyst level, most banks let you express a group preference without treating it as a binding contract — plenty of analysts land in a group they never mentioned in an interview, and plenty of others switch groups within their first two years on the job. Interviewers mostly use your stated interest as a proxy for whether you've done your homework, not as a permanent career bet.

At the lateral or MBA level, the stakes go up. You're often applying to a specific group's headcount, competing against other candidates who are also targeting that exact seat, and your story needs to hold together across every conversation you have — recruiters compare notes, and telling one alumnus you want TMT and another you want Industrials reads as unfocused rather than flexible.

## How to actually pick one

Don't pick a sector because it sounds prestigious or because you read that it pays better (comp differences between coverage groups are usually small and driven more by deal flow in a given year than by sector). Pick based on three inputs, in order:

1. **Genuine background or interest** — a major, an internship, a hobby you can speak about credibly for ten minutes without notes.
2. **What you'd enjoy reading about at 11pm** — you'll be tracking this sector's news for years if you get the offer; test whether you actually find it interesting before you commit to the pitch.
3. **Practical fit with your target list** — some sectors have deeper benches at certain banks or in certain cities; a strong Energy story matters more in Houston than it does in a firm with no Energy franchise at all.

If you're genuinely torn between two sectors, it's fine to explore both in early networking calls — you don't owe anyone a final answer in September of your junior year. Just narrow down before you start interviewing seriously, because inconsistency between conversations is what actually damages you, not open-mindedness early on.

## Common mistakes

- **Naming a sector you can't discuss.** If you say "Consumer & Retail" and can't name a single multiple or metric bankers in that group actually use, you'll get caught in the first technical follow-up.
- **Picking a sector purely off league-table prestige.** TMT and Healthcare get more headlines, but a strong Industrials or FIG story from a candidate with real background beats a generic TMT pitch from someone with none.
- **Flip-flopping across conversations.** Telling different alumni different group preferences is one of the fastest ways to torch a referral — bankers talk to each other more than candidates expect.
- **Treating the choice as permanent.** Especially at the analyst level, groups are more fluid than they seem from the outside — don't let fear of "locking in" stop you from stating a clear preference now.
