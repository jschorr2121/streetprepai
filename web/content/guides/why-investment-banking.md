## The question behind the question

When an interviewer asks "Why investment banking?", they're really asking three things at once:

1. **Do you know what the job actually is?** Or are you chasing a label?
2. **Are your reasons durable?** Will you still believe them at 1am on a Tuesday in your sixth month?
3. **Will you fit this team?** The answer reveals how you think and what you value.

A great answer addresses all three without you announcing that's what you're doing. A weak answer hits the first in a generic way and skips the rest.

## What doesn't work

These answers will get you cut — because every candidate uses them:

- "I love finance." (*What does that mean? Your classmates do too.*)
- "I want a steep learning curve." (*Every demanding job offers that. Why banking specifically?*)
- "I want to work on M&A deals." (*Which ones? Why M&A and not LBOs? Why advising and not investing?*)
- "The exit opportunities." (*Never lead with this. Interviewers can tell and it signals you don't want the job itself.*)

## The three-layer answer that works

Build your answer in three layers, roughly 20 seconds each.

### Layer 1: A specific origin story

Start with one concrete experience — a class, a conversation, an internship moment — that first made you interested in the work. It doesn't need to be dramatic. It needs to be **specific and true**.

> "In my corporate finance class sophomore year, we worked through the Dell-EMC merger. What hooked me wasn't the headline — it was realizing that behind the $67B number were hundreds of judgment calls about synergies, debt capacity, accretion math. I wanted to understand who makes those calls."

The origin story does two things: it proves you didn't just pick banking because your roommate did, and it gives the interviewer a hook to ask follow-ups (an interviewer's favorite moment).

### Layer 2: What specifically about the work

Next, name 2-3 things about the *day-to-day work of an analyst* that genuinely excite you. Not the abstract idea of banking — what analysts actually do.

Strong material here:
- Modeling deals across industries (analytical variety)
- The direct client exposure early-career (learning curve)
- Working in a small deal team where your work lands in the memo that goes to the board (ownership)
- The iteration between judgment and quantitative work (not pure math, not pure sales)

Example:
> "I like that it's a job where the same week I might be building an LBO for a private equity client and preparing a board presentation for a CEO who's deciding whether to sell. The variety keeps me sharp, but every output has to be precise. That combination — broad exposure, high rigor — is rare."

### Layer 3: Why you specifically

Close with what makes you a good fit for that work, referencing something from your actual background. This is where behavioral and technical overlap.

> "The research assistant work I did in the economics department was the closest I've come to that kind of work — one week was trade-policy data, the next was building a clean panel dataset for a regression. I liked being thrown into new domains. Banking feels like the professional version of that, at a higher stakes."

## How to tailor it by firm

Your structure stays the same. What changes is what you emphasize:

- **Bulge bracket (GS, MS, JPM):** emphasize scale, global platform, brand of the franchise. Bonus if you can name a specific recent deal their group advised on.
- **Elite boutique (Evercore, Lazard, Centerview, PJT):** emphasize pure advisory, lean deal teams, direct senior contact. Know the firm's culture (Evercore is entrepreneurial, Centerview is small and partner-driven, PJT is founder-led, etc.).
- **Middle market (Jefferies, HL, Harris Williams):** emphasize the sponsor-side work, the scale of the private M&A market they operate in, and that they run real processes. Avoid the mistake of treating MM as a "safety" — the best MM firms are a first-choice job for thoughtful analysts.

## The delivery matters

This isn't a monologue. Deliver the three layers with pauses between them — it reads as thoughtful, not rehearsed. Make eye contact (or look at the camera). Slow down on the specific details in Layer 1; they're your evidence.

If the interviewer jumps in during Layer 2 with a follow-up like "What do you mean by modeling variety?" — that's good. You've given them a hook. Answer the follow-up, then loop back to where you were.

## Practice drill

Write your three layers on a notecard today. Say the full answer out loud three times, no script. Then record yourself once. Listen back: does Layer 1 sound specific, or could any student have said it? If the answer is "any student," you have more work to do — dig for the real moment that made you interested. It's there.
