## What a league table actually is

A league table is a ranking of banks by their share of a defined market over a defined period — most commonly M&A advisory, equity capital markets, or debt capital markets, cut by region, sector, or deal size. Data providers (Refinitiv/LSEG and Dealogic are the two most cited) compile these from disclosed deal information and publish quarterly and annual rankings that banks then use in pitch books and press releases.

The core idea is simple: count something related to deal activity, sum it by bank, rank the banks. The complexity — and the part worth understanding before you cite one in an interview — is in what exactly gets counted.

## What gets measured, and why it varies

Two banks can both claim to be "number one in healthcare M&A" in the same year and both be telling the truth, because they're measuring different things. The main variables:

### Deal count vs. deal value

A table can rank banks by the number of deals they advised on, or by the total dollar value of those deals. A boutique that runs a high volume of smaller deals can top a deal-count table while a bulge bracket advising on a handful of mega-deals tops the value table for the same period. Neither is "more correct" — they're answering different questions (breadth of activity vs. scale of activity).

### Credit allocation on multi-advisor deals

Most large deals have more than one advisor per side, sometimes three or four. Data providers have to decide how to split credit. Some tables give full deal value credit to every advisor on the deal (which inflates the total value across the table beyond the actual sum of deal values); others split credit proportionally. This is the single biggest source of "how can these two tables disagree so much" confusion, and it's worth knowing exists even if you never need to explain the exact methodology in an interview.

### Announced vs. completed deals

A deal can be announced and later fall through — blocked by regulators, terminated by the parties, or simply abandoned. Some league tables count deals at announcement; others only count them once they close. A firm's ranking can shift meaningfully between the announced-deal table and the completed-deal table if it had one large deal that didn't make it to closing.

### Time window and cut

Rankings get sliced by trailing twelve months, calendar year, or quarter, and further sliced by geography (global, Americas, EMEA), sector (healthcare, TMT, energy), and deal-size bracket (all deals, deals over $500 million, middle-market deals under $500 million). A firm can legitimately be "top three" in a narrow enough cut almost every year — which is exactly why nearly every bank's marketing material can cite some league table position.

## The gaming problem

None of the above is fraud — it's methodology choice, disclosed if you dig into the provider's footnotes. But it means league table claims in marketing materials are optimized, not neutral. A firm will naturally choose to publicize the cut where it ranks best: the specific sector, size bracket, or region where its number looks strongest, even if its overall market share is unremarkable.

There's also a more direct incentive problem: because banks care about their league table position for recruiting and client-pitching purposes, there's long-documented pressure to get credited on deals even in a minor advisory role, since almost any credited role can count toward table position depending on the provider's rules. A "why us" answer that treats a league table number as an unimpeachable fact ("you're the #1 bank in the world") is a small tell that you haven't looked past the marketing slide.

## A worked example of how rankings can diverge

Suppose in a given year, three banks work on healthcare M&A deals with the following (illustrative) profile:

- **Bank A** advises on 40 deals, all under $200 million, totaling $3.2 billion in aggregate deal value.
- **Bank B** advises on 6 deals, including two mega-deals over $5 billion each, totaling $14 billion in aggregate deal value.
- **Bank C** advises on 18 deals, a mix of sizes, totaling $6.5 billion in aggregate deal value, but was a co-advisor (not sole advisor) on 10 of those 18.

By **deal count**, Bank A ranks first with 40 deals.

By **deal value** with full credit given to every advisor on a deal, Bank B ranks first at $14 billion, well ahead of Bank C's $6.5 billion and Bank A's $3.2 billion.

By **deal value with proportional credit** for co-advised deals, Bank C's number shrinks — if we estimate it got roughly half credit on the 10 co-advised deals, its adjusted total drops from $6.5 billion toward something closer to $4.5–5 billion, moving it closer to Bank A rather than clearly ahead of it.

So depending on which methodology a marketing deck uses, you could see any of the three banks described as "#1 in healthcare M&A" for the same year, each technically accurate under its chosen measure. That's not evidence of dishonesty — it's evidence that "we're #1" is meaningless without stating what it's #1 *in*.

## How to reference a league table credibly in an interview

The goal isn't to recite methodology like an analyst at the data provider — it's to use the number the way someone who understands its limits would use it. A few rules that keep you credible:

1. **Always attach a qualifier.** Not "they're the #1 bank in M&A," but "they've ranked in the top five in healthcare M&A by deal value over the last two years." Specificity signals you looked past the headline.
2. **Cite the source when you can.** "Based on Refinitiv's rankings" or "per the Dealogic league tables" takes two seconds to say and immediately signals the number isn't just something you saw on the firm's own website.
3. **Don't lean on it as your only evidence.** A league table position is a good supporting fact in a "why us" answer, not the whole answer. Pair it with something from your own research or networking — see the [firm research guide](/guide/how-to-research-a-firm) for how those fit together.
4. **Know roughly where the firm you're interviewing with actually ranks**, in the group you're recruiting for, not just firm-wide. If you're interviewing for a TMT group, the firm's overall M&A rank matters less than its TMT-specific rank.

## How interviewers probe this

- **"What does that ranking actually measure?"** — A blunt test of whether you understand deal count vs. deal value, or announced vs. completed, or just repeated a number.
- **"Where do you see that data?"** — Tests whether you have a real source or read it off the firm's own pitch deck without independent verification.
- **"Does that rank hold across sectors, or is that one specific cut?"** — Tests whether you know the number you're citing is broad or narrow.

## Common mistakes

- **Treating a single ranking as the full picture.** A firm's overall rank and its rank in your specific group of interest can diverge a lot; cite the one that's actually relevant to the interview you're in.
- **Repeating a number without a source.** "I heard you're number one" sounds like secondhand marketing. "Per [provider]'s most recent league table" sounds like you did the work.
- **Confusing deal count with deal value rankings**, or citing one as if it proves the other. They measure different things and a firm can lead in one while trailing in the other.
- **Over-indexing on the number instead of the deals behind it.** A league table position is a summary statistic. Knowing one or two actual deals that contributed to it (see the [firm research guide](/guide/how-to-research-a-firm)) makes your answer far more durable under follow-up.

## Practice this

Find the most recent annual league table for the product group and sector you're recruiting into — a quick search for "[sector] M&A league table [year]" will usually surface press coverage citing Refinitiv or Dealogic data even without a paid data terminal. Note where your target firm ranks, and note whether the coverage specifies deal count or deal value. Then practice saying the fact out loud with a proper qualifier: "they ranked top five in [sector] M&A by deal value last year, per [source]" — not "they're the best in the industry."
