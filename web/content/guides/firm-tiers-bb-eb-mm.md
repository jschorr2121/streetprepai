## Why tier matters before you build a target list

"Investment bank" is not one job. A bulge bracket analyst, an elite boutique analyst, and a middle-market analyst can have meaningfully different day-to-day work, deal sizes, hours, and career paths, even though all three carry the "investment banking analyst" title on LinkedIn. Understanding the three tiers — what they are, how they differ, and what each implies for your exits — is what turns a random list of bank names into a target list with a strategy behind it.

## Bulge brackets (BBs)

Bulge brackets are the largest global investment banks: full-service institutions with M&A advisory, capital markets (equity and debt underwriting), sales and trading, and often asset management and commercial banking under one roof. Think firms with balance-sheet lending capability alongside advisory — that's the defining feature. Because they can offer financing alongside advice, BBs tend to win the largest, most complex deals: multibillion-dollar mergers, large IPOs, cross-border transactions with financing components.

What that means for an analyst:

- **Deal flow**: high volume, and deals often larger and more complex, but with more people on each deal — you may be one of three or four analysts on a live transaction, meaning less end-to-end ownership of any single piece.
- **Pay**: strong base and bonus, generally standardized across the BB tier for a given class year.
- **Culture and structure**: larger analyst classes (dozens to low hundreds per bank in a given city), more formal training programs, more bureaucracy, and often (though not always) a generalist or coverage-group placement structure rather than direct group placement.
- **Exits**: brand recognition opens doors broadly — private equity, hedge funds, corporate development, and other buy-side seats all recognize BB names, which is valuable if you're not yet sure what you want to do after two years.

## Elite boutiques (EBs)

Elite boutiques are pure advisory firms — no balance sheet, no sales and trading, no lending. They compete with bulge brackets for the largest, highest-profile M&A mandates but do so purely on advice, which is part of their pitch to clients: no conflicts of interest from also wanting to underwrite the client's debt.

What that means for an analyst:

- **Deal flow**: smaller analyst classes mean more deal exposure and more responsibility per analyst — you're likely to be the only or one of two analysts on a deal, which means direct modeling and client-material ownership much earlier than at a BB.
- **Pay**: EB analyst compensation is typically at or above BB levels, often the highest in the industry for a first- or second-year analyst.
- **Culture and structure**: smaller, denser analyst classes; hours can be as intense or more intense than a BB given the leaner staffing; direct placement into specific groups (there usually isn't a generalist rotation).
- **Exits**: extremely strong for private equity recruiting specifically, because EB deal experience skews toward large, high-profile M&A — exactly what megafund and upper-middle-market PE firms want to see. Slightly narrower brand recognition outside of finance compared to a BB name.

## Middle-market banks (MMs)

Middle-market banks advise on smaller transactions — roughly deals in the tens of millions to low billions of dollars in value, though the exact range varies by firm and shifts over time. Some MM banks are regional, some are national, some specialize by sector (healthcare, industrials, technology) or by transaction type (sell-side M&A for founder-owned businesses, for example).

What that means for an analyst:

- **Deal flow**: the smallest teams of the three tiers, often just one analyst and one associate on a deal alongside a VP or MD — maximum ownership and exposure to the full deal lifecycle, from pitching through closing.
- **Pay**: generally below BB and EB analyst comp, though top MM shops pay competitively and the gap has narrowed in recent years.
- **Culture and structure**: smallest analyst classes, closest to the deal team's senior bankers, often the most direct mentorship of the three tiers.
- **Exits**: strong for smaller and middle-market private equity funds, and for candidates targeting a specific geography or sector where the MM bank has a strong regional reputation. Weaker than BB/EB for megafund PE or for candidates who want maximum brand-name optionality later.

## Worked comparison: three analysts, one hypothetical deal

Imagine a $2 billion sale of a mid-sized industrials company. At a bulge bracket running the sell-side process alongside a financing package for the buyer, the deal team might include four analysts split across the M&A and financing workstreams — you'd own a slice of the model, not the whole thing. At an elite boutique retained purely for sell-side advice on the same deal, you might be the single analyst building the entire model and drafting the entire management presentation, working directly with a VP and the MD who sourced the relationship. At a middle-market bank advising the same seller's smaller regional competitor on a $150 million sale, you'd likely be the only analyst on the deal from pitch to close, doing everything from comps to closing binders.

None of these is objectively "better" — they trade off deal size and brand against ownership and hours-per-deal-learned. The choice should follow from what you want two years from now: broad optionality (lean BB), maximum PE recruiting leverage on the largest deals (lean EB), or maximum hands-on ownership and a specific sector/geography niche (lean MM).

## Building your target list around tier

A sensible target list spans tiers rather than clustering entirely at the top. A common structure: 2-3 bulge brackets, 2-3 elite boutiques, and 2-4 middle-market or regional banks, weighted toward whichever tier best matches your priorities (brand optionality vs. deal ownership vs. sector fit). Applying only to the five most famous names in finance is the single most common target-list mistake — it maximizes rejection risk without improving your odds at any one firm. ## Common mistakes

- **Assuming EB always means better exits than BB.** It depends on the exit — EB experience is exceptional for PE megafunds specifically; BB brand recognition is broader for candidates still deciding between paths.
- **Ignoring middle-market banks entirely.** A strong regional MM bank with real deal flow often gives a better analyst experience — and a realistic shot at an offer — than reaching for a top-five BB with no fit.
- **Confusing firm size with deal size.** Some boutiques are enormous by headcount but do only small deals; some lean MM shops occasionally land billion-dollar mandates. Research the actual deal sheet, not just the firm's name recognition.

## How interviewers probe this

Interviewers at each tier want to hear that you understand what makes their firm distinct — not a generic "I want to do M&A" answer. At a BB, be ready to explain why you want the platform and scale. At an EB, expect to be asked directly why you'd choose a pure advisory shop over a full-service bank — have a real answer about ownership and deal focus, not just prestige. At an MM, expect questions probing whether you actually want the sector or geography focus, since MM banks are wary of candidates using them as a stepping stone to a BB.
