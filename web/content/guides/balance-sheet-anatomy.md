## A snapshot, not a period

The income statement measures activity over a stretch of time. The balance sheet is different — it's a photograph of the company's financial position at a single instant, usually the last day of a quarter or fiscal year. Every account on it answers "what do we own, and how did we pay for it?" as of that exact date.

The equation that defines it never breaks:

> Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

Think about your own finances. If you have $30K in a savings account, $50K in a brokerage account, and a $500K house, your assets total $580K. You got that $580K from somewhere — say $380K from a mortgage (a liability) and $200K of your own after-tax savings (equity). Assets and liabilities-plus-equity aren't coincidentally equal; the equation is definitional. Every dollar of assets has to be traceable to either something you owe or something you own outright.

## Assets, liabilities, and equity, defined

**An asset** is anything that will bring in cash, directly or indirectly, in the future — cash itself, a customer IOU, a factory that makes products to sell.

**A liability** is anything that will cost the company cash in the future — usually an obligation to an outside party: a supplier owed money, a bank that lent money.

**Equity** functions like a liability in that it also represents a source of funding, but the claim belongs to the company's own owners rather than an external creditor. It's the residual: what's left over for shareholders after every liability is subtracted from every asset.

## The full glossary

### Current assets (convert to cash within a year)

- **Cash & equivalents** — cash on hand and in bank accounts, plus anything essentially as liquid.
- **Short-term investments** — securities held for less than a year, slightly less liquid than cash.
- **Accounts receivable (AR)** — revenue the company has already recognized on the income statement but hasn't collected in cash yet. An IOU from a customer.
- **Prepaid expenses** — cash the company has already paid out for something it hasn't consumed yet (a year of prepaid insurance, for example). It's an asset because the company is owed the future benefit.
- **Inventory** — raw materials, work-in-process, and finished goods a company holds to manufacture or sell.

### Long-term assets

- **PP&E (property, plant & equipment)** — factories, land, buildings, machinery, anything expected to generate value for more than a year. Shown net of accumulated depreciation.
- **Goodwill** — the premium a company paid over a target's book value of equity in an acquisition. It doesn't amortize under US GAAP; it's tested annually for impairment instead.
- **Other intangible assets** — patents, trademarks, customer relationships, usually acquired rather than built in-house. Unlike goodwill, these do amortize over their useful lives.
- **Long-term investments** — securities the company intends to hold for more than a year.

### Current liabilities (due within a year)

- **Accounts payable (AP)** — the mirror image of AR: an expense the company has already recorded on the income statement but hasn't paid in cash yet. Typically tied to one-off invoices, like a specific vendor bill.
- **Accrued expenses** — the same idea as AP (recorded, not yet paid) but for recurring items without a discrete invoice — payroll, utilities, rent that builds up between payment dates.
- **Deferred revenue** — cash the company has already collected from a customer for a product or service it hasn't delivered yet. It's a liability because the company owes the customer that delivery (or a refund).
- **Short-term debt / the revolver** — debt due within a year, or a revolving credit facility a company draws on like a corporate credit card.

### Long-term liabilities

- **Long-term debt** — bonds or term loans due more than a year out, structurally similar to a mortgage.
- **Deferred tax liability** — the company has paid less in cash taxes than the tax expense shown on its income statement, and it will owe the difference eventually. (Full treatment in [Advanced accounting](/guide/advanced-accounting).)
- **Long-term deferred revenue** — the portion of a multi-year prepaid contract that won't be recognized as revenue within the next twelve months.

### Shareholders' equity

- **Common stock & additional paid-in capital (APIC)** — the value of shares at the moment the company issued them. This doesn't move when the stock price moves afterward.
- **Treasury stock** — a negative equity account tracking shares the company has bought back from investors; it reduces total equity.
- **Retained earnings** — cumulative after-tax profit the company has kept rather than paid out as dividends. This is the account net income flows into every period.
- **Accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI)** — a catch-all for items like foreign currency translation adjustments and unrealized gains/losses on certain securities that haven't hit the income statement yet.

## Why "current" matters

Splitting each side into current and long-term buckets isn't cosmetic — it tells you how much cash the company needs to keep flowing through day-to-day operations versus how much is tied up for years. That split is exactly what feeds working capital, covered in full in [Working capital & operational items](/guide/working-capital-and-operational-items).

## A worked mini balance sheet

Suppose a company has $40M cash, $25M AR, $15M inventory, and $220M net PP&E. On the other side it has $18M AP, $12M accrued expenses, $95M long-term debt, and everything else is equity.

- Total assets: $40M + $25M + $15M + $220M = $300M
- Total liabilities: $18M + $12M + $95M = $125M
- Shareholders' equity, by definition: $300M − $125M = **$175M**

Now suppose the company draws $10M more on its revolver and immediately spends it on inventory. Inventory rises $10M on the asset side, and the revolver (short-term debt) rises $10M on the liability side. Assets go from $300M to $310M, liabilities go from $125M to $135M, and equity is untouched at $175M — the equation still holds, because it has to.

## Common mistakes

- **Mixing up AR and AP.** AR is money owed *to* the company (an asset); AP is money the company owes (a liability). Say the wrong one under pressure and you'll immediately sound unsure of the fundamentals.
- **Forgetting goodwill doesn't amortize.** Confusing it with other intangibles (which do amortize) is one of the most common slips in a technical screen.
- **Calling deferred revenue a bad sign.** It's cash already in the bank for work not yet done — often a sign of a healthy, prepaid business model (software subscriptions are the classic example), not distress.
- **Treating "current" as "unimportant."** Current assets and liabilities are exactly what working capital math is built from — they're the most heavily tested category in accounting drills, not a footnote.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect to be handed an unfamiliar line item and asked to classify it: is it an asset or a liability, current or long-term, and why? The fastest way to answer any of these is to go back to the definitions — does it bring in future cash (asset) or cost future cash (liability) — rather than trying to recall a memorized list. You'll also get "what's the difference between accounts payable and accrued expenses?" constantly; the clean answer is that AP ties to a specific invoice from a vendor, while accrued expenses are recurring obligations that build up without one.
