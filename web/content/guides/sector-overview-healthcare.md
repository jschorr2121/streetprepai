## What healthcare actually covers

Healthcare is one of the broadest coverage groups, and the four subsectors inside it behave differently enough that a banker in one rarely does much work in another. **Pharmaceuticals** covers large, diversified drugmakers with approved products generating steady revenue. **Biotech** covers earlier-stage companies, often with no approved products yet, whose entire value rests on clinical trial outcomes. **Healthcare services** covers hospitals, physician groups, home health, and insurers — businesses that behave more like traditional operating companies. **Medtech** (medical devices and diagnostics) covers companies that manufacture and sell physical products, closer in structure to an industrials business than to a pharma company.

Interviewers rarely expect you to be equally fluent in all four. If you're targeting Healthcare broadly, know the differences well enough to speak intelligently about each, but expect to go deeper on whichever subsector matches your background or your stated interest.

## What deals look like

Large pharma companies frequently acquire smaller biotech companies specifically to buy a late-stage drug candidate or a newly approved product — this is often more efficient than running their own research from scratch, since a large share of drug development risk gets resolved (or killed) before a big acquirer needs to step in. Healthcare services sees consolidation activity — hospital systems and physician practice groups merging to gain negotiating leverage with insurers and scale on fixed costs. Medtech deals resemble industrials M&A: a larger device company buying a smaller one for a specific product line or distribution channel. Biotech itself sees relatively little M&A among biotechs themselves and a lot of licensing and partnership deals, where a larger company pays a smaller one for rights to develop or commercialize a drug.

## Key metrics differ by subsector

For pharma, healthcare services, and medtech, standard valuation multiples apply — EV/EBITDA and EV/Revenue work the same way they do for any established operating company. The differences show up in operational metrics used alongside those multiples:

- **Healthcare services**: revenue or EBITDA per bed (hospitals), same-store visit growth (physician groups), and reimbursement mix (the split between government payors like Medicare/Medicaid and private insurance, since reimbursement rates differ sharply and payor mix shifts can swing margins).
- **Medtech**: unit volumes, average selling price per device, and — like any hardware business — gross margin trends as a signal of pricing power.
- **Pharma**: pipeline value by therapeutic area, patent expiration timing (a "patent cliff" is when a blockbuster drug loses exclusivity and generic competition collapses its margins), and revenue concentration in a small number of products.

Biotech is where valuation departs from anything you'd use elsewhere. A clinical-stage biotech frequently has zero revenue and years of losses ahead of it, so EV/EBITDA and EV/Revenue are meaningless. Instead, bankers build a **risk-adjusted, far-in-the-future DCF**: project the drug's potential peak sales if it's approved, apply a probability of success at each remaining clinical trial phase, and discount the resulting expected cash flows back at a high discount rate that reflects the real risk of failure.

**Worked example.** Say a biotech has one drug candidate in Phase 2 trials targeting a niche autoimmune condition. Your model estimates that if approved, the drug reaches peak annual sales of $800M in year 8 after launch, with an 80% margin once at scale. Industry base rates put the probability of a Phase 2 drug eventually reaching approval at roughly 15%. You'd apply that 15% probability to your projected cash flows in every future year (not just at the end), because the drug could fail at any remaining trial stage. If the risk-adjusted, discounted value of those future cash flows comes out to $340M, and the company holds $60M of net cash with no other assets, your implied enterprise value is around $340M and implied equity value around $400M. Small changes to the probability of success swing this number enormously — moving the assumption from 15% to 20% raises the risk-adjusted value by a third, which is why biotech valuation conversations spend so much time debating clinical trial data rather than financial assumptions.

## Terminology to know cold

- **Phase 1 / 2 / 3 trials** — the sequential clinical trial stages a drug must pass before FDA approval; Phase 1 tests safety in a small group, Phase 2 tests efficacy and dosing, Phase 3 tests efficacy at scale against a control.
- **FDA approval / PDUFA date** — the regulatory decision point; a PDUFA date is the FDA's target date for deciding on a drug application, and it's a major stock-moving event.
- **Patent cliff** — when a drug's patent protection expires and generic competitors can enter, typically collapsing revenue on that product within a couple of years.
- **Payor mix** — the split of a healthcare provider's revenue between government payors (Medicare, Medicaid) and private insurers; shifts in this mix directly affect margins because reimbursement rates differ.
- **Orphan drug** — a drug developed for a rare disease, often eligible for expedited regulatory review and market exclusivity incentives.
- **Biosimilar** — the biotech equivalent of a generic drug, used once a biologic drug's exclusivity expires.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect "how would you value a pre-revenue biotech company?" as a near-guaranteed question if you're targeting this group — the answer is the risk-adjusted DCF, and you should be ready to explain why probability-of-success assumptions dominate the output more than any other input. You may also get asked to explain payor mix or why hospital M&A happens (scale against insurers, fixed-cost leverage). Know at least one real regulatory driver currently affecting the subsector you're targeting — a recent FDA policy change, a drug pricing reform, or a notable approval — since this group expects you to track regulation the way TMT candidates track product launches.

## Common mistakes

- **Applying EV/EBITDA to a clinical-stage biotech.** There's no EBITDA to speak of; naming the risk-adjusted DCF instead is the single most important signal you understand this subsector.
- **Treating "healthcare" as one homogenous sector in an interview.** Conflating pharma economics with hospital economics is an easy way to look unprepared to a healthcare-focused interviewer.
- **Ignoring regulatory risk in valuation discussions.** In this sector, regulatory and clinical-trial risk is often the single biggest driver of value — more than growth rate or margin assumptions.
