## Why this question shows up

"What would make a good LBO candidate?" is a favorite because it tests whether you actually understand what a leveraged buyout requires, rather than just memorizing the mechanics. The honest answer is a short checklist, and one item on it matters far more than the rest.

Before you list traits, anchor on the underlying logic: a sponsor is buying a company mostly with borrowed money, and that debt has to get serviced out of the company's own cash flow. Every criterion below is really just an extension of that one constraint.

## The checklist, in priority order

### 1. Price — the single biggest lever

Almost any company can be a workable LBO candidate at the right price. Even a wonderful business is a bad deal if you overpay, because a high entry multiple makes it nearly impossible to generate an acceptable return without unrealistic assumptions about growth or multiple expansion. Conversely, a mediocre business bought cheap can still produce solid returns. If you remember only one thing from this list, remember that price discipline dominates every other factor.

### 2. Stable, predictable cash flow

This is the most important *qualitative* trait, because it directly determines whether the company can service debt. A business whose cash flow could plausibly drop 40–50% in a bad year is dangerous to lever up — a covenant breach or missed interest payment can trigger a default long before the sponsor gets anywhere near an exit. This is why commodity-driven businesses (oil and gas producers, miners, undifferentiated agricultural processors) are structurally unattractive LBO targets: their cash flows swing with prices they don't control.

Growth is a nice-to-have, not a requirement. A slow-growing, highly stable business with a sensible entry price and enough free cash flow to pay down debt can still deliver a strong IRR. A fast-growing but volatile business is often harder to finance and harder to model with any confidence.

### 3. Low capital intensity

Every dollar the company must spend on capex is a dollar that can't go toward debt repayment. Businesses with light ongoing reinvestment needs — software, branded consumer products, asset-light services — convert more of their EBITDA into cash available to lenders and, eventually, equity holders.

### 4. Room to improve margins or cut costs

Sponsors like targets where there's an identifiable operational lever: consolidating vendor contracts, rationalizing SG&A, renegotiating supplier terms, or professionalizing a founder-run business. This creates a second source of return beyond simply riding the market.

### 5. A strong, retainable management team

Debt schedules and forecasts are only as good as the people executing the plan. Sponsors weight management quality heavily, and one useful signal is how much of their own equity management is willing to roll into the deal — a team that keeps meaningful skin in the game is more credibly aligned with the sponsor's return.

### 6. A collateral base and a fragmented, consolidatable industry

Hard assets (real estate, equipment, receivables) give lenders comfort and can lower the cost of debt. A fragmented industry — many small competitors, no dominant player — also gives the sponsor room to pursue add-on acquisitions and build the platform bigger during the hold.

## Why the target's existing capital structure barely matters

This trips up a lot of candidates. Intuitively you'd think a heavily indebted target is automatically a worse LBO candidate — but in most deals, the company's *existing* capital structure gets wiped out at close. New debt replaces old debt; the sponsor's equity check plus newly raised debt funds the purchase, and pre-deal leverage disappears from the picture. What actually persists into the new deal is the company's ability to generate cash going forward, not how it happened to be financed before the sponsor showed up.

There are two second-order exceptions worth naming in an interview: if the existing debt carries a large prepayment penalty, that raises the effective purchase price and hurts the deal. And if the company has a long, clean track record of borrowing and repaying on schedule, lenders may be more willing to extend the new debt package — making the deal easier (and possibly cheaper) to finance. Both are financing-friction effects, not fundamental business-quality effects.

## Worked example: ranking three candidates

Say you're screening three consumer-facing retailers for a hypothetical buyout:

**Northgate Outfitters** (apparel): EBITDA of $120M on a 9% margin, free cash flow that grew steadily from $60M to $95M over three years, trading at 7.5x EV/EBITDA.

**Ridgeline Athletics** (activewear): EBITDA of $85M on a 5% margin, free cash flow that swung from a $10M deficit to $140M and back down to $55M over three years, trading at 16x EV/EBITDA.

**Coastal Grocer** (discount grocery): EBITDA of $140M on a 4% margin, free cash flow that declined from $110M to $70M over three years, trading at 8.5x EV/EBITDA.

Walk through it the way an interviewer wants to hear it: Northgate has the most stable, growing FCF and a reasonable entry multiple — it's the strongest candidate. Ridgeline's cash flow is wildly erratic and it's priced at more than double Northgate's multiple, so even though the underlying brand may be attractive, the volatility and the price make it the weakest candidate — you'd need aggressive multiple expansion just to hit a base-case return. Coastal Grocer sits in the middle: its FCF trend is declining, which is a real concern, but it's cheap and grocery cash flows tend to be structurally stable even when margins compress, so it's a workable candidate at that price, just not as clean as Northgate.

Ranking: **Northgate Outfitters > Coastal Grocer > Ridgeline Athletics.** Notice that the ranking leans on stability and price far more than on which company is "better" as a business — that's the point of the exercise.

## Common mistakes

- **Leading with growth.** Candidates often say "I'd look for a fast-growing company" — that's backwards. Growth is welcome but secondary to stability and price.
- **Forgetting that price can override every other factor.** A perfect business at 20x EBITDA is a worse LBO candidate than a decent business at 7x.
- **Assuming a highly leveraged target is automatically disqualified.** Say explicitly that existing capital structure is mostly wiped out and largely irrelevant, with the prepayment-penalty and lender-track-record caveats.
- **Not connecting each trait back to debt service.** Every criterion on this list exists because the company has to pay interest and principal — say that out loud rather than reciting a memorized list.
