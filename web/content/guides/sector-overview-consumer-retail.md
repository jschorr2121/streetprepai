## What Consumer & Retail actually covers

Consumer & Retail spans a wide range of business models that all share one trait: they sell directly or near-directly to individual consumers. **Retail** covers brick-and-mortar and e-commerce chains selling apparel, general merchandise, and specialty goods. **Restaurants** covers quick-service and full-service dining, usually analyzed with a similar toolkit to retail because both are unit-based businesses. **Consumer products (CPG)** covers branded goods sold through retailers — food, beverage, household products, beauty — where the company itself typically doesn't operate stores. **E-commerce and direct-to-consumer (DTC)** covers companies selling primarily or entirely online, which increasingly blurs into TMT coverage for digitally-native brands.

## What deals look like

Retail and restaurant M&A frequently involves private equity buyers acquiring chains with stable, predictable cash flow and a clear path to adding new units — the classic "buy and scale the footprint" thesis. Strategic M&A in CPG tends to be large branded companies acquiring smaller, faster-growing challenger brands to plug a gap in their portfolio or to reach a demographic their existing brands don't. Consumer & Retail is also one of the sectors most exposed to distress and restructuring work, since thin margins and heavy fixed costs (store leases, in particular) mean a downturn in consumer spending can push weaker chains into financial trouble quickly — restructuring advisory is a meaningfully bigger part of this group's work than it is in most other sectors.

## Same-store sales: the core operating metric

The single most important metric in retail and restaurants is **same-store sales (SSS)**, also called comparable-store sales or "comps" — the year-over-year revenue growth of stores that have been open for at least a full year, excluding new store openings and closures. This isolates organic performance from growth that's purely a function of opening more locations.

Same-store sales matters because total revenue growth on its own is misleading — a chain can post 15% revenue growth almost entirely by opening new stores while its existing stores are actually losing customers. Same-store sales strips that out and answers the question investors actually care about: are the stores that already exist getting better or worse?

**Worked example.** Say a retail chain, HarborMart, reports total revenue growth of 12% for the year. It operated 400 stores at the start of the year and opened 60 new stores during the year, none of which are old enough to count toward the comp base. The 340 stores that were open the entire prior year generated $890M in revenue this year versus $860M last year. Same-store sales growth is ($890M − $860M) / $860M = 3.5%. That 3.5% — not the headline 12% — is the number that tells you whether the existing business is actually healthy; the rest of the 12% growth is coming entirely from new unit openings, which is a different (and less durable) growth story.

## Unit economics

Alongside same-store sales, retail and restaurant analysis leans on per-unit metrics that show whether individual locations are actually profitable to run:

- **Sales per square foot** — revenue divided by selling space, the standard productivity measure for physical retail.
- **Average unit volume (AUV)** — average annual revenue per store or restaurant location, commonly used in franchised concepts to gauge how attractive a unit is to franchisees.
- **Inventory turnover** — cost of goods sold divided by average inventory, measuring how efficiently a retailer moves product; low turnover is a red flag for markdown risk and cash tied up on shelves.
- **Payback period** — for a new store or restaurant, how long it takes the initial buildout investment to pay back in cash flow; a core input to any expansion thesis.

## Why lease-heavy names use EBITDAR

Retail and restaurant chains vary widely in how they finance their real estate — some own their stores outright, some lease everything, and most are a mix. Because rent expense sits above EBITDA on the income statement, two operationally identical chains can show very different EBITDA margins purely because one owns its real estate (no rent expense) and the other leases it (rent expense drags down EBITDA). That's not a real difference in operating performance — it's a financing choice.

**EBITDAR** (EBITDA plus Rent or Lease Expense) strips this distortion out, and the matching enterprise value multiple adds the value of operating leases back into EV as well, so both sides of the multiple are treated consistently. Under U.S. GAAP specifically, this adjustment matters because rental expense is presented on the income statement in a way that affects EBITDA; under IFRS, lease accounting rules mean EBITDAR and EBITDA often converge to the same number, so the adjustment matters less.

**Worked example.** Two restaurant chains have identical store-level operating performance:

- **Chain X** owns 80% of its real estate. Its EBITDA is $150M.
- **Chain Y** leases 100% of its real estate, paying $40M a year in rent that's already been deducted to get to its reported EBITDA of $110M.

A naive EV/EBITDA comparison makes Chain Y look structurally weaker. Adding back Chain Y's $40M of rent gets EBITDAR of $150M — now identical to Chain X's underlying performance. To keep the multiple consistent, you'd also add the value of Chain Y's operating leases to its enterprise value before computing EV/EBITDAR, so you're comparing apples to apples on both the numerator and the denominator.

## Terminology to know cold

- **Comps / same-store sales** — described above; the single most quoted metric in retail earnings calls.
- **Foot traffic** — the number of customers physically entering a store, a leading indicator that predates same-store sales in the reporting cycle.
- **Private label** — a retailer's own store brand, typically carrying higher margins than third-party branded products.
- **Omnichannel** — a retailer's integration of physical stores with e-commerce and mobile, now table stakes rather than a differentiator.
- **Markdown risk** — the risk that unsold inventory has to be discounted to move, directly eroding gross margin; a bigger concern in fashion and apparel than in staples.

## How interviewers probe this

Expect "why do we care about same-store sales instead of just total revenue growth?" as a standard question — the answer is isolating organic performance from the effect of opening new locations. You may also get "why would you use EBITDAR instead of EBITDA for a restaurant chain?" — normalizing away differences in how companies finance their real estate. Know a recent trend affecting your target subsector — a shift in consumer spending, a notable brand acquisition, or a restructuring — since this group expects current market awareness like most others.

## Common mistakes

- **Quoting total revenue growth as if it reflects underlying health.** Always ask whether growth is coming from same-store performance or from new unit openings — they tell very different stories.
- **Comparing EBITDA margins across owned-real-estate and leased-real-estate chains without adjusting.** This is the single most common error in retail comps and instantly signals unfamiliarity with the sector.
- **Ignoring inventory and working capital dynamics.** Retail businesses are often working-capital intensive around inventory cycles in a way that industrials and services businesses aren't; skipping this in a walkthrough is a gap interviewers notice.
