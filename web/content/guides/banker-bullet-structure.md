## Why resume bullets are different for banking

Investment banking is a field that selects heavily on the ability to communicate precisely and concisely. Your resume bullets are the first evidence an interviewer sees of whether you have that skill. A resume full of vague, passive, or inflated bullets signals that you either can't write crisp analytical prose or don't know what the job actually involves. Either is a problem.

The good news: banking resume bullets follow a clear structure. Once you learn it, rewriting a mediocre bullet into a strong one takes about three minutes.

## The framework: X, using Y, to achieve Z

Every strong banking bullet does three things:

- **X — What you did** (the action, specific and active)
- **Y — How you did it** (the method, tool, or skill used)
- **Z — What resulted** (the quantified or concrete outcome)

> "Built a three-statement operating model using Excel and public 10-K filings to analyze free cash flow dynamics for a target company in a live M&A process, enabling the deal team to identify a $15M working capital gap before closing."

That bullet is long, but let's look at what it contains: a specific action (built a model), a specific method (Excel, 10-K filings), a specific context (live M&A process), and a specific outcome (identified a $15M gap). Nothing in that bullet is vague.

## Before and after: five rewrites

### Example 1
**Before:** "Assisted with financial modeling for various deals."
**After:** "Supported analysis on four live M&A mandates by maintaining and updating precedent transactions databases and building merger model sensitivity tables in Excel, directly cited in two client presentations."

The before version says almost nothing. The after version tells the reader what you modeled, what deliverable you produced, and that it was used by the client.

### Example 2
**Before:** "Researched companies in the healthcare sector."
**After:** "Screened 40+ publicly traded healthcare services companies to build a comparable company universe for a sell-side process, using EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, and P/E multiples to identify the eight most defensible comps."

Specificity about the process (comps screening), the method (three named multiples), and the output (eight final comps) transforms a vague claim into proof of real analytical work.

### Example 3
**Before:** "Helped prepare presentations for clients."
**After:** "Drafted PowerPoint sections for three board presentations, including an acquisition rationale slide with deal structure, synergy waterfall, and pro forma accretion/dilution analysis, reviewed directly by the VP before delivery."

"Helped prepare" tells the reader nothing. The after version shows you know what goes in a board deck and that senior bankers reviewed your work.

### Example 4
**Before:** "Analyzed market trends in the technology sector for investment research."
**After:** "Conducted channel checks and synthesized earnings call transcripts from 12 enterprise software companies to develop a sector note on cloud infrastructure spending, resulting in a published research piece distributed to 200+ institutional clients."

The outcome (published research, 200+ clients) and the specific method (channel checks, earnings call transcripts) make this a verifiable, impressive bullet instead of a vague claim.

### Example 5
**Before:** "Worked on a capstone project to value a company."
**After:** "Led a three-person team to produce a full LBO analysis of a consumer products company in a semester-long finance course, projecting 5-year EBITDA growth, modeling a leveraged buyout at 7x entry and 9x exit, and presenting to a panel of practitioners for a top grade in the cohort."

Even coursework bullets can be strong if you include the specific analytical methods, your role, and the outcome.

## The rules for strong bullets

**Lead with a strong verb.** "Built," "analyzed," "modeled," "drafted," "led," "synthesized," "structured," "developed," "identified," "evaluated." Not: "Helped," "assisted," "participated in," "involved in."

**Quantify when you can.** Not always possible, but when it is — the number of companies screened, the deal size you worked on, the number of clients who received your output — use it. Numbers make bullets concrete and credible.

**Name the tool or method.** "Excel," "Bloomberg," "Capital IQ," "FactSet," "DCF," "WACC," "LBO model," "comparable company analysis." These signal fluency and are often searchable keywords.

**Include the output.** What did your work produce? A slide deck, a memo, a published report, a model used in a pitch, a recommendation the team accepted? The output is proof that your work was real.

**Keep it to one line.** Bullets that run two lines and contain three different ideas become hard to parse quickly. One action, one method, one outcome.

## What to do if your experience is thin

Students worry that their bullets are weak because they don't have impressive IB experience. Two things:

First, almost everyone at your stage has similar experience. The differentiation comes from how you write about what you've done, not from doing more impressive things.

Second, course projects, investment club work, research assistant positions, and entrepreneurial experience all have strong bullets hidden inside them. Look at what you actually did analytically — what data did you work with, what method did you apply, what was the output? That's your bullet.

## Practice this

Take your current resume and find the three weakest bullets — the ones that are vague, passive, or missing an outcome. For each one, spend five minutes extracting the X (what you actually did), Y (how you did it), and Z (what resulted). Rewrite each bullet using strong verb + specific method + concrete outcome. Then read the before and after side by side. The difference should be immediately obvious.
