## It's not the same question

"Tell me about yourself" and "walk me through your resume" look nearly identical but they pull in different directions. The resume question asks you to narrate your professional trajectory. This question invites something broader — who you are, how you think, what you care about.

Most candidates answer "tell me about yourself" as if it were the resume question. That's a missed opportunity. When an interviewer opens with this framing, they're often giving you more latitude — use it.

## What they want to hear

They want to know three things:

1. **Are you interesting?** Banking teams spend a lot of time together. An analyst who has only interests that map directly onto banking bullet points is less compelling than one who has a real intellectual life.
2. **Do you know yourself?** Genuine self-awareness — "I'm drawn to problems that require structured thinking under uncertainty" — is more convincing than rehearsed corporate-speak.
3. **Is there a connection between who you are and why banking?** The best answers create a line between your actual personality or interests and why this specific career makes sense for you.

## A three-part structure that works

### Part 1 — Who you are, briefly

Start one layer above resume line items. Not "I'm a finance major at Duke" — more like: "I've always been someone who gravitates toward problems with high stakes and limited information." Then anchor it quickly:

> "I'm a junior at NYU Stern, studying finance and data science. But the short version of who I am: I like being in the room where complex problems get solved."

That's personal without being theatrical. It sets a tone.

### Part 2 — What you've done that's interesting (pick 2–3)

This is where you highlight 2–3 experiences that illuminate something real about you — not just "here are my three most impressive bullet points." Choose experiences that connect to each other or that reveal a genuine intellectual curiosity.

> "I spent last summer working on M&A advisory at a boutique in Chicago, which taught me how deals actually get structured vs. how they get talked about in textbooks. Before that, I did a semester of policy research on healthcare pricing — which sounds unrelated, but it gave me a totally different lens on how to think about industry economics when I'm looking at companies now."

The healthcare policy detour isn't a weakness to explain away — it's interesting. Make it interesting.

### Part 3 — Why you're here

Land the answer. Connect who you are to why banking, briefly. This isn't the full "Why IB?" pitch — just a closing sentence that creates logical flow:

> "The combination of high-stakes analytical work, exposure to decisions that matter, and the variety of industries — that's what draws me to banking. And honestly, from the conversations I've had with people on this team, it seems like the kind of environment where I'd be surrounded by people I'd learn a lot from."

That last line, if genuine, is powerful. It shows you've done homework and signals culture fit, not just skill fit.

## What makes this different from the resume walkthrough

The resume question is essentially "what have you done, in order?" This question is more like "who are you, and why should I want to work with you?"

That means:
- You can lead with a personal observation or trait before listing credentials
- You can mention a non-professional interest if it genuinely connects ("I've spent three years writing for a financial column, which made me realize I care a lot about communicating complex ideas simply — something I think about in modeling and memo writing too")
- You can be slightly warmer and more conversational in tone

The risk of the resume walkthrough is that it sounds like you're reciting. This question is your chance to sound like a person.

## The mistake most people make

They treat "tell me about yourself" as an invitation to deliver their biography starting from birth. It isn't. The interviewer doesn't want a chronological life story. They want to understand you in the context of this conversation, right now. Keep it to two minutes max. Surface the things that will make them want to ask more questions.

## A note on preparation

Don't script this answer word-for-word. Write out the three components in bullet form, practice the through-line, then let yourself be a little more extemporaneous in delivery. It should feel like a thoughtful response to a question, not a recitation. The moments that sound slightly unplanned — a natural pause, choosing a word — often read as authenticity.

## Practice drill

Write down five things that are genuinely true and interesting about you — not resume lines, but actual traits, curiosities, or formative experiences. Now pick two that connect to banking in some way, and one that just makes you more human. Use those three as the basis for Part 2. Practice out loud, aiming for two minutes or under, without notes after the second try.
