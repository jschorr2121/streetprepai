## Why this is different from "tell me about a deal you followed"

Talking about a live deal in the news tests whether you follow the market. Talking about *your own* work tests something narrower and more personal: what did you actually do, and can you explain it well enough that a skeptical listener believes you understand it?

The trap most candidates fall into is one of two extremes. Either they give a vague, resume-bullet-level summary ("I helped with a sell-side process") that leaves the interviewer with nothing to grab onto, or they try to recite every number and process detail from the deal and run three minutes over, losing the thread halfway through. Both fail for the same reason: no structure.

## The four-part structure

Use this shape for any internship, project, or deal you're asked to discuss — aim for 60-90 seconds spoken aloud, which is roughly 150-220 words.

### 1. Company and context

One or two sentences: what did the company do, roughly how big was it, and what was the situation. You don't need exact figures — a rounded revenue or deal size is fine, and rounding is expected, not sloppy.

### 2. What the client or team needed

Why was this happening at all — raising capital, selling the business, evaluating an acquisition, entering a new market. This is the "so what" that gives the rest of your answer a reason to exist. Skip it and the listener has no frame for judging whether your contribution mattered.

### 3. What you personally did

This is the part interviewers actually care about, and it's the part most candidates rush through. Generic tasks — "I built comps," "I helped with the CIM," "I updated the model" — are what every intern on the deal did. What differentiates you is a specific moment: a number you caught that was wrong, a data point you found that changed how the team framed something to a buyer or investor, an assumption you pushed back on. If you genuinely did routine work with no standout moment, say so honestly and describe what you learned from the process instead of manufacturing a hero moment that didn't happen — see the honesty section below.

### 4. Where it stands now

Closed, ongoing, or fell apart — and if it closed, one line on how it's performed since. If it's still confidential or pending, say so and describe it in general terms ("a mid-sized healthcare services company") rather than naming it.

## A worked example

Say you spent a summer internship on a $340 million sell-side process for a regional industrial parts distributor, and the one thing you actually contributed was catching a channel-conflict issue in the customer data.

"I worked on the sell-side process for a regional auto-parts distributor doing about $340 million in revenue with roughly $45 million in EBITDA. The company's founder wanted to sell because he was retiring and didn't have a succession plan in the family.

While pulling together the customer concentration analysis for the CIM, I noticed that two of the company's largest listed customers were actually the same buyer purchasing through different subsidiaries — which meant the real customer concentration was higher than the raw data showed. I flagged it to my VP before it went into the marketing materials, and we adjusted the presentation and got ahead of the question instead of having a buyer catch it in diligence and use it to push on price.

The deal closed about four months after I left, at a price near the top of the initial range, and the business has kept both of those combined-entity customers since."

Notice what that answer does: it gives one number-backed contribution instead of five vague ones, states the stakes plainly (avoiding a price hit in diligence), and closes with an outcome. That's a complete, specific answer in well under two minutes — and every number in it should be one you can actually stand behind if asked a follow-up.

## Calibrating depth to the interviewer

Not every interviewer wants the same level of detail, and reading the room matters more than sticking to a fixed script.

- **A junior banker or associate** interviewing you is more likely to probe the mechanics — how you built the analysis, what the model assumed, why the team structured the deal the way it did. Be ready to go one layer deeper on process and numbers.
- **A senior banker (VP and above)** usually cares more about judgment and communication — why the deal made sense commercially, what the client's real motivation was, how you'd explain it to someone outside finance. Lead with the narrative, not the spreadsheet.
- **If you're not sure**, give the full 60-90 second version and let their first follow-up question tell you which direction to go. A technical follow-up means dig into mechanics; a "why" follow-up means stay at the narrative level.

In every case, stop after your outline-length answer and let them ask for more. Volunteering everything you know up front removes their ability to steer the conversation toward what they actually care about — and it's the single fastest way to run long.

## Honest boundaries: frame, never fabricate

There's a real difference between **framing** your experience favorably and **inventing** things that didn't happen, and it matters both ethically and practically — a fabricated detail is the one thing you can't defend under a good follow-up question.

What's fair game:
- Rounding numbers you don't remember exactly, as long as you're in the right range.
- Describing a deal that's still early-stage using your own reasonable judgment about likely outcomes, clearly framed as your expectation ("I'd expect this to close in the next few months given where discussions stand") rather than stated as fact.
- Emphasizing the most significant part of routine work — if you built the comp set that ultimately shaped the valuation range, it's fair to say your work informed the valuation, even if a senior banker made the final call.
- Connecting a task that seems narrow (checking one section of a data room, building one exhibit) to its actual effect on the deal, if you can explain that effect accurately.

What's not fair game:
- Claiming a deal closed when it didn't, or claiming you worked on a transaction you had no involvement in.
- Inventing a specific catch or contribution you didn't actually make — if your real contribution was building comps and updating a model, say that plainly rather than manufacturing a discovery that never happened.
- Stating numbers you're making up as if they're remembered facts, rather than flagging them as estimates when asked.

The practical reason to hold this line, beyond honesty: interviewers who've actually worked on deals ask sharp, specific follow-ups almost by reflex. A fabricated detail collapses the moment they ask you something adjacent to it that you haven't thought through, and that single moment does more damage than an honestly modest answer ever would.

## Common mistakes

- **Too many numbers, no narrative.** Reciting five metrics without explaining why any of them mattered leaves the interviewer with facts but no story.
- **Burying your actual contribution.** If your one real moment is in the middle of a long company description, it gets lost. Get to it directly.
- **No stated outcome.** Ending on "and then I built the model" with no resolution feels unfinished — always close with status.
- **Over-claiming ownership.** Saying "I ran the deal" as a summer intern is an immediate credibility problem with anyone who has actually run a deal. Own your slice precisely.
