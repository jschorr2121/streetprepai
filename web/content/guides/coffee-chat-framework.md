## What a coffee chat actually is

A coffee chat is not an informational interview. The moment you walk in (or dial in) treating it like an interview — nervous, performing, trying to impress — you've already lost the dynamic that makes these conversations valuable.

A coffee chat is a conversation between two people: one who is curious and one who has experience worth learning from. The best coffee chats feel like a great mentorship conversation, not an audition. Your job is to make the person on the other end feel that their time was well spent and that you're someone worth helping.

That shift in framing — from "I need to impress them" to "I want to learn from them and make this conversation worthwhile" — changes everything about how you prepare and how you behave in the call.

## The structure: four phases

### Phase 1 — Open naturally (2–3 minutes)

Brief, warm opener. Thank them for their time, acknowledge that you know they're busy, and give them a one-sentence reminder of who you are if you've never spoken:

> "Thank you so much for taking the time — I know you're juggling a lot. Quick context: I'm a junior at Duke studying econ, recruiting for IB roles, and I've been really interested in [firm] specifically based on conversations I've had with a couple of your colleagues."

This tells them who they're talking to without a lengthy speech. Then ask one warm-up question — something easy, not too loaded:

> "How long have you been in your current group? I'd love to hear a bit about your path before we dive in."

People like talking about themselves. Give them the opening.

### Phase 2 — The substantive questions (15–18 minutes)

This is the heart of the conversation. Have four to six prepared questions, but don't run through them like a list. Let the conversation breathe. If something they say is interesting, follow it — it's always better to go deep on something they're enthusiastic about than to stick rigidly to your script.

**Strong questions for an IB coffee chat:**

- "What does a typical week look like for an analyst in your group right now? I'd love to get past the general description and understand the day-to-day texture."
- "What surprised you most about the job that you didn't expect going in?"
- "What separates the analysts in your group who are exceptional from the ones who are just good?"
- "What's one thing you wish you'd known or done differently in recruiting?"
- "How do the different groups at [firm] interact? Do analysts rotate across coverage and product groups or stay in one lane?"
- "Is there anything you look for when evaluating a candidate beyond what's on their resume?"

That last one is powerful because it invites them to be helpful to you specifically, and most people will answer it directly and usefully.

**Questions to avoid:**
- "Can you get me an interview?" — Never say this out loud, ever.
- "What's your comp?" — Not appropriate.
- "Do you think I have a shot?" — Puts them in an awkward position.
- Generic questions you could answer yourself with 5 minutes of research — "Can you tell me what investment banking is?" wastes everyone's time.

### Phase 3 — The specific question (5 minutes)

Near the end, ask the question that is specific to your situation. This is where the call goes from generic to genuinely useful:

> "Given my background — I've done a semester at a boutique and I'm coming from a non-target school — what would you focus on in the next four months to put myself in the best position?"

Or:

> "I've been leaning toward [group] because of [reason]. Does that match what you see from the inside — is that a group where someone like me would be well-positioned?"

These questions require them to think about you specifically and give advice tailored to your situation. That's when the relationship shifts from stranger to person who might actually advocate for you.

### Phase 4 — Gratitude and the natural close (2–3 minutes)

Don't let the call trail off awkwardly. Land it:

> "This has been genuinely helpful — you've given me a much clearer picture of [specific thing they said]. I really appreciate you taking the time. Would it be okay to stay in touch as recruiting progresses?"

That last question almost always gets a yes. And it gives you permission to follow up later without feeling intrusive.

## The follow-up (within 24 hours)

Send a thank-you email the same day or the next morning. Three things make it effective:

1. **Specific reference** to something they said that was useful
2. **One update** that connects to the conversation (a deal you looked up based on their mention, a book they recommended)
3. **A clear signal** that you'll stay in touch (not that you'll bombard them)

> "Hi [Name], thank you again for the call earlier — your point about [specific thing] genuinely shifted how I'm thinking about group selection. I went and read about [deal they mentioned] afterwards, and it made the context much clearer. I'll keep you posted as recruiting unfolds. Thanks again for your time."

Short. Specific. Human.

## The long game

Networking is not a transaction. The bankers who advocate for you in recruiting are people who feel like they know you — not people who got one cold email from you. The coffee chat is the beginning, not the end.

Reach back out two months later with an update. Send a short note when you get an interview at their firm. When you get an offer, thank them. People remember the students who treat relationships like relationships, not like LinkedIn connections that serve a single purpose.

## Practice this

Before your next coffee chat, write your six questions on a notecard and practice saying each one out loud. Then practice your opener — who you are and why you reached out — in one sentence. Run through the whole arc once in your head. You won't follow the script exactly, but having it gives you a floor to fall back on when you're nervous.
