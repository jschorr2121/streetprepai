## The Morgan Stanley identity

Morgan Stanley is one of the two dominant global bulge brackets alongside Goldman Sachs, and while the two firms get lumped together constantly by recruiting students, they have genuinely different cultures and strengths. Knowing those differences — and being able to articulate them — is exactly what separates a good "why MS" answer from a bad one.

Morgan Stanley's DNA is historically rooted in equity capital markets. The firm built its reputation through landmark IPOs, equity underwriting, and a research franchise that shaped how institutions invest. That heritage still runs through the culture — there's an energy and institutional pride around equity markets and capital formation that you don't feel as strongly at other bulge brackets. More recently, Morgan Stanley has also been building aggressively in wealth management (via the E*TRADE and Eaton Vance acquisitions) as a strategic diversification, and understanding that pivot reflects well on candidates who follow the firm's strategy, not just its league table rankings.

## Group structure

Morgan Stanley recruits Summer Analysts into **Investment Banking** across coverage and product groups:

**Coverage:** Technology, Healthcare, Financial Institutions (FIG), Consumer & Retail, Industrials, Natural Resources, Real Estate, Sovereign & Public Finance.

**Products:** M&A, Leveraged Finance, ECM, DCM.

Morgan Stanley is particularly well-known for its Technology and Healthcare coverage groups — both are consistently among the top franchises globally. If you're interested in tech or biotech M&A and IPOs, Morgan Stanley's TMT and Healthcare groups are premier destinations.

The firm also has a notable presence in the infrastructure and energy transition space, which has grown as energy and sustainability-linked transactions have increased.

## What Morgan Stanley looks for

**Communication and presence.** More than some other bulge brackets, Morgan Stanley interviewers pay attention to how you present yourself — whether you're articulate, confident without being arrogant, and capable of holding a room. The analyst experience at MS often involves meaningful client exposure, and they select for people who can be in that room.

**Genuine engagement with markets and capital formation.** MS's equity markets heritage means interviewers often respond well to candidates who are genuinely interested in IPOs, secondary offerings, and how companies access capital. If that's an area of real interest for you, lean into it.

**Technical rigor.** The technical bar is high. Expect a thorough DCF walkthrough, accounting linkages, and possibly an IPO-specific technical question ("How would you value a company that's about to IPO and has no public comps?").

## Interview process

1. **First-round screens** — Typically behavioral plus technical, conducted by analysts or associates. On-campus or virtual.
2. **Superday** — Multiple rounds with people across seniority levels. Expect 4–6 rounds over a half-day. Morgan Stanley's superday can include a mix of technical deep-dives and behavioral questions focused on leadership and teamwork.

Morgan Stanley sometimes uses case-study or mini-deal-analysis components at later interview stages, particularly in structured formats. Prepare to discuss a company's valuation or a hypothetical deal scenario.

## Technical expectations

Expect all the standard topics: three-statement linkages, DCF, comps, precedents, WACC. Two areas where Morgan Stanley interviews sometimes go deeper than average:

**IPO / ECM-specific questions.** "How would you value a pre-revenue biotech company preparing for an IPO?" Answer: comparable company trading multiples on clinical milestone milestones or pipeline value, precedent biotech IPO transactions, and potentially an rNPV (risk-adjusted NPV) model for key assets. Know the concept even if you haven't built one.

**Equity markets mechanics.** "Walk me through the book-building process for an IPO." Know the basics: S-1 filing, roadshow, bookrunner roles, pricing relative to book, green-shoe over-allotment option.

## Recent context to know

Morgan Stanley has been active in technology and healthcare M&A, equity capital markets, and restructuring advisory. Look up the firm's recently announced advisory roles on its press page or on financial news outlets. Pay specific attention to ECM transactions (IPOs, follow-ons, converts) — being able to speak to one of these specifically is a Morgan Stanley-specific signal that you understand the firm's business.

## What "why Morgan Stanley" actually sounds like

Strong elements of a good answer:
- Naming a specific deal or ECM transaction Morgan Stanley ran and why you found it interesting
- Acknowledging the firm's ECM strength and why capital formation work interests you
- Something from a coffee chat — a specific detail about how deal teams operate or the culture within a specific group

Weak but common: "Morgan Stanley is consistently a top-ranked bank and I want to work at a firm with a global platform and strong deal flow." That's true of six other banks. Make it Morgan Stanley-specific.

## Practice this

Find one IPO that Morgan Stanley led in the last 12–18 months. Read the S-1 or prospectus summary. Understand the business model, why the company went public, how it was priced relative to peers, and what happened to the stock post-IPO. Being able to discuss an IPO transaction in this level of depth is a genuine differentiator for Morgan Stanley interviews specifically.
