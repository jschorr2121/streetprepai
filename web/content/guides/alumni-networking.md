## Why alumni networking is different

When you cold-email a random banker who has no connection to you, you're asking them to spend 20 minutes helping a stranger. When you reach out through a shared alumni connection, you're invoking a small but real social contract: "We went to the same school, and that makes us part of the same community." It's a lower barrier. The response rate is materially higher.

This is true at every school — but it's especially true for students from non-target universities, where alumni networking can be the primary path into IB recruiting. If your school doesn't have Goldman or Evercore showing up at on-campus events, alumni are your event.

## Finding alumni in banking

### Start with your school's career portal or alumni database

Most universities have an alumni directory accessible through the career center or alumni association. Search by industry (financial services, investment banking), by firm name, or by graduation year. Recent alumni — those within 5 years of graduation — are usually more willing to talk because recruiting feels less distant to them.

Filter for people in investment banking or private equity specifically. "Financial services" is too broad — you want bankers.

### LinkedIn search

LinkedIn is underutilized by students who are intimidated by it. Use the "Alumni" tool on your school's LinkedIn page. Filter by industry ("Investment Banking," "Capital Markets"), company (Goldman Sachs, Evercore, etc.), and graduation year. Look for people who have been at their current firm for 1–5 years — they remember recruiting, they have enough experience to give good advice, and they're not too senior to have time to talk.

### Ask professors, coaches, and career advisors

Faculty with industry connections, coaches who know alumni, and career advisors who track where students go — these are all warm leads into someone who might be willing to make an introduction. A warm intro trumps a cold email every time.

### Your upperclassman network

Students two or three years ahead of you in your finance club, your economics program, or your research lab are in banking right now. They're the most willing to talk because they remember exactly where you're sitting. Don't ignore this resource because they're not senior enough — they know your school's recruiting timeline better than any MD, and they can introduce you to people above them.

## The non-target reality and how to work around it

If your school isn't a recognized IB feeder, here's what you're actually dealing with: less on-campus recruiting infrastructure, fewer on-campus events, and bank recruiting portals that may prioritize target school OCR (on-campus recruiting). This is real, but it's not as prohibitive as students often assume.

**What actually works:**

**Regional offices.** Top banks have offices in cities beyond New York (San Francisco, Chicago, Houston, Los Angeles, Dallas). Alumni from non-target schools are often better represented in regional offices. Research which firms have offices in cities near your school.

**Middle-market and regional boutiques.** Firms like Houlihan Lokey, Baird, William Blair, Harris Williams, and dozens of regional boutiques hire from a much broader set of schools. Getting into banking at one of these firms is a legitimate path — many analysts move to bulge brackets or private equity after two years at a quality MM firm.

**Off-cycle recruiting.** Not all IB hiring happens in the formal summer analyst OCR cycle. Off-cycle internships and first-year analyst roles sometimes open outside the standard timeline, and non-target students who network aggressively during these windows can break through.

**Referrals carry weight.** If an alum you've built a real relationship with offers to put in a word, that referral moves your resume from the bottom of the pile to the top. This doesn't happen from a single coffee chat — it happens when someone feels they actually know you and can vouch for you.

## The cadence of a real networking strategy

Don't treat networking as a sprint you do two weeks before recruiting opens. Here's a realistic timeline:

**Six months before applications open:**
- Build your list of 30–50 target contacts across target firms and backup firms
- Start reaching out: 3–5 new outreach emails per week
- Coffee chat goal: 1–2 per week over 2–3 months

**Three months before:**
- Focus on building deeper relationships with 5–10 contacts who were receptive
- Check back in with updates: "I've been working on my technicals / I just finished a relevant project / I have an interview coming up"
- Ask for introductions within their firm to specific groups or offices

**One month before applications / interviews:**
- Let the contacts you've built real relationships with know you're applying to their firm
- Ask directly (but graciously): "Would it be appropriate for me to list you as a connection when I apply, or is there anything you'd be willing to do to put in a word?"

**After interviews:**
- Thank your contacts regardless of outcome
- Maintain the relationship — even if this cycle doesn't work, they'll remember you for next cycle or for a full-time referral

## What not to do

**Transactional follow-up.** "Did you hear anything about my application?" within 48 hours of meeting someone is aggressive and off-putting.

**Mass emails.** Sending 200 identical messages to everyone in the alumni database will get you nowhere and will quietly close doors with people who got the same message four other students sent.

**Disappearing after getting what you need.** If an alum helped you get an interview and you got an offer, tell them. If you didn't get the offer, thank them anyway. People notice when students only engage when they need something.

## Practice this

Open your school's alumni database right now. Filter for investment banking. Find five alumni at firms where you genuinely want to work, and five more at firms you'd be happy to work at but haven't prioritized. Research each person for ten minutes. Then write ten personalized outreach emails this week. Keep a simple spreadsheet: name, firm, date of outreach, date of response, notes from the call. Your network is a living thing — treat it like one.
