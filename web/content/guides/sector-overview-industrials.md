## What industrials actually covers

Industrials is one of the broadest and most heterogeneous coverage groups, spanning any business built around making, moving, or building physical things for other businesses rather than individual consumers. Common subsectors include **capital goods and machinery** (equipment manufacturers selling to other industrial companies), **aerospace & defense**, **transportation & logistics** (trucking, rail, freight, shipping), **construction & engineering**, **chemicals**, and **diversified manufacturing** (multi-segment conglomerates that don't fit cleanly into one bucket). What ties these subsectors together isn't the end product — a jet engine and a chemical plant have little in common physically — but the shared characteristics of how these businesses behave financially: capital intensity, exposure to economic cycles, and a customer base that's largely other businesses rather than consumers.

## What deals look like

Industrials M&A frequently follows a "bolt-on" pattern — a larger diversified manufacturer acquiring a smaller, focused competitor to add a product line, a geography, or a distribution channel, rather than making a single transformative acquisition. Private equity is also a large and consistent buyer in industrials specifically because many businesses in this sector throw off stable, predictable cash flow once mature — a classic profile for leveraged buyouts. Aerospace & defense sees a distinct pattern: consolidation among suppliers to the small number of large prime contractors (Boeing, Lockheed Martin, and similar), plus significant government-contract-driven deal activity that comes with its own regulatory review process.

## Why cyclicality dominates the story

Industrials businesses sell largely to other businesses, and business capital spending is one of the most cyclical categories of economic activity — companies expand factories, buy new equipment, and place large orders when they're confident about future demand, and they pull back hard and fast when they're not. This makes industrials earnings swing more sharply across an economic cycle than a typical consumer staples or healthcare business, where demand holds up regardless of the broader economy.

This cyclicality shows up directly in valuation conversations. A banker analyzing an industrials company needs to be explicit about where in the cycle current earnings sit — a company trading at a low EV/EBITDA multiple on peak-cycle earnings might actually be expensive once you normalize for where earnings are likely to land at the bottom of the next downturn, and vice versa. Comparing two industrials companies' multiples without accounting for where each sits in its own demand cycle is one of the fastest ways to reach the wrong conclusion.

## Backlog and book-to-bill: reading forward demand

Because industrials products — especially in capital goods, construction, and aerospace — often take months or years to build after an order is placed, current-period revenue tells you what was ordered a long time ago, not what demand looks like today. Two metrics fill that gap:

- **Backlog** — the total value of confirmed orders that have been signed but not yet delivered or recognized as revenue. A growing backlog signals rising forward demand even if current-period revenue looks flat; a shrinking backlog is an early warning sign well before it shows up in the income statement.
- **Book-to-bill ratio** — new orders received in a period divided by revenue billed (recognized) in that same period. A ratio above 1.0x means the company is booking more new business than it's delivering, so its backlog is growing and future revenue is likely to accelerate. A ratio below 1.0x means it's delivering faster than it's replacing that work, so the backlog is shrinking and future revenue is likely to decelerate.

**Worked example.** Say an industrial equipment maker, Ferrowell Corp, reports the following for the quarter: revenue recognized (billed) of $420M, and new orders booked of $480M. Book-to-bill = $480M / $420M = 1.14x. That tells you Ferrowell is adding to its backlog faster than it's working through it — a signal that revenue over the next several quarters is likely to grow, even before that shows up anywhere on the income statement. If its opening backlog was $1.1B, the closing backlog would be roughly $1.1B + $480M − $420M = $1.16B. A banker walking through Ferrowell's story would lead with book-to-bill and backlog growth as the forward-looking signal, not with the trailing revenue number alone.

## Standard multiples, sector-specific operating metrics

Valuation multiples in industrials are largely standard — EV/EBITDA is the primary multiple across most subsectors, with EV/Revenue used for lower-margin or earlier-stage businesses. What differs by subsector are the operating metrics layered alongside those multiples: **production capacity** and **capacity utilization** in chemicals and manufacturing; **backlog, option years** (additional years of potential work beyond the confirmed minimum), and **book-to-bill** in construction and capital goods; and **load factor, revenue per mile, and fleet utilization** in transportation & logistics.

## Terminology to know cold

- **Capital expenditure (CapEx) intensity** — how much of revenue a business must reinvest into plant, property, and equipment just to sustain operations; industrials businesses run meaningfully higher than most services or software businesses.
- **Cycle-adjusted earnings** — an attempt to normalize a company's earnings for where the broader economic or industry cycle currently sits, rather than taking a single year's reported number at face value.
- **Aftermarket / services revenue** — recurring revenue from parts, maintenance, and service contracts tied to equipment already sold; typically higher-margin and more stable than the original equipment sale, and increasingly emphasized in how industrials companies pitch their own growth story.
- **Vertical integration** — a company controlling multiple stages of its own supply chain (raw materials through finished product) rather than relying on outside suppliers; a common strategic theme in industrials M&A.
- **Prime contractor / subcontractor** — in aerospace & defense specifically, the small number of large companies that hold direct government contracts (primes) versus the larger set of suppliers underneath them (subs).

## How interviewers probe this

Expect "why does book-to-bill matter, and what does a ratio above or below 1.0x tell you?" as a standard technical question in this group — the answer centers on backlog as a leading indicator of future revenue. You may also be asked to explain how you'd normalize a cyclical company's earnings for valuation purposes, or asked directly where you think a specific industrial subsector currently sits in its cycle — know at least a rough view on this if you're targeting the group, since it's a favorite market-awareness question here.

## Common mistakes

- **Valuing a cyclical company off a single peak or trough year without flagging it.** Interviewers expect you to name the cyclicality explicitly, not just apply a multiple mechanically.
- **Treating current revenue as the best signal of demand.** In backlog-driven businesses, book-to-bill and backlog trends are more forward-looking than trailing revenue and should be cited first.
- **Assuming "industrials" is one uniform sector.** A chemicals company and an aerospace supplier have very different cost structures, cycle sensitivity, and deal patterns — conflating them in an interview is an easy tell that you haven't gone past the surface.
