## What you're actually walking into

Most bulge bracket and large elite boutique programs now route first-round candidates through a recorded video platform before any human ever sees your file. The format is rigid on purpose: a fixed set of questions, usually five to seven, each preceded by a short prep window (commonly around 30 seconds) and a capped response window (commonly around two minutes). Your camera is on the whole time. There is no interviewer on the other end reacting to you in real time — you're talking to a countdown timer and a webcam dot, which is a genuinely different skill from talking to a person.

You don't choose the order, you can't skip a question and come back to it, and in most implementations you get one take. Treat the fixed structure as the whole game: the platform is testing whether you can perform under a clock, not whether you can charm a room.

## What the algorithmic screen actually rewards

Before a human recruiter looks at your video, many programs run it through automated screening that flags candidates for human review based on measurable signal, not vibes. You don't need to know the exact model to prepare correctly — you need to know what's measurable, because that's what gets rewarded:

- **Talking, not silence.** Long pauses, dead air, and trailing off read as low signal. Filling your prep window planning a structure matters more here than in an in-person interview, because you don't get partial credit for a thoughtful pause.
- **Words per minute in a normal band.** Racing through an answer because you're nervous, or speaking so slowly you run out the clock, both look worse than a steady, conversational pace.
- **On-topic keyword density early.** State your answer's core claim in the first sentence. If the question is "tell me about a time you worked under pressure," say what the pressure was and what you did about it before you narrate the whole backstory.
- **Consistent eye contact with the camera**, not the screen or your notes. Looking down repeatedly reads as low confidence even if your answer is strong.
- **Completing the answer inside the window**, not getting cut off mid-sentence. An answer that ends cleanly at 1:45 beats one that gets truncated at 2:00 with your best point unsaid.

None of this is about gaming a machine — it's the same behavior a human recruiter would reward on a fast first pass through fifty videos. The algorithm just makes the incentive explicit and unforgiving.

## Structuring an answer for a hard 2-minute cap

Use your prep window to build a three-beat skeleton, not a script. A workable split for a 120-second answer:

- 0:00–0:20 — Setup: the situation and the stakes, in one or two sentences.
- 0:20–1:35 — Action: what you specifically did, with one concrete detail (a number, a decision, a tradeoff) that makes it yours and not generic.
- 1:35–2:00 — Result and reflection: what happened and what you'd take forward.

### Worked example: pacing your own answer

Say you're given "describe a time you had to learn something quickly under time pressure," with a 30-second prep window and a 2-minute response cap. You decide your setup needs about 15 seconds, your result needs about 20 seconds, and everything else goes to the action.

Total window: 120 seconds. Setup: 15 seconds. Result: 20 seconds. That leaves 120 − 15 − 20 = 85 seconds for the action section — enough for roughly two to three concrete beats (what you did first, what changed, what you decided), at a conversational pace of about 130 words per minute.

85 seconds at 130 words per minute: (85 / 60) × 130 = 1.4167 × 130 ≈ 184 words for the action section alone. Check the full answer: setup at 15 seconds ≈ 33 words, action ≈ 184 words, result at 20 seconds ≈ 43 words, for a total of 33 + 184 + 43 = 260 words across 120 seconds — a rate of 260 / 2 = 130 words per minute, which matches your target pace. The math holds, so you know your three-beat split is achievable at a normal speaking rate rather than requiring you to rush.

Practicing this kind of split in advance means that during the real prep window, you're only slotting your specific story into a shape you've already rehearsed, not inventing structure from scratch under a countdown.

## How to practice with strict timers

Rehearsing in a mirror with no clock doesn't transfer. Set an actual 30-second prep timer and a 2-minute response timer (or whatever your specific platform states in its instructions email — formats vary by employer) and run full mock reps with both running. Record yourself on your laptop camera, not just audio — you need to see your own eye contact and fidgeting, not just hear your pacing.

Build a bank of 15-20 stories before you ever sit for the real one, tagged by theme (leadership, failure, conflict, quantitative pressure, teamwork), so that under the 30-second prep window you're retrieving a story you already have in a folder, not writing one from nothing. Do at least three full timed run-throughs of a complete question set in the 48 hours before your actual session — the goal isn't polish on any single answer, it's building a felt sense of how long two minutes actually is, since almost everyone underestimates it the first several times.

## Common mistakes

- **Restarting instead of finishing.** If your platform allows a retake, resist re-recording something rough — a slightly imperfect answer that arrives naturally beats a stiff, over-rehearsed second take that reads as scripted.
- **Front-loading throat-clearing.** Opening with "that's a great question" or a long restated preamble burns 10-15 seconds you don't have.
- **Treating prep time as writing time.** You cannot draft a script in 30 seconds. Use it to pick which story and lock the three beats — nothing more.
- **Ignoring the camera.** Position your webcam at eye level and glance at the lens, not your own face in the preview window, or you'll read as evasive on playback.
