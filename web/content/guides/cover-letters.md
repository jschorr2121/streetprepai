## Where cover letters actually matter

Cover letter requirements vary sharply by region and firm size, and treating them as universally essential (or universally skippable) will cost you somewhere.

**Required, and read closely:** UK and broader EMEA applications almost always require a written response — sometimes framed as a formal cover letter, sometimes as short "competency question" answers of 100–250 words each that function the same way. These get scrutinized for both content and mechanical polish; a misplaced comma or a typo can be a real reason for rejection at firms that receive far more qualified EMEA applicants than they have interview slots.

**Often required, lightly read:** many US bulge bracket and elite boutique portals still have a cover letter upload field, but for a candidate with a strong resume and referral, it's frequently a formality that a first-pass screener skims for thirty seconds rather than a document read line by line.

**Rarely required:** many US middle-market firms, boutiques, and Handshake/LinkedIn Easy Apply postings skip the requirement outright. Some campus recruiting platforms don't even offer a field for one.

The safest default: **write one whenever the portal has a field for it**, even an optional one. An optional field left blank signals less effort than a field filled in with three solid paragraphs, and the marginal cost of writing it is low once you have a reusable structure.

## The structure that works

A cover letter that earns its thirty seconds of attention has three short paragraphs and nothing more. Long cover letters get skimmed, not read, so length past roughly 300–350 words works against you.

### Paragraph one: who you are and why this firm, specifically

Open with your current status (school, year, major) and one sentence that ties you to this specific firm — not banking in general. "I'm a junior at [school] studying finance, and I'm applying to [firm]'s technology group because of the team's recent work advising growth-stage software companies through their first institutional raises" reads as researched. "I'm applying because I want to work in investment banking" reads as generic, and a generic opener is the fastest way to signal you copy-pasted this letter across twenty firms.

### Paragraph two: the evidence

Pick your single strongest piece of relevant experience — an internship, a research project, a stock pitch competition, a finance club leadership role — and connect it directly to a skill the group needs: financial modeling, client communication, attention to detail under deadline pressure. Don't restate your resume bullets verbatim; use the space to explain the *why* behind one accomplishment the resume can only state flatly.

### Paragraph three: the close

One or two sentences restating interest and inviting the next step. Keep it plain: "I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss how my background fits the team, and I've attached my resume for your review." Don't oversell with adjectives like "passionate" or "thrilled" stacked on top of each other — one clear, confident sentence beats three enthusiastic ones.

## What kills a cover letter

- **Genericness that's obviously copy-pasted.** Naming the wrong firm (a leftover from a template used for a different application), naming a group the firm doesn't have, or writing generically about "investment banking" instead of the specific firm and team are the fastest disqualifiers, because they read as carelessness rather than lack of polish.
- **Restating the resume.** If every sentence in your cover letter could be reconstructed from your resume bullets, the letter added nothing. It should explain motivation and fit — information the resume, by its bulleted format, can't convey.
- **Typos and formatting errors.** This matters more than students expect, especially at EMEA firms where mechanical accuracy is explicitly part of the evaluation. Type it in a word processor with spell-check on, read it aloud, and have one other person proofread it before you submit.
- **Overlength.** A four-paragraph, 600-word letter doesn't get read more carefully — it gets read less carefully, or not at all, because a time-constrained screener will skim only the opening lines.
- **Overwrought tone.** Cover letters that read like a cold sales pitch ("I am the ideal candidate because...") undercut the same confidence they're trying to project. Direct and specific beats aggressive and generic.

## When it's fine to skip one

If the portal has no field for a cover letter and doesn't prompt for one, don't manufacture a reason to attach one — it won't be read and may even look like you didn't follow the application instructions. Some smaller-firm and off-cycle applications (see [off-cycle and recovery paths](/guide/off-cycle-and-recovery-paths)) are a single email with a resume attached; in that case, the body of the email functions as your cover letter, and the same three-paragraph logic applies, just shorter — two or three sentences of context before the resume attachment is enough.

## How interviewers probe this

Interviewers rarely quote your cover letter back to you directly, but two things carry over into the interview room. First, if you wrote something specific about the firm or group in your letter — a deal, a sector focus, a piece of research — be ready to speak to it fluently; a detail that reads well on paper but that you can't discuss when asked signals the letter wasn't really yours. Second, the "why this firm" answer you develop for the cover letter is often close to the answer you'll give live in the first round (see [why our firm](/guide/why-our-firm)) — write the letter well and you've already drafted that answer.
