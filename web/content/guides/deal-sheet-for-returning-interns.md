## The returning intern's advantage — and the risk

If you've done a prior internship in investment banking, private equity, equity research, or a closely related field, you have a meaningful advantage over most of your recruiting peers. You can speak to deal experience, analytical process, and day-to-day banking reality in a way that students without that background can't.

But that advantage comes with a higher standard: interviewers will probe your experience more aggressively, assume you know the technical material cold, and expect you to be able to speak about your work at a level of granularity that someone without banking experience cannot. "I worked on an M&A deal" won't cut it. "I built the merger model for a $350M sell-side process in the consumer packaging space, focusing on the EBITDA bridge from standalone to pro forma accounting for $18M in cost synergies" is the kind of answer that reflects real experience.

## What a deal sheet is

A deal sheet is a summary of the transactions you've worked on — deal name (or a descriptor if it was a confidential private company), deal type, size, and your specific role. Some returning interns attach a deal sheet as a supplementary page to their resume; others reference it in interviews without formally including it.

**Deal sheet format (simple):**

| Deal | Type | Size | My Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Confidential] Consumer Packaging Company | Sell-Side M&A | $350M | Built merger model, ran comparables analysis, drafted management presentation sections |
| [Public Company] Healthcare Services | Buy-Side M&A, Not Completed | ~$1.2B | Supported diligence process, built operating model, prepared board materials |
| [Confidential] Industrial Distribution Company | Capital Markets Roadshow Support | ~$150M equity offering | Prepared investor presentation, financial model updates |

Be accurate on size — bankers can verify public deals. For private deals, use approximate size or say "confidential." Do not round up deal sizes to make them sound more impressive.

## What you can and cannot claim

**What you can credibly claim:**
- "I built the model" (if you actually built it, even with templates)
- "I ran the comp analysis" (if you did the research and populated the tables)
- "I drafted sections of the management presentation" (specify which sections)
- "I sat in on diligence calls" (if you were present and taking notes)
- "I prepared materials that went to the client" (be precise about what)

**What to be careful about:**
- "I advised on" — unless you were in client meetings giving advice, say "supported analysis on" instead
- "I structured the deal" — this is senior banker territory; you can say "I modeled deal structures" or "I analyzed alternative deal structures"
- Claiming sole credit for group work — say "I led the comp analysis" only if you actually did

Interviewers who worked on the same deal you're referencing can verify what analysts actually do. Overclaiming is a fast way to lose credibility.

## Discussing deal experience in interviews

When asked "Walk me through a deal you worked on," structure your answer:

### 1. Context (15 seconds)
> "I worked on a sell-side M&A process during my internship at [firm]. The client was a consumer packaging company — private, roughly $350M deal. We were engaged to run a full sale process."

### 2. What the deal involved (30 seconds)
> "The process ran over about three months of my internship. We built a CIM, ran a management presentation process with strategic and financial buyers, and ultimately sold the business to a strategic acquirer."

### 3. Your specific role (45 seconds — this is the key part)
> "My specific contribution was the merger model — I maintained the standalone operating model, built out the buyer's pro forma model showing accretion/dilution under different financing assumptions, and ran the sensitivity analysis. I also built and maintained the comparables set, which we updated weekly as new quarterly filings came in. Toward the end, I helped draft two sections of the management presentation — the financial overview and the EBITDA bridge slide."

### 4. What you took away (15 seconds)
> "The biggest thing I learned was how the modeling and the marketing materials interact — every assumption in the model eventually becomes a claim in the deck, and you need to be able to defend the math behind every slide."

That answer shows genuine experience, specific analytical contribution, and intellectual reflection. It's what a returning intern's deal discussion should sound like.

## Technical expectations for returning interns

If you've worked in banking, interviewers will assume your technical foundation is solid and may skip the basics entirely, going straight to:

- "Walk me through your model for [deal you mentioned]" — Be ready to describe the model architecture, key assumptions, and how you stress-tested them
- "What were the key value drivers for that company?" — Know the business, not just the model
- "How did you think about the comps?" — Be ready to defend comp selection logic
- "What surprised you about the diligence process?" — Shows intellectual engagement, not just execution

The difference between a strong returning intern and a weak one is usually depth. Strong returning interns talk about their deals like they understood what was happening and why. Weak ones describe outputs without demonstrating understanding.

## If you worked in PE or ER, not IB

Prior PE experience: emphasize the deal evaluation and modeling work. You've probably built LBO models, done due diligence, and seen how investors think about company selection. That's highly relevant and should be presented as such — not as "I did private equity" but as "I evaluated 12 opportunities, built detailed LBO models on four of them, and supported two due diligence processes from initial meeting through LOI."

Prior ER experience: emphasize the depth of industry and company analysis. You've modeled quarterly earnings, built DCFs, and formed views on company valuation. Be specific about coverage: "I covered three specialty chemical companies, maintained detailed financial models, and contributed to four published research notes." Transition the discussion toward the analytical rigor and financial model depth, which translates directly to banking.

## Practice this

If you have deal experience, write down five things you specifically did on each deal — five actual analytical contributions, not generic roles. Then practice describing one deal start to finish in two minutes, with your specific role taking at least half the time. Record yourself. If the "my role" section sounds vague or junior, dig for more specific language.
