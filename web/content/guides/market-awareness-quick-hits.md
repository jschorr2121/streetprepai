## Why this question shows up in almost every interview

"Walk me through the markets today" or "what's your view on the market right now" is one of the most common interview openers, and it's also one of the most poorly prepared for. Candidates spend weeks drilling DCFs and LBOs and then freeze when asked what the 10-year did last week. Interviewers use this question as a cheap, fast filter: it tells them whether you actually read anything finance-related outside of your prep materials, or whether you crammed technicals in isolation. You don't need to be a macro strategist. You need three things: the numbers, a view, and a story about a live deal.

## The numbers to know walking in

Before any interview, refresh four categories the morning of (or the night before, since most of these don't move much overnight):

- **Major index levels and recent direction.** You should know the approximate level of the S&P 500, the Dow, and the Nasdaq, plus whether they're up or down over the past week and year-to-date. You don't need the exact close to the decimal — "S&P's around 6,100, up something like 12% year-to-date" is a perfectly good answer.
- **The 10-year Treasury yield.** This is the single most-referenced number in any markets conversation because it's the reference rate for discounting nearly everything and it signals growth/inflation expectations. Know the current level (say, "around 4.3%") and the general trend (rising, falling, range-bound).
- **The Fed funds rate and the Fed's stance.** Know the current target range, when the last move was (hike, cut, or hold), and what the market is pricing for the next meeting. You should be able to say something like "the Fed's on hold at 4.25–4.50%, and the market's pricing maybe one more cut this year."
- **One or two sector or commodity data points that matter right now.** Oil price, a notable earnings miss, unemployment print, or a specific stock that moved a lot. Pick whatever is actually in the news that week — this shows you're reading current coverage, not reciting a memorized script.

The goal isn't precision, it's fluency. An interviewer who hears you rattle off a level, a trend, and a driver in one breath concludes you read the news that morning. An interviewer who hears "I'm not sure, I think the market's up?" concludes the opposite.

## Forming one defensible market view

Numbers alone don't answer "what's your view on the market" — you need a thesis. A defensible view has three parts: a direction, a reason, and an acknowledged risk. Don't try to have an opinion on everything; pick one lane (equities, rates, or a sector) and go one level deep.

A weak answer: "I think the market will keep going up because the economy is strong."

A stronger answer: "I'm cautiously bullish on equities over the next six to twelve months. Earnings growth has held up better than expected and the Fed looks done hiking, which usually supports multiples. My biggest risk to that view is that valuations are already stretched relative to history, so any disappointment on inflation data could trigger a sharp pullback."

Notice the structure: direction (cautiously bullish), reason (earnings + Fed trajectory), risk (valuation, specific catalyst). Interviewers rarely care whether you're "right" — they care whether you can reason from evidence to a conclusion while acknowledging the counterargument. That's the same skill they want to see in a stock pitch or a deal recommendation.

### A worked example: reading a yield move into a view

Say the 10-year yield moves from 4.10% to 4.35% over two weeks on stronger-than-expected jobs data. Walk through the logic out loud in an interview:

1. Higher yields raise the discount rate used in a DCF, so the present value of future cash flows falls, all else equal.
2. Growth and long-duration stocks (think unprofitable tech, high-multiple names) are more sensitive to this because more of their value sits in cash flows far in the future.
3. Quick sensitivity check: if a stock's value is roughly split 70% terminal value / 30% near-term cash flows, and the discount rate moves from 9.0% to 9.25% (a 25 bps pass-through from the 25 bps Treasury move, holding the equity risk premium constant), the terminal value using a Gordon Growth perpetuity with a 3% terminal growth rate shifts as follows: TV multiple = 1 / (r − g). At r = 9.0%, multiple = 1 / 0.06 = 16.7x. At r = 9.25%, multiple = 1 / 0.0625 = 16.0x. That's roughly a 4.2% haircut to terminal value alone (16.0 / 16.7 − 1 = −4.2%), before touching near-term cash flows.
4. So your view might be: "Rising real yields are a headwind for high-multiple growth names specifically, more than for the market broadly, because terminal value is more discount-rate-sensitive than near-term cash flows."

That's a self-contained, quantitatively grounded view built from one macro data point — exactly the kind of answer that separates a memorized talking point from real understanding.

## Keeping a living current-deals note

Set up a running note (a phone note, a Google Doc, whatever you'll actually open) and add to it two or three times a week, not the night before an interview. For each deal or transaction you read about, capture four things in one or two lines: the parties, the deal size and structure (cash, stock, or mix), the strategic rationale as reported, and your own one-sentence take on whether it makes sense. Include at least one M&A deal, one IPO or capital markets transaction, and one deal in the sector you're targeting (tech, healthcare, industrials, whatever the group is).

The note does two jobs. First, it gives you two or three ready answers to "walk me through a deal you've been following," which comes up almost as often as the markets question. Second, rereading your own past entries the week before interviews is far faster than re-researching from scratch, and it shows you a running record of how your judgment has developed.

### Common mistakes

- **Memorizing a script instead of understanding the numbers.** Interviewers will ask a follow-up ("why does a stronger jobs report push yields up?"), and a memorized answer collapses immediately under one probing question.
- **Having a view with no risk attached.** A one-sided answer ("stocks go up because earnings are good") reads as unexamined, not confident.
- **Picking a deal you can't actually explain.** If you cite a transaction in your note, be ready to explain why the acquirer paid what it paid, not just that the deal happened.
- **Letting the note go stale.** A note last updated two months ago is obvious the moment an interviewer asks "what have you been following lately" and you cite something old.
