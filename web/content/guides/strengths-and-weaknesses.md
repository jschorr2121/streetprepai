## Why these questions matter more than you think

"What's your greatest weakness?" is the question people stress about most and prepare for worst. Most candidates prepare a fake weakness that's secretly a strength ("I work too hard," "I care too much about quality") — and most interviewers have heard those non-answers so many times they've become a minor negative signal rather than a neutral response.

The good news: answering these questions honestly and specifically is a real differentiator. Most people don't.

## Strengths — not a list, a story

The instinct is to give three bullet points: "I'm analytical, detail-oriented, and a team player." Those answers are fine and forgettable. A better answer names one strength and makes it specific with an example.

**The structure:** Name the strength, give a one-sentence example from your actual experience, connect it to why it's relevant to banking.

> "I'd say my strongest skill is translating ambiguous data into a clear recommendation. Last year, I was covering the healthcare sector for my school's investment club and had to synthesize three conflicting sell-side reports to form a single view on a company's pipeline value. I had to decide which inputs to trust and why, and then communicate that clearly to the full club. That kind of structured thinking under uncertainty is something I genuinely enjoy — and something I think the work of banking requires constantly."

That answer is concrete, self-aware, and relevant. It shows the skill rather than asserting it.

### Strengths to avoid

**"I'm a hard worker"** — Expected baseline. Not a differentiator.
**"I'm a people person"** — Vague. Everyone says this.
**"I'm good under pressure"** — Can't be evaluated without context. Prove it with an example.

Strong differentiating strengths for banking roles: structured thinking, communication under time constraints, ability to synthesize large amounts of information quickly, persistence on messy problems, attention to the logic (not just the numbers) in a model.

## Weaknesses — the real answer wins

Here's the truth about the weakness question: interviewers are not looking to eliminate you because you have a weakness. Everyone has weaknesses. They're evaluating whether you're self-aware enough to know yours, honest enough to name them, and mature enough to be actively working on them.

The framework that works:

1. **Name a real weakness** (not a disguised strength)
2. **Own it briefly** — don't over-explain or apologize
3. **Describe what you're actively doing about it**
4. **Show progress**

> "I tend to want to fully understand every part of a problem before I say something — which means I can be slower than I should be to surface a preliminary view. In high-deadline environments, that's a real tension. I've been working on it deliberately: in my current internship, I've been pushing myself to share a '70% answer' clearly labeled as preliminary and iterate from there, rather than holding back until I'm more confident. It's uncomfortable, but the feedback has been positive."

That answer is honest, shows self-awareness, and demonstrates growth. It's infinitely more compelling than "I care too much about getting things perfect."

### Weaknesses to genuinely avoid

You want to avoid weaknesses that would raise serious flags for a banking role:

- "I sometimes lose focus" — attention in banking is non-negotiable
- "I can be disorganized" — analysts manage enormous amounts of detail
- "I struggle to meet deadlines" — this is a threshold failure

Pick something real that's a genuine weakness but doesn't call into question your ability to do the job.

### The disguised-strength trap (and why it backfires)

"My weakness is that I'm a perfectionist" is the most common non-answer in the history of IB recruiting. Interviewers hear it multiple times per interview day. The problem is that it signals either dishonesty or a lack of self-awareness — both are worse than having a real weakness. If you have been using this answer, replace it today.

## How to prepare

Write down three genuine weaknesses on paper. For each one, ask: is this actually true? Is this a problem a banking analyst would have? Am I actively working on it? Keep the one that best answers yes to all three, and build the full answer around it.

For strengths: look back at three moments in the last two years when you felt like you were genuinely operating at your best. What skill was driving that? Name it, and tell the story.

## Practice drill

Answer the weakness question out loud right now, without using "perfectionism," "working too hard," or "caring too much." Time yourself — aim for 60–75 seconds. If you can't name a real weakness in under 75 seconds without a cliché, keep revising until you can.
