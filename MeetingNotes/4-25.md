Zuf Call 4-25 — Product Discussion on AI Interview Prep App
This call focused on refining the product concept for an investment banking interview-prep app. The group discussed how the app should be structured, what features would make it useful and differentiated, and how to validate the idea by interviewing recent candidates. A major theme was making the product feel personalized, real, and useful long-term, especially by combining technical prep, networking tracking, and AI-driven interview support.

Product Structure: User-Directed Learning Flow
Core app organization
		The app should likely be split into two major sections:
		Preparing
		Tracking
		The experience should not force users through a fixed chapter-by-chapter flow.
		Instead, the user decides what to use and when.
Preparing section
		Users can:
		Read any chapter they want
		Use AI to learn within a chapter
		Practice technical questions
		Mix and match prep methods
		The app should support a Quizlet-like experience:
		Users can create their own technical question tests
		Users can choose specific chapters
		Users can create a full sample test across all chapters
		Users can focus on weak areas based on past performance
Personalization idea
		The app should track where the user struggles.
		Example:
		If a user repeatedly misses questions from one chapter, the app should recommend focusing there.
		The technical question system should allow:
		Chapter-specific tests
		Mixed tests
		Custom preferences when generating a test

Technical Questions and Case Studies
Technical prep
		A dedicated technical questions tab should include all available questions.
		Users should be able to say “create test for me” and set preferences.
		The system should adapt based on:
		Chapter difficulty
		User weaknesses
		Desired mix of topics
Case studies
		The group noted that harder interviews include case study questions.
		This is a feature that still needs to be added and thought through.
		It was recognized as an important part of the prep experience, especially for more advanced interview stages.

Validating the Product with Real Users
Need for user interviews
		Cheryl emphasized that the team should interview people who are closer to the current interview process.
		The goal is to understand:
		How people prepare now
		What gaps exist
		What problem the app is actually solving
		Suggested approach:
		Interview recent Vanderbilt students who just went through recruiting
		Learn what worked, what didn’t, and what information is hard to find
Why this matters
		Interviewing recent candidates could reveal:
		Current prep habits
		Missing resources
		Opportunities for a more useful product
		It would also help make the product feel more real and grounded in actual experience.

Differentiation: Bank-Specific Interview Guides
Idea for proprietary content
		One possible differentiator is bank-specific interview guidance.
		The app could include:
		A list of banks
		What the interview process looks like at each bank
		What kinds of questions each bank tends to ask
How to build it
		This information could come from:
		Recent students
		Friends who already completed interviews
		People with spreadsheets or notes from recruiting
		Example:
		A user clicks Morgan Stanley
		The app shows the process, likely rounds, and common question types
Challenge: accuracy
		There was concern that public forums like Wall Street Oasis are unreliable.
		People may post false or misleading information.
		Because of that, the team discussed making this section more of a guide than a guaranteed source of truth.
		The value would come from:
		Consolidating scattered information
		Making it easier to understand the big picture
		Helping users feel more prepared
Competitive insight
		A more trustworthy version of Wall Street Oasis could be compelling.
		Even if the information is not perfect, users may still find it useful if it feels more real and less anonymous.

Networking Tracking as a Major Use Case
Importance of networking
		The group identified networking tracking as a major feature that had not been discussed enough.
		This could be one of the strongest parts of the product.
What the tracking feature would do
		Track:
		Coffee chats
		Networking conversations
		Interviews
		Technical questions completed
		Reading or prep materials used
		The data would be integrated so the app has a full picture of the user’s recruiting journey.
Why it matters
		Users can use networking insights to:
		Stand out in interviews
		Give more personalized answers
		Reference specific conversations with bankers
		Instead of generic answers, users can build responses based on:
		What they learned in networking calls
		What they heard about a specific bank
		What they personally did in internships or school
Example use case
		If a user learns something in a networking call, they can:
		Save it in the app
		Use it later in behavioral interview answers
		Bring it up in future networking conversations
		This creates a feedback loop between:
		Networking
		Interview prep
		Personalized storytelling

AI Chatbot: Personalized Interview Prep
Core concept
		The app will include an AI chat feature similar to Ask Stanley or ChatGPT-style interfaces.
		The difference is that this chatbot will have access to the user’s:
		Technical prep history
		Coffee chats
		Interviews
		Reading history
		Profile and experiences
What makes it useful
		When a user asks:
		“Help me prepare for my JP Morgan interview”
		The chatbot can respond with:
		General interview prep
		Plus personalized context from the user’s own history
Memory and profile access
		The AI should not just rely on one-off prompts.
		It should be able to call a profile tool to retrieve:
		Resume information
		Past experiences
		Relevant chats
		Bank-specific notes
		This makes the AI more useful over time because it can tailor responses to the user’s background.
Key value proposition
		Most people do not store much personal information in AI tools long term.
		This app would be different because it would:
		Retain relevant user context
		Use that context to improve prep
		Help users build stronger, more specific answers

Behavioral Interview Prep and Storytelling
Personalized answers
		A major use case is helping users answer behavioral questions with more specificity.
		The app can turn user experiences into:
		Stories
		Sample answers
		Bank-specific talking points
Why this is valuable
		Interviewers can tell when candidates use generic answers.
		The app should help users:
		Stand out
		Sound more authentic
		Show genuine interest in a specific bank
Example workflow
		The user logs networking notes from a JP Morgan conversation.
		Later, the app helps them:
		Turn that into a behavioral answer
		Reference it in a follow-up networking call
		Prepare a stronger “Why this bank?” response

Monetization and Cost Concerns
AI cost issue
		One concern raised was that the AI feature is not free.
		The team needs to analyze:
		How much each request costs
		Whether the app can support usage at scale
		Whether a subscription model makes sense
Pricing thoughts
		There was discussion of a monthly subscription model.
		The team compared it to ChatGPT’s pricing:
		ChatGPT charges around $20/month
		It does not heavily limit usage
		The main concern is avoiding a situation where the company loses money on AI usage.
Open question
		The team still needs to do a cost analysis before finalizing pricing or usage limits.

Summary
The call centered on shaping an AI-powered investment banking recruiting app that is more personalized than existing tools. The strongest ideas were a flexible prep flow, technical question testing, networking tracking, and an AI chatbot that uses the user’s own history to generate tailored interview prep. The team also discussed validating the concept through interviews with recent candidates and making sure the AI product is financially sustainable.
